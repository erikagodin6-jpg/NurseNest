Pneumothorax
-	Air enters pleura space and causes partial or total collapse of the lung
-	Normally negative pressure in the space but now there is air
-	Can be caused by hole in chest wall (open)
-	Closed (air enters after lung surgery
-	Tension: closed but air enters and can’t get out – affected and possibly unaffected lungs collapse – trachea shifts to unaffected side
-	Heart and great vessels are compromised
-	Features:
o	Progressive dyspnea 
o	Restless, anxious
o	Cyanosis
o	Chest pain
o	Hypotension, tachycardia (IVC not returning blood)
o	Chest x-ray supports diagnosis only in stable clients
-	Assessment
o	Hyperressonance (low pitched)
o	Diminished sounds on affected sides
o	Decreased tactile fremitus
o	Hypotension
-	Nursing
o	Three way occlusive dressing over sucking chest wound 
o	Keep in fowler position if possible
o	Prepare for chest tube insertion 
	Air escapes
	Air entry prevented 
	Negative pleural pressure reestablished
	Lung reexpands
o	Administer pain medications

Chest Drainage System
-	Measure and collect fluid
-	Water seal chamber
-	Air exits through one way valve
-	Tidaling is an expected finding until lung reexpands
-	Bubbling = air leak = continuous = bad, if they cough or stand up, intermittent bubbling is okay
-	Suction chamber 
o	Administer pain medications
o	Ensure that the system is closed and has no leaks or kinks from insertion site down to seal
o	Ensure there are no dependent loops in the tubing
o	Monitor volume and characteristics of drainage
o	Notify HCP if drainage is more than 100 ml an hour and if its sudden bright red or free flowing
o	Keep the collection device below the insertion site at all times 
o	Do not strip tubing.- increases pressure
o	Reinforce occlusive dressing
o	Ensure that the appropriate chest x-rays are ordered and follow changes

Pancreatitis
-	Inflammation of pancreas
-	Normally breaks down fat and regulates blood glucose levels
-	Can happen with direct injury due to viruses, alcohol, medications
-	Can also happen with pancreatic duct obstruction with cancer or gallstones
-	Autodigestion of pancreatic tissue
-	Can lead to impaired exocrine and endocrine function
-	Risks:
o	Alcohol – direct injury 
o	Gallstones – obstruction – traps enzymes and causes pancreas to eat itself
o	Thiazide diuretics
o	Viral infections
o	Trauma
-	Features:
o	Severe pain after eating in epigastric region or upper left abdomen
o	Referred back pain
o	Exacerbation when laying flat
o	Pain may be relieved by leaning forward 
o	Difficulty processing foods 
o	Nausea and vomiting
o	Elevated lipase and amylase levels 
o	Ct scan may show inflammation
-	Complications: 
o	Inflammation causes systemic effects
o	Release of cytokines = increased capillary permeability and vasodilation
o	Hypovolemic shock due to third spacing
o	ARDS
o	Retroperitoneal hemorrhage (ecchymosis in flank around umbilicus)   Cullen sign
o	Hypocalcemia (Chvostek or trousseau sign)
o	Hyperglycemia 
-	nursing
o	pain management  administer IV opioids 
o	IV fluids 
o	Antiemetics 
o	Initiate NPO
o	Alcohol cessation 
o	Monitor for hyperglycemia and give insulin prn 
o	Hypocalcemia (calcium replacement)

Chronic Pancreatitis
-	Repeated inflammation that leads to fibrosis 
-	Caused by alcohol, obstruction, CF, autoimmune
-	Features:
o	Chronic or episodic epigastric pain
o	Malabsorption (steatorrhea, weight loss)
o	DM (due to destruction of beta cells) 
o	Asymptomatic (less common)
-	Nursing
o	Alcohol cessation
o	Hyperglycemia (insulin)
o	Diet: small, bland, frequent meals low in fat
o	Pancreatic enzymes to break down fats that are ingested
o	Take enzymes with meals or a snack
o	Evaluate efficacy by observing the stools for steatorrhea
o	Supplemental vitamins and minerals


Angina 
-	Chest pain in response ot MI or vasospasm
-	3 types
o	Chronic 
	Occurs due to a fixed partial obstruction
	Exertional angina 
	Symptoms result when myocardial oxygen demand exceeds supply
	Chest pain, nausea, and dyspnea
	Relieved with rest or nitro
	Usually has a stable pattern of onset, duration, severity, relieving factors 
	Usually lasts up to five minutes 
•	Nursing
o	Treat as outpatient
o	Decrease o2 demand, increase o2 supply
o	Treat with beta blockers, calcium channel blockers, long acting nitrates to reduce stress on heart and vasodilate coronary arteries
o	Acute pain: short acting nitro
o	Treat underlying atherosclerosis 
	Antiplatelet agents (clopidogrel or asa)
	Statin therapy – reduce cholesterol levels 
	If the cholesterol continues to rise, can progress to unstable angina or MI
o	Unstable
	Preinfarction angina
	Rupture of usnstable plaque in coronary artery
	Occurs with an unpredictable degree of exertion or emotion
	Increases in occurrence, duration, severity
	Can occur at rest or with minimal exertion
	Pain may not be relieved with nitro
	Can last longer than 10 mins
•	Nursing:
o	Position upright, provide 02 to reduce peripheral vascular resistance 
o	BP will go down 
o	Assess vitals and heart and lung sounds – clients can be hypertensive and go into crisis. If there is an increase the plaque can rupture and cause MI
o	Obtain 12 lead ECG – St segment changes?
o	Obtain cardiac biomarkers such as troponin – not an immediate result, redraw until peaks 
o	Pain relief: nitro and IV opioid analgesic (morphine to reduce preload) 
o	Coronary angiography if evidence of myocardial tissue damage 
o	Prinzmetal/variant
	Coronary vasospasm 
	Occurs at rest or during sleep
	Triggered by smoking
	May or may not be related to blockage of coronary arteries 
	Mimics an MI
	ECG may show ST elevation similar to MI, temporary occlusion
	Pain resolves in a few minutes
	Collect blodowork and watch troponin
•	Nursing
o	Preventative measures -prescribed and scheduled long acting nitrates and calcium channel blockers to reduce spasms 
o	Acute pain spasm: subling nitro or light exercise to increase blood flow and open coronary arteries 

Aortic Dissection
-	Tear develops in inner layer of aorta (intima)
-	Blood surges through tear into the middle layer and the space fills with blood and separates (dissect)
-	A false lumen forms 
-	2 types:
o	Type A – ascending (heart and above)
	Sudden onset anterior chest pain, tearing, worst ever, ripping pain
	May radiate to neck, jaw, upper back
	Tachycardia, restless, anxious
	Diaphoretic
	Hypertension
	Widening aorta on chest imagine
	Most fatal
o	Type B – subclavian artery (below heart) 
	Abdominal or back pain
	Complication: life-threatening aortic rupture  cardiac tamponade 
	Brain  stroke
	Bowel  ischemia
	Kidneys  aki (decreased urinary output, increased BUN and creatinine)
	Spinal cord  paralysis
	Extremities  cold leg
	Coronary arteries  MI 
-	Risk:
o	HTN (especially if they suddenly stop taking meds)
o	Cocaine use (sudden increase in BP)
o	Underlying aortopathy
	Marfan syndrome
-	Diagnostic Studies
o	Widening aorta 
o	CT scan
o	TEE 
-	Nursing: 
o	Reduce HR and BP
o	Administer betaadrenergic antagonist (labetalol or esmolol)
o	Lower HR before BP or you will cause reflexive tachycardia 
o	Keep client in semi fowler
o	Cluster care, limit visitors, maintain quiet environment 
o	Keep BP between 100-120 
o	Arterial catheter may be needed (art line) for continuous monitoring 
o	Continuous cardiac monitoring
o	Opioid analgesics (morphine)
o	Monitor for complications (stroke, cardiac tamponade, heart failure)
o	Prepare for emergency surgery  - at least 2 IVs

Alzheimer Disease
-	Irreversible and progressive dementia
-	Caused by neurodegenerative changes in the brain by abnormal protein buildup (betaamyloid and tau proteins) which results in cell damage and neuronal death 
-	Likely due to genetic and environmental factors 
-	Changes develop overtime so manifestations can vary
-	Manifestations:
o	Early:
	Decline in short term memory and social skills 
	Visuospatial deficits 
	Language difficulties (may forget words)
	Cognitive impairment (can’t focus or learn new skills = self-care deficit)
o	Late
	Neuropsychiatric symptoms
•	Delusions or hallucinations
	Noncognitive neurological deficits
	Extrapyramidal motor movements
	Myoclonus
	Seizures
	Loss of bladder or bowel control
o	Dx:
	No confirmatory test 
	Rule out of other diseases
•	Hypothyroidism, b12 deficiency, depression
	MRI  diffuse atrophy (small brain)
o	Nursing: 
	Cognitive assessment (MMSE or MoCA)
	Check clients level of orientation, attention, and recall 
	Establish a baseline 
	Cholinesterase inhibitors (anticholinergics)
	Donepezil and rivastigmine
	NMDA receptor antagonist – memantine to retain cognitive function 
	Designate someone to administer medication or request medication in patch form 
	Legal issues (living will, POA, EOL)
	Structures and consistent daily routine to prevent anger and irritability
	Establish normal daytime and nighttime cycle – decrease stimulation (caffeine) and watching TV late at night
	Increase daytime exposure to light 
	Reorient the client 
	Establish a normal sleep cycle to promote overall well being 
	Safety – door sensor, grab bar, 
	Wander in a safe place 
	Medical identification bracelet
	Household hazards- gas appliances, throw rugs, toxic chemicals 
	Install grab bars in shower and bathroom
	Caregiver burden – respite care or LTC, support groups 
o	Behaviour Management
	Neuropsychiatric symptoms
•	If experiencing, do not negate experience, establish reality and reorient the client. Reassure they are safe, use familiar music of photographs, directions one step at a time, stand near client, use eye contact, use visual cues and gestures 
•	Do not challenge delusions

Basal Cell Carcinoma
-	Most common type of skin cancer from basal cells due to UV 
-	Low risk of metastasis but can destroy skin
-	Risk: 
o	Overexposure to UV light, sun exposure, tanning, history of sunburns, outdoor occupation 
o	Fair skin, red, blond hair, blue or green eyes, freckles, family history, numerous moles, older age
-	Features:
o	Develops in areas exposed to skin = head, neck, trunk
o	Slow growing 
o	Superficial: red pink macule or patch 
	May not associate it with cancer because it looks normal 
o	Nodular – skin coloured, pearly papule with telangiectasia (visible blood vessels)
o	Develops central crater over time
o	Progresses to ulceration with raised, pearly borders 
-	Tx:
o	Surgical excision or curettage and electrodesiccation 
o	Performed by HCP 
o	Prepare client
-	Teaching:
o	Prevention – frequent skin assessments 
o	Educate on skin cancer prevention = avoid tanning beds, limit sun exposure between 10-4, wear protective clothing, apply broad spectrum sun screen SPF > 15 even if in shade 
o	Apply 15-30 mins before exposure, reapply frequently especially if sweating or swimming
o	Apply even on cloudy days 


BPH
-	Affects males over 50
-	Gradual and abnormal prostate enlargement leading to voiding dysfunction due to compressed urethra 
-	Happens slowly
-	Cause is unknown/multifactorial 
-	Risks: HTN, insulin resistance, obesity 
-	Asymptomatic in early stages but as it enlarges we may see mild to moderate symptoms 
-	Features:
o	Weak urinary stream
o	Intermittency
o	Incomplete emptying
o	Hesitancy
o	Straining to void
o	Frequency, urgency, nocturia, incontinence 
-	Complications:
o	Hydronephrosis
o	Kidney dysfunction 
o	Pyelonephrosis 
o	Can affect both kidneys 
o	UTI
o	Cystitis 
o	Hematuria 
o	Hydroureter 
o	Bladder hypertrophy 
-	Dx:
o	Yearly digital rectal exam
o	Routine urinalysis for bacteria and WBC (infection)
o	Routine lab tests = serum BUN and creatinine
-	Tx:
o	Tamsulosin, doxazosin (alpha 1 receptor blockers)
	Relax smooth muscle in prostate and bladder neck 
	Works immediately 
o	Tell clients to expect orthostatic hypotension and client teaching
o	Finasteride, dutasteride
	Reduces prostate tissue volume but doesn’t decrease prostate size 
	Hormonal manipulation 
	Takes months to work and reduce symptoms
o	Saw palmetto extract but this is no proven efficacy
-	Nursing: 
o	Collect urinalysis (hematuria, bactetiuria) 
o	Assess for bladder distension
o	Assess for high postvoid residual volume (bladder scan)
o	Provide time and privacy while voiding 
o	Avoid diuretics like alcohol and caffeine 
o	Urinate frequently (every 2-3 hours)
o	Avoid constipation (puts pressure on the bladder) 
o	Encourage high fiber and lots of fluids 
o	Avoid medications that cause urinary retention: anticholinergics, antihistamines, antidecongestants

COPD
-	Progressive, irreversible, lower respiratory tract condition caused by: 
o	Alveolar enlargement  air trapping 
o	Inflammation and increased sputum production  airway obstruction 
o	Swelling closes the airway 
o	Leads to hyperinflation, flattens the diaphragm 
-	Causes:
o	Smoking 
o	Air pollution, environmental exposure 
o	Genetics (alpha 1 antitrypsin deficiency)
o	History of recurrent respiratory illnesses in childhood 
-	Features:
o	Hypoxia 
o	Respiratory complications 
o	Dyspnea (exertional at first, then at rest)
o	Wheezing
o	Chronic cough with sputum production 
o	Barrel chest as the lungs grow larger
o	Fatigue 
o	Decreased tissue perfusion = decreased energy
o	Flattened diaphragm = accessory muscle usage
o	Extremity clubbing 
o	Diagnosis – spirometry 
	Cxr
	Abg  increased paco2 and decreased pa02
	Co2 retention = respiratory acidosis 
o	Complications: 
	Right sided heart failure 
	Pulmonary hypertension  right sided heart failure (cor pulmonale) 
•	High jugular venous pressure (jugular vein distension)
•	Ascites
•	Peripheral edema
•	Secondary polycythemia 
•	Hypoxia  increased erythropoietin secretion 
•	Bluish red mucus membranes and skin 
•	High risk for pneumonia and ARDS
o	Management
	Tobacco cessartion
	Inhaled bronchodilators (salmeterol, tiotropium)
	Inhaled corticosteroids
	Guaifenesin (expectorant)
	Supplemental o2, maintain o2 around 90 with rest, exercise, and sleep
	Too much o2 suppresses the oxygen drive 
	Infection prevention, vaccines
	Pulmonary rehab
	Lung reduction surgery
o	Management during exacerbation
	Nebulized bronchodilators (albuterol and ipratropium)
	Oral or IV corticosteroids
	Abx
	Bipap or intubation
o	Nursing:
	Teach huff coughing
	Percussion and vibration
	Postural drainage 
	Huff coughing
•	Inhale through nose, lean forward and exhale sharply 2-3 times 
	Pursed lip breathing – opens airway and allows air to be expelled from the alveoli 
	Cool mist humidifier maintain hydration
	Sit upright 
	Nutrition 
	Drink fluids between meals
	Small frequent meal

Iron Deficiency Anemia
-	Reduction in RBC caused by lack of iron
-	Iron is needed for Hgb synthesis 
-	Results in microcytic and hypochromic rbc (hgb gives the red appearance) 
-	Leading cause of anemia 
-	Results from GI problems and also from blood loss
-	Causes: 
o	Decreased oral intake
o	Vegetarian or vegan diets
o	Decreased absorption of iron: celiac
o	Blood loss – heavy menstruation, GI bleeding 
o	Increased demand
o	Pregnancy or lactation
-	Risks: 
o	Infants
	Prematurity 
	Delayed introduction of solids 
	Exclusive breastfeeding after 6 months
	Consumption of cows milk prior to 1 year of age
	Excessive intake of cows milk after age 1
-	Features:
o	Initially asymptomatic and as it progreses:
	Fatigue, weakness
	Pale skin and mucous membranes
	Shortness of breath on exertion
	Tachycardia
	Fissures at corners of mouth
	Glossitis
	Concave fingernails
	Pica – pregnancy (but can happen to anyone)
	Inflammation of the lips (bumps in the corner of the lips)
-	Tx:
o	Oral replacement with ferrous sulfate  until hgb levels are normal (1-2 months)
	Take on an empty stomach 
	Do not take with milk or antacids as they bind together 
	Take between meals for maximal absorption 
	Take with citrus juice (high in vitamin c and increase iron absorption)
	Use a straw and brush teeth after taking
	Increase fluid intake to prevent constipation
	Black stools are expected 
o	Diet: 
	Meats and leafy green vegetables 
	Poultry 
	Nuts
	Whole grains
	Vitamin c: Citrus foods, tomatoes, strawberries

o	Place client in semi fowlers
o	Administer corticosteroids
o	Prepare for IV immunoglobulin 
o	Prepare for plasmapheresis or IV immunoglobulin 

Osteoporosis
-	Rate of bone resorption is faster than bone formation = porous bones
-	Weak bones 
-	Risk for fractures especially wrist, hip, vertebral column 
-	Risk:
o	Advanced age = women over 60
o	Postmenopausal status
o	Low estrogen (women)
o	Men = low testosterone
o	Low body weight, thin
o	Smoking
o	Excessive alcohol intake
o	Sedentary lifestyle 
o	Secondary osteoporosis = vitamin d deficiency, malabsorption 
o	Glucocorticoid use
-	Dx: no symptoms unless fracture is present 
o	Loss of bone mass (DEXA scan)
o	Low impact trauma  fracture
o	Vertebral compression fracture:
	Spontaneous with no trauma
	Sudden back pain
	Can be multiple fractures
-	Complications:
o	Multiple fractures can lead to kyphosis 
o	(hunchback)
o	Loss of height and worsening back pain 
-	Nursing:
o	Medications: vitamin D, calcium carbonate supplements, bisphosphonates
o	Diet rich in calcium and vitamin d, milk, fish, tofu, almonds 
o	Adequate sun exposure
o	Smoking cessation
o	Limit alcohol 
o	Weight bearing exercises like walking every day 
o	Fall prevention 

Pleural Effusion
-	Excess fluid in pleural space 
-	Not a disease but an indication of disease
-	Occurs secondary to:
o	Heart failure
o	Nephrotic syndrome
o	Pneumonia
o	Malignancy
-	Features
o	Dyspnea, cough
o	Sharp, non-radiating chest pain  increases during inspiration
o	Decreased breath sounds and movement of the chest on the affected side 
o	Dullness to percussion 
o	Tachycardia
o	Diagnosed via CXR, diagnostic thoracentesis to test fluid for malignant cells or microorganisms
-	Complication:
o	Empyema – pus collection 
o	The longer we have the PE the higher the chance of empyema developing
	Fever, chills, toxic appearance
	Weight loss
	Decreased appetite
	Leukocytosis
-	Nursing
o	Elevate HOB
o	Therapeutic pleural drainage via thoracentesis 
	Pt lays over the bed table 
	Chest x ray after procedure
	Monitor breath sounds
	Encourage coughing and deep breathing
Traction
-	Used to realign long bones following complete fracture 
-	Applies bidirectional pulling force to align and immobilize the fractured bones
-	One side weights and the other side body weight or weights 
-	Two main types: skin, skeletal
-	Skin/buck
o	Pulling force is applied directly to the skin by using boots, splints, tape
o	Temporary measure. Usually the first type applied 
o	Can only be used 48-72 hours before surgery or the use of long term skeletal fraction
o	Can only use small weights to prevent nerve and tissue damage (5-10 lbs) or (2-3 with infants)
o	Complications:
	Skin breakdown
	Detachment of traction device 
	Monitor skin frequently 
-	Weights:
o	Pulling force realigns bone and prevents shortening of limbs
o	Weight continuously suspended in the air, hanging freely off the edge of the bed because they need gravity 
o	Supine position with foot of bed elevated 15 degrees
o	Limb should remain straight and in a neutral position 
-	Skeletal traction
o	Traction is applied directly to the bone
o	Can be used up to 4 months
o	Larger weight can be sued 5-45 lbs or 2.3-20.4 kg
o	Screws, wires, and or pins are surgically inserted directly into the fractured bone
o	Pins are exposed and attached to a pulley system with free hanging weights
o	Patient must be supine and perpendicular to the ends of the bed
o	Affected extremity in proper body alignment
o	HOB is flat or semi fowler but not higher than that
o	Trapeze for upper body movement or for repositioning 
o	Free-hanging weights 
-	Halo external fixation device: static traction
o	Stabilizes a cervical or high thoracic fracture
o	Cannot have damage to ligaments or spinal cord
o	Halo ring is applied around head and attached to vest when spine Is stable 
o	Keep the correct sized wrench available at all times because it needs to be removed for CPR
o	Keep vest clean and dry 
o	Clean pin site daily
-	Complications:
o	Improperly maintained skeletal traction can cause bones to become misaligned 
o	Muscle spasms
o	Skeletal traction can lead to infection at pin site  osteomyelitis  make sure to clean pins daily 
o	Skin breakdown 
o	Prolonged immobilization
	Atelectasis 
	Constipation
	DVT
o	Nursing:
	Check pins frequently to watch for infection
	If it becomes infected increases risk for osteomyelitis or sepsis 
	Check pulses and cap refill frequently 
	Prevent immobility related complications
	Incentive spirometer
	High fiber diet, stool softeners, fluids
	Anticoagulants
	Monitor skin and reposition frequently
	Promote urination with bedpan, urinal, intermittent catheterization 

Transfusion Associated Circulatory Overload
-	Life threatening blood transfusion reaction caused by rapid rate or from a client receiving a large amount of blood product over a certain period of time
-	Circulatory cannot handle the volume
-	Can happen during or 6-12 hours after transfusion
-	Risk:
o	Younger than 3, greater than 60
o	Preexisting cardiac or kidney impairments
-	Features: 
o	Pulmonary edema  respiratory distress
	Dyspnea, tachypnea, hypoxemia, crackles
o	Jugular venous distension
o	Peripheral edema
o	Tachycardia
o	Bounding pulses
o	Hypertension
-	Tx: 
o	Stop transfusion immediately 
o	Position in high fowler
o	Supplemental oxygen
o	Diuretics 
o	Notify blood bank to test the blood
o	Daily weights
o	Intake output

Hip Dysplasia (peds)
-	Range of hip abnormalities as the hip joint is not securely joined
-	Presents in infancy or at birth, on a spectrum
-	Risk
o	Large infant
o	Breech position
o	Family history of DDH
o	Tight lower extremity swaddling
	Hips should be bent up and out when swaddled 
o	Positioning devices or car seats
-	Features:
o	Spectrum
o	Asymmetrical gluteal or thigh folds 
o	Limited ROM- wont be able to have hip abduction 
o	Shortening of femur 
o	If not diagnosed early on, there are signs that they may notice later on
	Positive Trendelenburg sign
•	Child stands on affected leg and picks up unaffected legs while being unable to bear weight while staying central
•	Trunk moves asymmetrically

o	Avoid lotions or creams
o	Padding under the straps to avoid friction on skin
o	Massage skin under straps to stimulate blood flow
o	Wear shirt and socks under harness
-	After 6 months of age = possible surgery
o	Followed by spica cast
o	Regular cast on affected joint
	Keep cast dry and intact
	Encourage not sticking anything in cast 
	Proper skin hygiene
	Sponge baths 
	Check for skin breakdown 
	No lotions or creams
	Proper technique for diaper changes – limited range of motion

Molluscum Contagiosum
-	Caused by pox virus
-	Affects anyone 
-	Firm, skin coloured dome shaped papules
-	Direct contact – skin to skin, sexual contact
-	Contaminated objects, towels, surfaces
-	Common in warm climates and daycares, schools
-	Features:
o	Asymptomatic
o	Dome shaped with central umbilication
o	Firm flesh coloured popular lesions
o	Diameter 1-5 mm
o	Can appear anywhere on the skin except for palms and soles 
-	Nursing:
o	Self-limiting = goes away on its own
o	Resolves in few months in immunocompetent individuals (takes months to years)
o	No treatment
o	There are chemical removing agents tretinoin or cantharidin, cryotherapy, but this is cosmetic only 
o	Treatment can be painful
o	Can return to school or daycare immediately. 

Tinea
-	Superficial fungal infection
-	Highly contagious 
-	Transmission via direct contact or via surface objects
-	Features: 
o	Tinea corporis (ringworm)
	Scaly, pruritic patch 
	Circular or oval, known as ringworm
o	Tinea capitis 
	On scalp
	Round scaly patches on the scalp, red and itchy, alopecia
	Needs oral antifungal
o	Tinea pedis (athlete’s foot) 
	Scaly red itchy rash typically between the toes or on the sole of the foot
o	Tinea unguium 
	Thick, brittle, nail with yellow discoloration 
	Needs oral antifungal
o	Tinea cruris (jock itch)
	Red itchy scaly patches in groin, most common in athletes 
-	Nursing:
o	Topical antifungal (miconazole)
o	May need oral antifungal if the topical one doesn’t work 
o	Keep skin clean and dry
o	Avoid sharing personal items
o	Clip nails short
o	Change socks and underwear every day
o	Wear shoes with good circulation 

Varicella
-	Caused by varicella zoster virus
-	Causes two forms of illness
-	Transmitted via direct contact and aerosol droplets 
o	1-2 days before rash appears 
o	Contagious until all lesions have crusted 
-	Lesion stages: maculopapular (small red pimples), vesicular (blister), pustular (pus), crusting (no longer contagious)
-	Prevention: vaccine
-	Infection reactivation: herpes zoster
o	Latency and travels to anterior root and can be reactivated with aging or immunosuppression 
o	Shows up as shingles and is very painful – affects the nerves
-	Features:
o	Prodrome: mild cold
	Low grade fever, fatigue, cough, anorexia
o	Followed by: pruritic, maculopapular rash on face, trunk, extremities
o	Crops of lesions in different stages 
-	Nursing:
o	Prevent secondary infections from scratching 
o	Soothing lotions (calamine)
o	Keep fingernails short 
o	Mittens during sleep

o	Avoid overheating 
o	Wear loose fitting clothing 
o	Bathe in cool oatmeal baths 
o	Hand hygiene 
-	Management
o	Hospital setting
	Airborne, droplet, and contact precautions
	Immunocompromised child may need an antiviral such as acyclovir
•	At risk for life threatening complications so implement acyclovir quickly
	Supportive care like acetaminophen and gravol 
	Isolate child until all lesions have crusted over
