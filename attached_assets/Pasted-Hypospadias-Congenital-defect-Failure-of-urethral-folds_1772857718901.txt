Hypospadias
-	Congenital defect 
-	Failure of urethral folds to fuse in utero
-	Abnormal urethral opening
-	Usually develops on the underside of the penis 
-	Features:
o	Urethral opening between the glans and the perineum on the underside of the penis
o	If left untreated can cause toilet training problems, frequent urinary tract infections
o	Inability to achieve erections
-	Nursing:
o	Delay circumcision
o	Prepare child for surgery age 6-12 months
o	Temporary catheter is used to empty bladder
o	Postop meds:
	Abx
	Analgesics
	Antispasmodics or anticholinergic meds (oxybutynin)
-	Teaching:
o	Urinary diversion for 5-10 days
o	No tub baths during this time
o	Give sponge baths
o	Avoid straddle toys and strenuous activity 
o	Offer fluids frequently 

Infant Botulism
-	Type of food poisoning of clostridium botulinum spores
-	Colonize in immature GI tract that inhibits release of acetylcholine = muscle paralysis 
-	Features:
o	Paralysis occurs symmetrically and from the top down 
o	Hypotonia and decreased head control
o	Ptosis
o	Absent gag reflex
o	Poor feeding
o	Poor swallowing
o	Constipation due to lack of peristalsis
-	Nursing:
o	Recognize symptoms early to avoid respiratory failure 
o	Accessory muscle usage in infants or younger babies
o	Nasal flaring
o	Tripod position 
o	IV botulism immune globulin (BIG IV)
	Reduces severity and duration of symptoms
o	Supportive care
	Mechanical ventilation
	Enteral tube feedings
o	Prevention: avoid honey before age 12 months 


Newborn Circumcision
-	Methods:
o	Clamp – deadens nerves and cuts
o	Plastibell – falls off in a week due to cut off blood supply 
-	Clinical significance
o	Reduction of STI including HIV, prevention of STI 
-	Typically performed for healthy newborns during first week
-	Contraindications:
o	Preterm infants – do not meet weight requirement 
o	Sick newborn – can increase likelihood of complications 
o	Hypospadias, epispadias 
-	Complications:
o	Hemorrhage
o	Difficulty voiding (edema)
-	Nursing:
o	Prior to procedure
	Education and consent – check that it has been done and is completed
	Circumcision is a choice
o	Apply topical numbing cream if prescribed
o	During procedure:
	Comfort/pain management
	Swaddle upper body with warm blanket
	Nonnutritive sucking 
	Oral sucrose (brain can only process sugar or pain but not both at the same time)
	Sterile technique
o	Postprocedure
	Apply loose fitting diapers 
	Apply petroleum jelly with each diaper change 
•	Not indicated for plastibell method
	Monitor for infection – bleeding, edema, purulent discharge 
	Active bleeding – apply gentle pressure with sterile gauze 
	A few spots of blood on diaper is okay 
	If bleeding does not stop – notify provider 
	Monitor urinary output 
	Do not use soap or alcohol based wipes
	Do not remove plastibell device early 
	Yellow exudate is normal – do not be concerned and do not remove forcefully 
	Green discharge = infection

-	Symptoms occur within 4-6 weeks after birth
-	Angry outburst
-	Sleep disturbances
-	Spontaneous episodes of sobbing
-	Intense and persistent irritability and sadness
-	Ruminations of guilt and an inadequacy as a parent
-	Appetite changes and food cravings, leading to weight gain
-	Lack of interest in the newborn 
-	Thoughts of harming the newborn
-	Contributing factors:
o	History of depression
o	Unintended pregnancy
o	Lack of support
o	Relationship conflict
o	Adverse pregnancy outcomes or traumatic birth
o	Sleep deprivation
-	Nursing:
o	Screen for postpartum depression

	Assess for thoughts of suicide and infanticide
	Observe interactions
	Involve family in developing a plan
	Antidepressants (SSRI)
	Psychotherapy
	Postpartum support group

Postpartum Psychosis
-	Rare but is the most severe form of perinatal mood disorders
-	Medical emergency
-	Risk for suicide and infanticide
-	Triggers:
o	Rapid hormonal shifts following childbirth
o	Decrease in estrogen and progesterone
o	Sleep deprivation
o	Typically manifests within 2 weeks postpartum
-	Features:
o	Depressive symptoms
o	Withdrawal from others, tearfulness
o	Accompanied by acute mania, abnormally increased energy levels, severe insomnia, agitation, disorganized behaviour
o	Psychotic symptoms:
	Paranoid delusions, hallucinations (often related to the infant)
-	Risks:
o	Obstetric complications
o	Personal or family history of mental illness such as bipolar or postpartum psychosis
o	Discontinuing psychiatric medications before pregnancy 
-	Nursing:
o	Inpatient hospitalization
o	Pharmacological interventions
o	Mood stabilizers (lithium or valproic acid)
o	Antipsychotics (olanzapine)
o	Benzos (alprazolam for severe insomnia)
o	Assess for delusions, hallucinations, risk for suicide or infanticide
Gestational Diabetes
-	Pregestational – normal type one or type 2 diabetes 
-	Gestational 
o	Physiologic changes of pregnancy cause impaired blood glucose regulations  pancreas cannot compensate  gestational diabetes mellitus
o	Screening around 24-48 weeks gestation when pregnancy related hyperglycemia peaks 
o	Screening: one hour glucose challenge test
o	No fasting prior to test
o	Between 24-28 weeks gestation ingest sugar and then check blood glucose one hour after
o	NPO for one hour after ingesting
o	Diagnosis:
	3 hour glucose test:
	Required if 1 hour is elevated
	Fasting required prior to test
	Abnormal when 2 or more of the 4 levels are elevated
o	Risks:
	Obesity
	Excessive weight gain during pregnancy
	Family history of diabetes
	Previous large for gestational age newborn 
	Polycystic ovarian syndrome
o	Complications:
	Pregestational: Congenital heart and neural tube defects 
	Gestational: maternal development of type 2 DM
	Fetal macrosomia  shoulder dystocia 
	Polyhydramnios 
	Preeclampsia 
	Stillbirth 
	Newborns tend to have hypoglycemia – babys pancreas continues to make lots of insulin due to being used to high glucose environment
o	Nursing:
	Modify diet – carbs in moderation
	Daily exercise
	Monitor blood glucose
	Signs and symptoms of hypoglycemia and hyperglycemia
•	Hypoglycemia: 
o	Altered mental status, shakiness, diaphoresis, clammy skin, tachycardia, hunger, weakness, fatigue
	Insulin
	Monitor
	Daily fetal kick counts and report decreased movement
	Blood pressure
	Vaginal or urinary infections

Alcohol Withdrawal Syndrome
-	CNS depressant
-	Symptoms start 6-24 hours after last drink
-	Wide range of symptoms
-	Mild withdrawal symptoms
-	Alcohol hallucinations
-	Withdrawal seizures
-	Delirium tremens (life threatening)
-	Mild withdrawal: autonomic hyperactivity, anxiety, irritability, tremors, diaphoresis, headache, nausea or vomiting
-	Moderate to severe: alcohol hallucinosis, auditory hallucinations, tactile sensations, visual disturbances – may have no other features – normal vital signs or just a slight increase
-	Features:
o	Lowers seizure threshold 
-	Delirium tremens:
o	Severe alcohol withdrawal 
o	Extreme autonomic hyperactivity
o	Fever
o	Severe hypertension
o	Drenching sweats
o	Rapid onset altered mental status
-	Nursing:
o	Benzodiazepines
o	Lorazepam (PO or IV)
o	Diazepam or chlordiazepoxide 
o	Replace vitamins: thiamine, folic acid 
o	Fluid and electrolyte replacement 
o	Normal saline 
o	Magnesium, potassium, phosphorus
o	Monitor cardiac rate and rhythm 
o	Evaluate clients behaviour and severity of withdrawal
	Standard assessment tool (CIWA)
o	Calm, quiet environment with minimal lighting
o	Long term recovery: naltrexone, identify relapse triggers, activities for stress relief 
o	Set goals for personal growth 
o	Express accountability for maladaptive drinking

Anorexia Nervosa
-	Hormonal effects on brain, appetite and stress
-	Cooccurring mental illnesses
-	Ideal body type portrayed in the media
-	Restrictive eating disorder
-	Fasting, dieting, excessive exercise 
-	May include purging 
-	Most commonly seen in adolescents and young adults 
-	Can lead to life threatening complications due to electrolyte imbalances and arrhythmias
-	Features:
o	Confusion, fainting
o	Dry skin, lanugo, hair loss
o	Decreased libido, amenorrhea
o	Low BMI < 18.5
o	Fatigue
o	Cold intolerance
o	Hypothermia – body is trying to conserve energy
o	Hypotension
o	Bradycardia
o	Emaciation
o	Constipation
o	Muscle wasting, osteoporosis, stress fracture (decrease in calcium)
-	Mental health features:
o	Low self esteem and body dysmorphia
o	Unusual or ritualistic behaviours around food
o	Preoccupation with food activities
o	Need for control
o	Counting calories
o	Manipulative behaviours
o	Social withdrawal 
o	May move food around the plate instead of eating
-	Indications for hospitalization
o	<70% of expected weight or BMI <15kg/m2
o	Reintroduce nutrition slowly because of the risk of refeeding syndrome
o	Hemodynamic instability 
o	Refusal to eat
o	Suicidal ideation
o	Psychosis
-	Nursing: 
o	Assist with nutritional rehab 
o	Assess for thoughts of suicide or self-harm
o	Monitor for ECG changes
o	Correct electrolyte imbalances
o	IV fluids to increase blood pressure and perfusion
o	Enteral feeding or parenteral nutrition
o	CBT
o	Reinforce parental monitoring during meals for adolescents 
o	Monitor for changes in weight and caloric intake

-	Inattention, distractability, hyperactivity
-	Symptoms at least six months
-	Are present in at least 2 environments
-	Genetic predisposition 
-	Present before age 12
-	Not better explained by substance use, mental disorder, medical condition
-	Cause significant distress or impairment
-	Features:
o	Inattention >6 symptoms
o	Difficulty maintaining focus
o	Does not listen or follow instructions
o	Forgetful, loses things
o	Disorganized
o	Hyperactive, impulsive
o	Interrupts others, disrupts 
o	Talks excessively
o	Fidgeting
o	Restlessness

o	Psychotherapy, behavioural therapy (positive reinforcement)
o	Pharmacotherapy:
	Stimulants:
•	Methylphenidate, amphetamines
	SNRI
•	Atomoxetine

o	Consistent home and classroom environment
o	Minimize distractions (turn off tv)

o	Regular and frequent breaks in activity 
o	Medication teaching
	Appetite suppression and weight loss

Avoidant Personality Disorder
-	Cluster c
-	Stem from an intense fear of criticism and rejection
-	Present in a variety of environments 
-	Causes impairment or distress 
-	Similar to social anxiety disorder 
-	Negative self-evaluation in comparison to others, feelings of personal inadequacy, fear rejection and criticism, limited intimate relationships, need a lot of reassurance and unconditional acceptance, avoids interpersonal contact 
-	Low self-esteem
-	Feelings of inferiority 
-	Reluctant to try new activities because of fear of rejection of new people
-	Can lead to depression, anxiety, anger because they cannot form relationships
-	SSRI, SNRI
-	Increase levels of serotonin and norepinephrine in the brain 
-	Beta-adrenergic blockers can also help 
-	Behavioural therapies – CBT – helps identify what causes anxiety and how to handle it
-	Social skills training 

Schizophrenia
-	Complex psychiatric disorder where the person is unable to react with reality normally
-	Genetics and environment play a major role
-	Chronic condition caused by imbalances in the neurotransmitter dopamine
-	Positive symptoms = dopamine excess
-	Negative = dopamine deficit
-	Features:
o	Positive symptoms: 
	Hallucinations – based on senses, false sensory perception in the absence of stimuli, auditory, hearing voices or noises, visual = seeing things or people, tactile – feeling bodily sensations, olfactory – smelling odors
•	Monitor cues, ask about content of hallucination 
•	Decrease stimuli or assist the client to another area
•	Provide distraction – music with headphones
•	Explain verbally what is real and what is unreal, do not joke or judge
•	Engage through structures, small activities 
•	Check for suicidal or homicidal ideation due to command hallucinations
	Delusions- based on beliefs and ideas 
•	Provide care with active listening and empathy 
•	Do not argue or try to convince that the delusions are false
•	Shift conversation on reality based topics rather than the delusion 
•	Canned or prepackaged foods
•	Open medication in front of the client 
•	Check mouth and palms after medication administration 
•	Positive reinforcement 
	Disorganized thought/incoherent speech – derailment, made up words, diverges from the main topic, word salad: jumbling with no meaning, echolalia: repeats what they say over and over
	Disorganized or agitated behaviour – catatonia (immobile, peculiar postures, unresponsive
o	Negative
	Apathy
	Lack of motivation
	Poor hygiene and self care deficits
	Asociality = withdrawal
	Depression
	Affective flattening – emotional flattening/monotone speech
	Anhedonia – inability to experience pleasure
	Alogia: lack of speech
-	Treatments:
o	Antipsychotics – help with positive symptoms 
	First generation (haloperidol) 
•	Side effects
	Second generation (atypical) 
•	Apriprazole, olanzapine, quetiapine, risperidone
o	Side effects: usually weight gain 
o	Long acting – improves adherence 
-	Nursing:
o	General:
	Build client trust 
	Be honest and consistent in interactions
	Frequent and short interaction times
	Sit calmly in the clients room if needed
	Avoid touching the client without informing 
	Maintain a balanced neutral approach (not overly warm)
	Explain procedures step by step
	Initiate activities on a one on one basis  then small groups
o	Negative symptoms:
	Social skills training
	Measure client readiness to engage with others
	Praise progress towards increased interaction
	Assist with ADLs
	Catatonia: fluid and nutrition is priority 

Obsessive Compulsive Disorder
-	Deficiency of serotonin in the brain 
-	Dx:
o	Significant distress and impairment, compulsions are so distressing to patient they cannot carry out activities of daily living

o	Not better explained by substance abuse or other condition
-	Features:
o	Obsessions: persistent and intrusive thoughts, impulses, or images
o	Attempts to suppress or ignore obsessive thoughts 
o	Compulsions: ritualistic, repetitive behaviours performed to decrease stress/anxiety
	Repeated hand washing  skin breakdown 
o	Patients are aware that it is irrational but their stress and anxiety get so high they need to do the thing
-	Nursing:	
o	CBT
	Anxiety triggers for ritualistic behaviours  relaxation/redirection techniques (deep breathing)
	Allow them time to do the rituals then slowly decrease the time 
	Initially allow time for and then begin to limit rituals
	Positive reinforcement for non ritualistic behaviour 
	Predictable, structured schedule 
	SSRI
	Tricyclic antidepressants

Serotonin Syndrome
-	Excess serotonergic activity in the brain 
-	Does not occur naturally and is a result of taking meds or supplements
-	Causes: 
o	Antidepressants initiation or dose increase 
	SSRI
	SNRI
	MAOI
	Important to know there are many different drug interactions which can increase likelihood for serotonin syndrome
	St Johns wort, tramadol, and ondansetron 
	Cocaine, amphetamines 
-	Symptoms:
o	Onset within 2-72 hours 
o	Autonomic hyperactivity 
o	High fever, diarrhea, HTN, tachycardia, sweating
o	Neuromuscular excitability
	Tremors, clonus, hyperreflexia, shivering
	Mental status changes 
	Agitation, restlessness, confusion, hallucinations 
o	Can lead to life-threatening complications such as CV shock, seizures, death 
	Foot is moved into a dorsiflexion movement and then starts to shake 
-	Nursing:
o	Discontinue medications and other meds that will interfere
o	Give benzodiazepines
o	Make sure oxygen is over 94%
o	Continuous cardiac monitoring 
o	IV fluids to prevent rhabdomyolysis due to increased muscle contractility
o	Clonus causes increased body temp so have ice packs and a cooling blanket available 
o	Place pt on seizure precautions and monitor for seizures 

Bipolar Disorder
-	Bipolar 1: 
o	Manic phases up to several days replaced by depression phases
-	Bipolar 2: 
o	Hypomanic and then followed by depression 
-	Recurring and remitting episodes 
-	Pathogenesis is unknown but there may be a biological and psychological factor
-	Manic phase:
o	DIG FAST: elevated, irritable or expansive mood plus the following:
	Distractibility (cannot maintain focus)
	Impulsivity or indiscretion
	Grandiosity
	Flight of ideas/racing thoughts 
	Activity increase
	Sleep deficit, decreased need for sleep 
	Talkativeness/pressured speech (may not realize how much they are talking)
o	Psychotic symptoms, delusions and hallucinations and marked functional impairment 
-	Hypomanic phase:
o	Similar to manic but less severe 
o	Don’t need to be hospitalized as no functional impairment
-	Depressive phase
o	Daily sadness, difficulty concentration
o	Eating problems
o	Feeling worthless, hopeless or guilty
o	Loss of pleasure in activities once enjoyed
o	Difficulty sleeping 
-	Management:
o	Mood stabilizers 
	Lithium, valproic acid (take a few weeks to be effective)
o	Second generation antipsychotic
	Risperidone, lurasidone, olanzapine, quetiapine
-	Nursing
o	Manic phase:
	Safety and physiological needs (maslow – lower tiers)
	High protein high calorie finger foods 
	Supplements for weight loss, malnutrition
	Daily weights 
	Set limits on manipulative behaviour 
	Positive reinforcement for appropriate behaviour 
	Low stimulus environment to promote rest
	Assist with personal hygiene – promote normal clothing
	Check for psychotic symptoms – present reality without arguing – comfort and acknowledge, monitor for suicidal ideation, remove all hazardous items, assist in identifying feelings of frustration and aggression, encourage client to talk instead of acting out 
	Teach client and family the cyclic nature of the symptoms 
	Supportive services – crisis hotline, support groups, individual therapy

Intimate Partner Violence

-	Cuts across all social groups
-	Risks:
o	Perpetrator:
	Controlling behaviour, blaming the survivor, has unrealistic expectations, violence outside the home, substance use, hx of injuring a partner, access to firearms, poor impulse control 
o	Survivor
	Attempts to leave relationship 
	Seeks outside help
	Pregnancy
	Becomes more independent
	Lack of support systems 
o	Concerning signs:
	Physical manifestations
	Behavioural symptoms in survivor
	Frequent visits to ER
	Inconsistent explanation of injuries
	STI
	Overly attentive or intrusive partner

o	Tension building:
	Withdrawing affection, irritable, minor abuses occur

o	Identify support systems
o	Document findings on a body map 
o	Assess for thoughts of self harm 
o	Assess client readiness to end relationship

Borderline Personality Disorder

-	Emotional dysregulation: mood swings
-	Unstable relationships 
-	Poor self image
-	Lack a clear sense of self and have self image distortion that causes significant distress
-	Starts in early adulthood 
-	Features:
o	Poor self image
o	Intense fear of abandonment
o	Splitting – coping mechanism to view others as wonderful or horrible, cant understand that both good and bad exist
o	Mood instability

o	Transient paranoia or distrust

o	Impulsivity

o	Pharmacotherapy:
	Atypical antipsychotics  decrease anger 
•	Apripiprazole, quetiapine
	Mood stabilizers
•	Lithium, lamotrigine
	SSRI
•	Sertraline, escitalopram
