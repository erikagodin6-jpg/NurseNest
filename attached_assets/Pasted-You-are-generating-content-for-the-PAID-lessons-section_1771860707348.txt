GLOBAL CONTENT RULES (MANDATORY)
1.	Cognitive Level & Depth
Write at a licensure-exam clinical reasoning level rather than an introductory educational level. Every concept must explain causal relationships and physiologic logic. Avoid generic textbook phrasing, oversimplified descriptions, or superficial symptom lists. Emphasize why findings occur, not merely what occurs.
Content must explicitly address:
•	Cellular or molecular disturbances when relevant
•	Tissue and organ-level consequences
•	Disrupted physiologic pathways
•	Compensatory responses and maladaptive effects
•	Mechanistic explanation of clinical manifestations
•	Pathogenesis of complications
•	Biological targets of treatments
Descriptions must follow cause → mechanism → manifestation logic.
 
2.	Pathophysiology Depth Requirements
For each disorder, describe the underlying cellular or biochemical disruptions, the resulting changes in tissue or organ function, and the physiologic consequences that produce observable clinical findings. Explain symptom development mechanistically. Replace descriptive statements with causal reasoning.
Example standard:
Avoid: “Causes edema.”
Use: “Reduced plasma oncotic pressure leads to intravascular fluid shifts, promoting interstitial fluid accumulation and dependent edema.”
 
3.	Exam-Relevance Constraint
Include only information that is realistically testable within nursing education contexts, including REX-PN, NCLEX-RN, and NP-level examinations where appropriate. Exclude rare trivia, esoteric research details, historical commentary, or specialist-only medical management.
 
4.	Strict Professional Scope Enforcement
Generate content that reflects role-appropriate cognitive and clinical expectations.
•	RPN Lessons: Focus on assessment findings, symptom interpretation grounded in pathophysiology, monitoring priorities, expected provider orders, nursing interventions, patient safety considerations, and escalation triggers. Exclude independent diagnostic reasoning, prescribing decisions, or advanced medical management.
•	RN Lessons: Include deeper physiologic reasoning, interpretation of trends, recognition of complex deterioration patterns, and understanding of advanced therapies. Exclude NP-exclusive autonomy or unnecessary medical specialization.
•	NP Lessons: Include full clinical reasoning depth, diagnostic logic, differential considerations, investigation strategies, pharmacologic mechanisms, and management rationale.
Do not duplicate identical text across roles. Adjust depth instead.
 
5.	Narrative Structural Template (MANDATORY)
Present each lesson using the following labeled sections. Write in full sentences and cohesive clinical prose rather than bullet points.
Pathophysiology and Cellular Mechanisms
Describe the initiating disturbances at the cellular or biochemical level, including signaling abnormalities, inflammatory processes, structural damage, perfusion changes, hormonal effects, or metabolic disruptions. Explain how these disturbances alter normal physiology.
Clinical Manifestations with Mechanistic Explanation
Explain why hallmark signs and symptoms develop based on physiologic disruption. Link manifestations directly to underlying mechanisms.
Compensatory and Systemic Responses
Describe adaptive or maladaptive responses activated by the disorder, such as neurohormonal activation, buffering systems, vascular responses, or metabolic compensation.
High-Risk Complications and Pathogenesis
Explain why complications occur, emphasizing deterioration pathways and physiologic failure patterns.
Treatment Targets and Rationale
Explain what therapies attempt to correct, inhibit, or stabilize at the physiologic or cellular level. Focus on mechanisms of action rather than listing treatments.
Nursing Priorities and Monitoring Logic
Describe assessment priorities, monitoring strategies, and safety considerations derived directly from pathophysiology.
Red Flags and Clinical Deterioration Patterns
Identify early indicators of instability and explain why they signify worsening physiology.
 
6.	Clinical Pearls Section (EXCEPTION TO NARRATIVE RULE)
Conclude every lesson with a bullet-point section labeled Clinical Pearls and Exam Traps. This section should contain only high-yield exam-relevant insights, commonly tested patterns, frequently confused conditions, safety priorities, or classic distractors.
 
7.	Language and Quality Safeguards
Use precise clinical language. Avoid repetition, filler content, vague statements, decorative language, emojis, or generic textbook tone. Do not fabricate statistics, drug dosages, or unsupported claims. Correct spelling automatically and eliminate duplicate disorders. Maintain strict clinical accuracy.
