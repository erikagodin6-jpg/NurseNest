Blood Transfusion Reactions
Comprehensive Clinical Recognition & Management Lesson
Learning Objectives
By the end of this lesson, learners will be able to:
Classify acute vs delayed transfusion reactions
Recognize hallmark signs and symptoms of each reaction type
Differentiate life-threatening from benign reactions
Initiate immediate nursing and medical management steps
Understand underlying pathophysiology
Apply prevention strategies in clinical practice
1. Why Transfusion Reactions Occur






4
Transfusion reactions occur when:
Donor antigens react with recipient antibodies
Recipient antibodies react with donor plasma proteins
Immune activation causes inflammation
Circulatory overload develops
Contaminated blood introduces infection
Reactions are classified as:
Acute (within minutes to 24 hours)
Delayed (days to weeks later)
2. Immediate Nursing Priority (For ANY Suspected Reaction)
If a reaction is suspected:
STOP the transfusion immediately
Maintain IV access with normal saline
Assess airway, breathing, circulation
Notify physician & blood bank
Recheck patient ID and blood product
Monitor vital signs
Send blood bag and tubing to lab
Never restart the same blood unit.
3. Acute Transfusion Reactions
A. Acute Hemolytic Transfusion Reaction (AHTR)
Cause
ABO incompatibility (most severe)
Pathophysiology
Recipient antibodies destroy donor RBCs → hemolysis → free hemoglobin → kidney injury → shock






4
Symptoms
Fever
Chills
Flank pain
Hypotension
Hemoglobinuria (dark urine)
Chest tightness
Anxiety
Severity
Medical emergency
Management
Stop transfusion
IV fluids
Maintain urine output
Treat shock
Monitor renal function
B. Febrile Non-Hemolytic Reaction
Cause
Recipient antibodies reacting to donor leukocytes
Symptoms
Fever
Chills
Mild discomfort
No hemolysis
Severity
Common and usually mild
Management
Stop transfusion temporarily
Antipyretics
Restart only if serious reaction ruled out
C. Allergic (Mild) Reaction
Cause
Hypersensitivity to donor plasma proteins
Symptoms
Urticaria
Itching
Rash
No fever, no hypotension.
Management
Stop transfusion
Antihistamines
May restart if mild and symptoms resolve
D. Anaphylactic Reaction
Cause
IgA deficiency with anti-IgA antibodies
Symptoms
Hypotension
Bronchospasm
Angioedema
Shock
Management
Stop transfusion
Epinephrine
Airway support
IV fluids
Life-threatening emergency.
E. Transfusion-Associated Circulatory Overload (TACO)
Cause
Volume overload

Symptoms
Dyspnea
Hypertension
Pulmonary edema
Crackles
JVD
High Risk
Elderly

F. Transfusion-Related Acute Lung Injury (TRALI)
Cause
Donor antibodies trigger lung inflammation
Symptoms
Sudden hypoxia
Respiratory distress
Bilateral pulmonary infiltrates
Hypotension
Occurs within 6 hours.
Management
Stop transfusion
Oxygen
Ventilatory support
Can resemble ARDS.
G. Septic (Bacterial) Reaction
Cause
Contaminated blood product
Symptoms
High fever
Hypotension
Rigors
Shock
Management
Stop transfusion
Blood cultures
IV antibiotics
4. Delayed Transfusion Reactions
A. Delayed Hemolytic Reaction
Occurs days to weeks later.
Cause
Previously sensitized antibodies (e.g., anti-Kell)
Symptoms
Mild fever
Jaundice
Fatigue
Decreasing hemoglobin
Usually less severe than acute hemolytic reactions.
B. Transfusion-Associated Graft vs Host Disease (TA-GVHD)
Rare but fatal.
Cause
Donor lymphocytes attack recipient tissues
Symptoms
Fever
Rash
Liver dysfunction
Bone marrow suppression
Prevention: irradiated blood products in immunocompromised patients.

6. Prevention Strategies
• Verify patient identity carefully
• Double-check blood type
• Monitor closely during first 15 minutes
• Administer slowly in high-risk patients
• Use leukocyte-reduced blood products
• Irradiate blood for immunocompromised patients
Most fatal reactions are due to clerical error.
7. Early Warning Signs Nurses Must Never Ignore
Fever increase ≥ 1°C
Back or flank pain
Sudden dyspnea
Hypotension
Rash
Anxiety or sense of “impending doom”
First 15 minutes are highest risk.
8. Clinical Priority Framework
If reaction suspected:
STOP → SUPPORT → REPORT → DOCUMENT
Do not delay action waiting for confirmation.
9. Key Clinical Pearls
• Acute hemolytic reaction = most dangerous
• Fever during transfusion is never “normal”
• Dyspnea could be TRALI or TACO
• Hypotension + fever = emergency
• Always treat the patient first, then confirm diagnosis
