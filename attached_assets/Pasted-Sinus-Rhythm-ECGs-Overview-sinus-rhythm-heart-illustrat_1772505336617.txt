Sinus Rhythm ECGs
Overview
sinus rhythm heart illustration
Get the inside beat on sinus rhythm! This page offers an introduction to this ECG pattern, as well as providing lessons and sinus rhythm strips for practice.

What is a normal sinus rhythm in ECG?
A normal sinus rhythm, seen on an electrocardiogram (ECG) tracing, has a regular heart rate and shape. The cardiac electrical impulse originates in the sinoatrial node (SA), with P waves appearing upright before each QRS complex and having uniform shape. The intervals between the P waves are regular, although some variations can occur with respiration. Sinus rhythms are classified as:

Normal Sinus Rhythm ECG
Sinus Bradycardia
Sinus Tachycardia
Sinus Arrhythmia
Sinoatrial Block
Sinus Pause
Sinus Rhythm Classifications
Normal Sinus Rhythm
 sinus rhythm ECG tracing
The electrical impulse originates within the SA node and travels through the atria to the AV node. After a brief delay, the impulse travels down the bundle branches via the Purkinje fibers to the ventricles.

Sinus Pause
sinus rhythm ECG tracing
The heart's normal rhythm is driven by signals from the sinoatrial (SA) node, but when sinus arrest occurs this regularity can be thrown off. This happens either with a failure to generate an impulse from the SA node or if there is blockage of transmission. Depending on which type of pause in R-R intervals - known as R-R regularity continues with the beats that follow the missed beat. Depressed ST segments may also be observed. Sinus pause includes sinus arrest and sinus exit block.

Sinus Arrhythmia
 Sinus Arrhythmia ECG tracing
Sinus arrhythmia ECG looks normal with slight irregularities. A common cause of sinus arrhythmia is rhythm variations caused by respiration.

Sinus Bradycardia ECG
 Sinus Bradycardia ECG tracing
Sinus bradycardia is a sinus rhythm with a rate of 40-60 bpm.

Sinus Tachycardia
 Sinus Tachycardia ECG tracing
Sinus tachycardia is a normal sinus rhythm with a heart rate over 100 bpm. This occurs with exercise, excitement and with some illnesses.

Sinus Rhythm – Comprehensive ECG Lesson (NurseNest)
Learning Objectives
By the end of this lesson, learners will be able to:
Define sinus rhythm using strict electrocardiographic criteria
Describe the electrophysiologic basis of sinus rhythm
Identify normal sinus rhythm on a rhythm strip and 12-lead ECG
Differentiate sinus rhythm from atrial or junctional rhythms
Correlate sinus rhythm with autonomic regulation
Recognize normal physiologic variation within sinus rhythm
Apply a systematic interpretation framework to confirm sinus origin
1. Electrophysiologic Basis of Sinus Rhythm






4
The Sinoatrial (SA) Node
Sinus rhythm originates from the sinoatrial (SA) node, located in the high right atrium near the superior vena cava. The SA node is the heart’s primary pacemaker due to its highest intrinsic automaticity.
Intrinsic Firing Rate
SA node: 60–100 bpm
AV node: 40–60 bpm
Ventricular pacemakers: 20–40 bpm
Because the SA node fires fastest under normal conditions, it suppresses slower pacemakers via overdrive suppression.
Sequence of Depolarization
SA node generates impulse
Atrial depolarization → produces the P wave
Impulse reaches AV node (physiologic delay)
Conducts through Bundle of His
Travels down right and left bundle branches
Purkinje fibers depolarize ventricles → QRS complex
2. Definition of Sinus Rhythm
A rhythm is sinus in origin if all of the following criteria are met:
Criterion	Requirement
P wave morphology	Upright in lead II, uniform shape
P before QRS	Yes, every QRS preceded by a P
P:QRS ratio	1:1
PR interval	Constant (0.12–0.20 sec)
Rhythm	Regular
Pacemaker origin	SA node
Sinus rhythm refers to origin, not rate. A rhythm can be sinus and still be slow or fast.
3. ECG Characteristics of Normal Sinus Rhythm (NSR)






4
Diagnostic Criteria for NSR
Feature	Normal Range
Rate	60–100 bpm
Rhythm	Regular
PR interval	0.12–0.20 sec
QRS duration	< 0.12 sec
QT interval	Appropriate for rate
Morphology Details
P Wave
Rounded, upright in lead II
Consistent shape throughout strip
Duration < 0.12 sec
Amplitude < 2.5 mm
PR Interval
Represents atrial depolarization + AV nodal delay
Must remain constant if rhythm is sinus and stable.
QRS Complex
Narrow (< 120 ms)
Indicates normal ventricular conduction
4. Physiologic Regulation of Sinus Rhythm
Sinus rhythm is modulated by the autonomic nervous system.
Sympathetic Stimulation
Increases SA node firing rate
Increases conduction velocity
Shortens PR interval
Increases heart rate
Triggered by:
Exercise
Anxiety
Hypotension
Pain
Parasympathetic (Vagal) Stimulation
Decreases SA node firing
Slows AV conduction
Prolongs PR interval
Triggered by:
Sleep
Carotid massage
Valsalva maneuver
5. Rate Variability Within Sinus Rhythm
Sinus rhythm does not always mean exactly 75 bpm.
Normal physiologic variation includes:

• Mild respiratory variation (respiratory sinus arrhythmia)
• Rate increase with inspiration
• Rate decrease with expiration
• Transient rate changes with movement

These changes reflect intact autonomic balance and are often normal.

6. Distinguishing Sinus Rhythm from Other Rhythms
A rhythm may appear regular, but not be sinus in origin.
Key Differentiators
Atrial Ectopic Rhythm
Abnormal P wave morphology
May be inverted or peaked
Origin outside SA node
Junctional Rhythm
P waves absent, inverted, or after QRS
Rate typically 40–60 bpm
Atrial Fibrillation
No discrete P waves
Irregularly irregular rhythm
Atrial Flutter
Sawtooth flutter waves
Multiple atrial depolarizations per QRS
The presence of consistent upright P waves in lead II is the strongest indicator of sinus origin.
7. Sinus Rhythm in 12-Lead ECG
In a 12-lead ECG:
Sinus rhythm is confirmed if:

P waves upright in lead II
P waves upright in aVF
P waves negative in aVR
Uniform P morphology across beats
These findings confirm right atrial SA node origin.
8. Clinical Significance of Sinus Rhythm
Sinus rhythm indicates:
• Intact SA node automaticity
• Functional AV conduction
• Organized atrial depolarization
• Coordinated ventricular contraction

Hemodynamically, sinus rhythm supports:

Effective atrial kick
Optimal ventricular filling
Adequate cardiac output
Loss of sinus rhythm may reduce stroke volume by up to 20–30%, especially in older adults.
9. Systematic Interpretation Framework
When evaluating any rhythm:
Calculate rate
Assess rhythm regularity
Identify P waves
Confirm P before every QRS
Measure PR interval
Measure QRS width
Confirm sinus origin
If all criteria are satisfied → sinus rhythm confirmed.
10. Common Exam Pitfalls
• Assuming all regular rhythms are sinus
• Ignoring P wave morphology
• Missing inverted P waves
• Failing to check PR interval consistency
• Confusing sinus rhythm with junctional rhythm
Always analyze P waves first.

11. Clinical Case Example
A 65-year-old patient presents for routine ECG.
Findings:

Rate: 78 bpm
Rhythm: Regular
Upright P waves in lead II
PR interval: 0.16 sec
QRS: 0.08 sec
Interpretation: Normal sinus rhythm.
No intervention required.

12. Advanced Concepts
Sinus Rhythm and Cardiac Output
Cardiac output = Heart rate × Stroke volume
In sinus rhythm:

Atrial contraction contributes to ventricular filling
Known as “atrial kick”
Especially important in diastolic dysfunction
Loss of sinus rhythm (e.g., AFib) reduces preload and may worsen heart failure.
Sinus Node Dysfunction
Even if sinus rhythm is present intermittently, dysfunction may include:
Sinus pauses
Chronotropic incompetence
Bradycardia-tachycardia syndrome
Persistent abnormal sinus activity requires further evaluation.
13. Key Clinical Pearls
• Sinus rhythm refers to origin, not rate
• Upright P wave in lead II confirms sinus origin
• Every QRS must have a preceding P wave
• PR interval must be constant
• Sinus rhythm supports optimal cardiac output
• Always assess symptoms, not just ECG
14. Summary
Sinus rhythm represents the heart’s normal pacemaking mechanism, originating from the SA node and conducted through an intact atrioventricular system. It is defined by consistent P waves, 1:1 conduction, regular rhythm, and stable intervals.
Recognition of sinus rhythm is foundational to ECG interpretation. Every other arrhythmia is compared against this baseline pattern.

Mastering sinus rhythm identification ensures accurate detection of deviations and appropriate clinical decision-making.

Lesson #1: Rhythm Analysis Method

Intro
The five steps of rhythm analysis will be followed when analyzing any rhythm strip. Analyze each step in the following order.

Rhythm Regularity
Heart Rate
P wave morphology
P R interval or PRi
QRS complex duration and morphology
Step 1
Rhythm Regularity
ekg rhythm regularity
Carefully measure from the tip of one R wave to the next, from the beginning to the end of the tracing.
A rhythm is considered “regular or constant” when the distance apart is either the same or varies by 1 ½ small boxes or less from one R wave to the next R wave.
Step 2
Heart Rate Regular (Constant) Rhythms
regular ekg rhythm
The heart rate determination technique used will be the 1500 technique.
Starting at the beginning of the tracing through the end, measure from one R wave to the next R wave (ventricular assessment), then P wave to P wave (atrial assessment), then count the number of small boxes between each and divide that number into 1500. This technique will give you the most accurate heart rate when analyzing regular heart rhythms. You may include ½ of a small box i.e. 1500/37.5 = 40 bpm (don’t forget to round up or down if a portion of a beat is included in the answer).
Step 2 (Cont.)
Heart Rate - Irregular Rhythms
irregular ekg rhythm
If the rhythm varies by two small boxes or more, the rhythm is considered “irregular”.
The heart rate determination technique used for irregular rhythms will be the “six-second technique”.
Simply count the number of cardiac complexes in six seconds and multiply by ten.
Step 3
P wave Morphology (shape)
ekg p wave tracing
The lead most commonly referenced in cardiac monitoring is lead II.
In this training module, lead two will specifically be referenced unless otherwise specified.
The P wave in lead II in a normal heart is typically rounded and upright in appearance.
Changes in shape must be reported. This can be an indicator that the locus of stimulation is changing or the pathway taken is changing.
P waves may come in a variety of morphologies i.e. rounded and upright, peaked, flattened, notched, biphasic(pictured), inverted and even buried or absent!
Remember to describe the shape. This can be very important to the physician when diagnosing the patient.
Step 4
PR interval (PRi)
PR interval regular
Constant PR Interval

PR interval irregular
Variable PR Interval
Measurement of the PR interval reflects the amount of time from the beginning of atrial depolarization to the beginning of ventricular depolarization.
Plainly stated, this measurement is from the beginning of the P wave to the beginning of the QRS complex.
The normal range for PR interval is: 0.12 – 0.20 seconds (3 to 5 small boxes)
It is important that you measure each PR interval on the rhythm strip.
Some tracings do not have the same PRi measurement from one cardiac complex to the next. Sometimes there is a prolonging pattern, sometimes not.
If the PR intervals are variable, report them as variable, but note if a pattern is present or not.
Step 5
QRS complex
QRS Complex
QRS represents ventricular depolarization.
It is very important to analyze each QRS complex on the tracing and report the duration measurement and describe the shape (including any changes in shape).
As discussed in step 3, when referring to P waves, remember changes in the shape of the waveform can indicate the locus of stimulation has changed or a different conduction pathway was followed. It is no different when analyzing the QRS complex. The difference is that in step 3, we were looking at atrial activity. Now we are looking at ventricular activity.
Measure from the beginning to the end of ventricular depolarization.
The normal duration of the QRS complex is: 0.06 – 0.10 second

Introduction
The previous slides presented the five-steps of rhythm analysis. These five steps must be followed regardless of how simple of complex the tracing is you are reviewing.
The information gathered in these steps are telling a story.
The title of that story is the interpretation.
Sinus Rhythm Types
The dysrhythmias in this category occur as a result of influences on the Sinoatrial (SA) node. Rhythms in this category will share similarities in a normal appearing P wave, the PR interval will measure in the “normal range” of 0.12 – 0.20 second, and the QRS typically will measure in the “normal range” of 0.06 – 0.10 second. For the most part, dysrhythmias in this category either effect the rate, rhythm regularity or both within a particular tracing. We will be discussing the following complexes and rhythms:

Lesson #3: Normal Sinus Rhythm

Introduction
normal sinus rhythm ekg
Also known as Sinus Rhythm is the only rhythm when each of the five steps of rhythm analysis are “normal”.
All other rhythms you will analyze will have at least one of the 5-steps presenting an abnormality.
This rhythm will be regular, in a heart rate range between 60 – 100 bpm, P waves are upright and uniform in appearance (in Lead II), with a P wave for each QRS complex, the PR interval will measure in the normal range of 0.12 – 0.20 second (and measure the same each time), and the QRS complex morphology will be similar beat-to-beat and measure between 0.06 – 0.10 second (and measure the same each time).

Lesson #4: Sinus Bradycardia

Description
sinus bradycardia tracing
Rhythms are often named according to the origin of the electrical activity in the heart or the structure where the problem is occurring.
Sinus Rhythms are aptly named due to the locus of stimulation being the SA (sinoatrial) node.
With Sinus Bradycardia the locus of stimulation is the same as normal sinus rhythm, just now the rate will be less than 60 bpm. Recall that “brady” means slow.
The only difference between sinus bradycardia and normal sinus rhythm is the rate. All other steps in the rhythm analysis are “normal”.

Lesson #5: Sinus Tachycardia

Description
sinus tachycardia ekg tracing
Sinus Tachycardia occurs when the rate of electrical impulse formation occurs at a rate exceeding 100 bpm.
This can occur for a number of different reasons i.e. diet, stress, illness, response to physical exertion etc.
The only difference between Normal Sinus Rhythm and Sinus Tachycardia (Sinus Tach) is the rate exceeds 100 bpm. All other steps of rhythm analysis will be “normal.
An additional challenge that will present as rhythm rates accelerate is that the cardiac complexes will come closer together. This can result in the P wave becoming partially or completely buried within the T wave of the previous cardiac complex.
The result of a partially buried P waves means you are unable to establish the beginning of atrial depolarization. Meaning you will be unable to measure and report the PR interval. The only way it will be possible is if the physician instructs you to increase the machine printing speed (remember interval times will double at 50 mm/sec).

Lesson #6: Sinus Dysrhythmia (Arrhythmia)

Description
sinus arrhythmia tracing
Sinus Dysrhythmia often occurs as a normal variant. It is commonly seen in young healthy people and athletes. It is frequently related to breathing and pressure on the vagus nerve. As the patient inhales and the lungs expand, pressure is applied to the vagus nerve which causes a parasympathetic response and a decrease in heart rate.
Sinus Dysrhythmia ECGs may also occur as a result of medication effects or diseases such as multiple sclerosis.
This dysrhythmia often requires no treatment, but may require medication or therapy such as a pacemaker to regulate the heart rate if the ventricular response becomes too slow.
Sinus Dysrhythmia closely resembles Normal Sinus Rhythm with the only distinction being the intervals from one cardiac complex to the next are changing as influenced by the patients respiratory pattern.
Note the changing R to R Intervals.

Lesson #7: Sinus Arrest

Description
Sinus Arrest occurs when there is a sudden absence of electrical activity initiated by the SA node. This results in a pause in the electrical activity seen on the tracing. Remember, no electrical activity = no depolarization and contraction. Hence, a drop in blood pressure.
The longer the pause, the further the blood pressure will drop.
A pause of six-seconds is considered a medical emergency and emergency procedures must be initiated.
The ECG sinus arrest rhythm typically will demonstrate constant R to R intervals prior to and following the pause. This pause results in the rhythm tracing presenting as irregular.
Analysis
sinus arrest ekg tracing
It is important to measure the duration of the pause and report this information along with the frequency of the pauses if there are more than one.
The pause is measured by placing your caliper tips on the last R wave just prior to the pause and the R wave immediately following the pause. Count the number of small boxes and multiply by 0.04 second. This will be reported as part of the interpretation.

Lesson #8: Sinus Exit Block

Description
sinus exit block ekg tracing
Sinus Exit Block looks very much the same as Sinus Arrest with one important distinction.
The duration of the pause with Sinus Exit Block is in a direct multiple of the R to R interval of the underlying rhythm. Sinus Arrest does not have this specific feature.

Lesson 1: The Five-Step Rhythm Analysis Method
Accurate ECG interpretation requires a structured approach. The five steps must be applied to every rhythm strip in the same order.
Step 1: Rhythm Regularity






4
Measure from the peak of one R wave to the peak of the next across the entire strip.
A rhythm is considered regular if the R–R intervals are equal or vary by ≤ 1.5 small boxes.
If the variation exceeds 2 small boxes → rhythm is irregular.

This step determines ventricular regularity.

Step 2: Heart Rate Determination
For Regular Rhythms – The 1500 Method
Count the number of small boxes between two consecutive R waves.
Heart Rate = 1500 ÷ number of small boxes

Example:
1500 ÷ 25 = 60 bpm

This method is the most precise for regular rhythms.

For Irregular Rhythms – The Six-Second Method
Count the number of QRS complexes in 6 seconds and multiply by 10.
Example:
8 beats in 6 seconds → 80 bpm

Step 3: P Wave Morphology






4
Lead II is the standard reference lead in rhythm monitoring.
Normal sinus P waves are:

Upright
Rounded
Uniform in shape
Precede each QRS
Abnormal findings may include:
Inverted P waves
Peaked P waves
Flattened or notched waves
Absent or buried P waves
Changes in morphology may indicate altered atrial conduction or pacemaker shift.
Step 4: PR Interval (PRi)
Measure from the beginning of the P wave to the beginning of the QRS complex.
Normal PR interval:
0.12 – 0.20 seconds (3–5 small boxes)

It must:

Be measured on every beat
Remain consistent
A variable PR interval must be reported and described.
Step 5: QRS Complex






4
QRS represents ventricular depolarization.
Normal duration:
0.06 – 0.10 seconds

Assess:

Width
Morphology
Consistency
Changes may indicate conduction delays or ventricular origin.
Lesson 2: Sinus Rhythm Overview
Sinus rhythms originate from the sinoatrial (SA) node, located in the right atrium.






4
Sinus rhythms share these features:
Normal appearing P waves
PR interval within normal limits
Narrow QRS complex
Origin at SA node
Differences between sinus rhythms involve:
Rate
Regularity
Presence or absence of pauses

Indicates intact SA node function and normal conduction pathway.

4

Normal P waves

Definition: Sudden absence of SA node impulse generation.






Features:
Missing P-QRS-T complex

Rhythm becomes irregular

Small boxes × 0.04 seconds

4

Pause equals exact multiple of underlying R–R interval.

4

In sinus bradycardia, the SA node rate slows but still overrides lower pacemakers.

sinus tachycardia ECG tracing

Narrow

Heart Block Rhythms ECG Course: Bundle Branch Blocks
Overview
This page offers an introduction to Heart Block rhythms, as well as providing lessons and ECG practice skills for further exploration.

When the cardiac electric impulse is delayed or blocked, heart blocks in the AV node, bundle of His or the Purkinje system can be the cause. Heart block rhythms categories include:

First Degree Heart Block
Second Degree Heart Block Type I
Second Degree Heart Block Type II
Third Degree Heart Block
Bundle Branch Block
Heart Block Categories
First Degree Heart Block
 First Degree Heart Block ECG tracing
First degree heart block is caused by a conduction delay at the AV node or bundle of His. This means than the PR Interval will be longer than normal (over 0.20 sec.). So its actually a delay not a complete block.

Second Degree Heart Block Type I
 Second Degree Heart Block Type I ECG tracing
Second degree heart block, Type I, causes some impulses to be blocked. This can been seen on the ECG training as more P waves than QRS Complexes. Usually, each successive impulse undergoes a longer delay. Then after 3 or 4 beats the an impulse is blocked. After this blocked beat, the cycle of lengthening PR Intervals resumes. This heart block is also called a Wenckebach block.

Second Degree Heart Block Type II
 Second Degree Heart Block Type II ECG tracing
With Mobitz Type II blocks, the impulse is blocked in the bundle of His. Every few beats there will be a missing beat, but the PR Interval will not lengthen.

Third Degree Heart Block
 Third Degree Heart Block ECG tracing
In third degree heart blocks, atrial impulses are not transmitted to the ventricles. The ventricles may then create an escape impulse, independent of the atrial beat. In such cases the atria will usually beat at 60-100 bpm while the ventricles asynchronously beat at 30-45 bpm.

Bundle Branch Block
 Bundle Branch Block ECG tracing
With this block, either the left or right bundle branch is blocked intermittently or fixed. The QRS complex is wider than normal (> 0.12 sec.). Using a 12 lead ECG, conduction blocks in either the left or right bundle branch may be diagnosed.

4

SA node initiates impulse
Atrial depolarization (P wave)
AV node delay (PR interval)
Bundle of His
Right and Left Bundle Branches

Heart blocks are divided into:

Second Degree AV Block Type I (Mobitz I / Wenckebach)
Second Degree AV Block Type II (Mobitz II)
Third Degree (Complete) AV Block
Bundle Branch Block (BBB)

3. First Degree AV Block






4
Definition
A conduction delay at the AV node or bundle of His.
ECG Criteria
PR interval > 0.20 seconds
PR interval constant
Every P wave followed by QRS
Rhythm regular

It is a delay, not a dropped beat.

Often benign.

Athletes

Progressive AV nodal delay until one impulse fails to conduct.

Progressive PR interval lengthening
Eventually a dropped QRS complex
Cycle repeats
More P waves than QRS complexes
Mechanism
Usually occurs at the AV node.

Often benign and transient.

4

PR interval constant
Sudden dropped QRS complex
More P waves than QRS complexes
Mechanism
Usually occurs in the bundle of His or below.
Clinical Significance
Serious.
High risk of progressing to third-degree block.
Often requires pacemaker placement.

4

No atrial impulses are conducted to the ventricles.

P waves and QRS complexes unrelated
Atrial rate 60–100 bpm
Ventricular escape rhythm 20–45 bpm
PR interval varies randomly

Complete AV dissociation.

Medical emergency.
Often causes:
Severe bradycardia
Hypotension
Syncope (Stokes-Adams attacks)

Wide, abnormal QRS morphology

Right Bundle Branch Block (RBBB)
Often benign
RSR’ pattern in V1

Often indicates structural heart disease
Broad notched R waves in lateral leads

RBBB may be incidental.

1. Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)
Definition
Ventricular tachycardia is a rapid rhythm originating from the ventricles, typically ≥ 100 bpm (commonly 120–250 bpm), characterized by wide QRS complexes.
It is a potentially life-threatening rhythm.

ECG Characteristics






4
Feature	VT Finding
Rate	120–250 bpm
Rhythm	Usually regular
P waves	Absent or dissociated
PR interval	Not measurable
QRS duration	> 0.12 sec (wide)
Morphology	Monomorphic or polymorphic
Types
Monomorphic VT
All QRS complexes appear similar.
Polymorphic VT
QRS complexes vary in shape and amplitude.
Includes torsades de pointes.
Pathophysiology
Originates from:
Ventricular myocardium
Re-entry circuits
Scar tissue (post-MI)
Cardiomyopathy
Because depolarization does not follow normal conduction pathways, QRS complexes are wide and abnormal.
Clinical Presentation
Stable VT:
Palpitations
Mild hypotension
Unstable VT:
Chest pain
Hypotension
Altered LOC
Shock
Pulseless VT:
Cardiac arrest
Management Overview (ACLS Principles)
Stable VT:
Antiarrhythmics (e.g., amiodarone)
Unstable VT:
Synchronized cardioversion
Pulseless VT:
Immediate defibrillation
CPR
Epinephrine
VT is treated aggressively because it may deteriorate into ventricular fibrillation.
2. Atrial Fibrillation (AFib)
Definition
Atrial fibrillation is a chaotic atrial rhythm with disorganized electrical activity and an irregular ventricular response.
Most common sustained arrhythmia in adults.

ECG Characteristics






4
Feature	AFib Finding
Rhythm	Irregularly irregular
Rate	Variable
P waves	Absent
Baseline	Fibrillatory waves
PR interval	Not measurable
QRS	Usually narrow
Pathophysiology
Multiple atrial foci fire rapidly (350–600 bpm).
AV node blocks most impulses, allowing only some to conduct to ventricles.

Loss of coordinated atrial contraction leads to:

Reduced atrial kick
Blood stasis in atria
Risk of thrombus formation
Clinical Significance
Increased risk of:
Stroke
Heart failure
Rapid ventricular response (RVR)
Management Overview
Stable AFib:
Rate control (beta blockers, calcium channel blockers)
Anticoagulation
Rhythm control (selected patients)
Unstable AFib:
Synchronized cardioversion
Stroke prevention is a major priority.
3. Atrial Tachycardia (AT)
Definition
Atrial tachycardia is a rapid atrial rhythm originating from an ectopic focus outside the SA node, typically 150–250 bpm.

5. Clinical Red Flags
Immediate concern if:
Wide complex tachycardia (assume VT until proven otherwise)
Hypotension
Chest pain
Syncope
Altered LOC
When in doubt: treat wide complex tachycardia as VT.
6. Key Clinical Pearls
• Wide + fast + regular → think VT
• Irregularly irregular → think AFib
• Regular narrow tachycardia with abnormal P waves → think atrial tachycardia
• AFib increases stroke risk
• VT can deteriorate into ventricular fibrillation
• Always assess patient stability first
Final Takeaway
Ventricular tachycardia originates from the ventricles and is potentially fatal.
Atrial fibrillation is chaotic atrial activity with irregular ventricular response and stroke risk.
Atrial tachycardia originates from an ectopic atrial focus and produces regular narrow tachycardia with abnormal P waves.
Accurate identification depends on rhythm regularity, QRS width, and P wave analysis.
