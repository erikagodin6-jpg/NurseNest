1. Conduction System Review






4
Normal Conduction Pathway
SA node initiates impulse
Atrial depolarization (P wave)
AV node delay (PR interval)
Bundle of His
Right and Left Bundle Branches
Purkinje fibers → ventricular depolarization (QRS complex)
Heart blocks occur when conduction is delayed or interrupted anywhere along this pathway.
2. Heart Block Categories
Heart blocks are divided into:
First Degree AV Block
Second Degree AV Block Type I (Mobitz I / Wenckebach)
Second Degree AV Block Type II (Mobitz II)
Third Degree (Complete) AV Block
Bundle Branch Block (BBB)
AV blocks involve delayed or failed conduction between atria and ventricles.
Bundle branch blocks involve intraventricular conduction delay.
3. First Degree AV Block






4
Definition
A conduction delay at the AV node or bundle of His.
ECG Criteria
PR interval > 0.20 seconds
PR interval constant
Every P wave followed by QRS
Rhythm regular
Key Concept
It is a delay, not a dropped beat.
Clinical Significance
Often benign.
May be seen in:
Athletes
Increased vagal tone
Beta blocker use
Typically requires monitoring only.
4. Second Degree AV Block Type I (Mobitz I / Wenckebach)






4
Definition
Progressive AV nodal delay until one impulse fails to conduct.
ECG Criteria
Progressive PR interval lengthening
Eventually a dropped QRS complex
Cycle repeats
More P waves than QRS complexes
Mechanism
Usually occurs at the AV node.
Clinical Significance
Often benign and transient.
May occur during sleep or inferior MI.
Monitor unless symptomatic.
5. Second Degree AV Block Type II (Mobitz II)






4
Definition
Intermittent failure of conduction without PR interval prolongation.
ECG Criteria
PR interval constant
Sudden dropped QRS complex
More P waves than QRS complexes
Mechanism
Usually occurs in the bundle of His or below.
Clinical Significance
Serious.
High risk of progressing to third-degree block.
Often requires pacemaker placement.
6. Third Degree (Complete) AV Block






4
Definition
No atrial impulses are conducted to the ventricles.
ECG Criteria
P waves and QRS complexes unrelated
Atrial rate 60–100 bpm
Ventricular escape rhythm 20–45 bpm
PR interval varies randomly
Mechanism
Complete AV dissociation.
Clinical Significance
Medical emergency.
Often causes:
Severe bradycardia
Hypotension
Syncope (Stokes-Adams attacks)
Requires immediate pacing.
7. Bundle Branch Block (BBB)






4
Definition
Delay or block in right or left bundle branch conduction.
ECG Criteria
QRS duration > 0.12 seconds
Wide, abnormal QRS morphology
Sinus rhythm usually present
Types
Right Bundle Branch Block (RBBB)
Often benign
RSR’ pattern in V1
Left Bundle Branch Block (LBBB)
Often indicates structural heart disease
Broad notched R waves in lateral leads
Clinical Significance
RBBB may be incidental.
LBBB may obscure MI diagnosis and often indicates underlying pathology.
8. Comparison Summary Table
Block Type	PR Interval	QRS	Dropped Beats	Severity
1st Degree	>0.20 sec (constant)	Normal	No	Mild

2nd Degree II	Constant	Normal	Yes	Serious

9. Clinical Red Flags
Immediate concern if:
Mobitz II present
Third-degree block identified
Ventricular rate < 40 bpm
Hypotension
Syncope
Chest pain
Prepare for pacing if unstable.
10. Management Overview
First Degree:
Monitor
Review medications
Mobitz I:
Monitor
Treat underlying cause
Mobitz II:
Cardiology consult
Likely pacemaker
Third Degree:
Emergency
Transcutaneous pacing
IV access
Atropine (often ineffective)
Transvenous pacing
Bundle Branch Block:
Evaluate underlying cardiac disease
Assess for ischemia
11. Key Clinical Pearls
• PR interval reflects AV nodal conduction
• Progressive PR lengthening → Mobitz I
• Dropped beat without PR change → Mobitz II
• AV dissociation → Third-degree block
• Wide QRS with sinus rhythm → Bundle branch block
• Mobitz II and third-degree blocks require urgent intervention
12. Final Takeaway
Heart block rhythms result from delayed or interrupted conduction through the AV node or ventricular conduction system. Accurate identification requires systematic rhythm analysis and understanding of conduction anatomy.
Mobitz II and third-degree blocks represent unstable conduction disturbances and require urgent intervention. Bundle branch blocks reflect ventricular conduction delay and must be interpreted in the clinical context.
