

Head Lice
-	Highly contagious via direct or indirect contact
-	Features:
o	Pruritis 
o	Excoriation on the scalp
o	Trouble sleeping
o	Light coloured specks on the hair
o	Nits may be seen in the hair and on the nape of the neck
-	Tx:
o	Apply medicated shampoo
o	Leave on for 10 minutes and wash hair in the sink and use warm instead of hot water
o	Use a comb to remove the nits
o	Repeat shampoo 7-10 days after treatment to kill newly hatched lice 
o	Soak all brushes, combs, and hair accessories in hot water for ten minutes 
o	Screen all household members for lice
o	Vacuum carpets, mattresses, and upholstered furniture
o	Wash bedding in hot water and dry on the hottest setting 
o	Throw all nonwashable items in a plastic bag for at least two weeks to kill the nits 
o	Do not need to miss school 

Impetigo
-	Highly contagious bacterial skin infection in children
-	Spread directly or indirectly 
-	Enter cuts in skin and cause infection
-	Features:
o	Nonbullous form 
	Papules  pustules  rapidly break into golden coloured thick adherent crusts
o	Bullous impetigo
	Fluid filled blisters or bullae rupture and have a brown thin crust 
-	Nursing:
o	Contact precautions
o	Mupirocin (abx ointment) 
o	Cephalexin orally
o	Limit the spread of infection (hand hygiene, clean lesions appropriately)
o	Remove the crusting after soaking lesions, then pat dry
o	Wash hands before and after applying medication
o	Cover draining lesions 
o	Wash and dry linens separately on high heat
o	Keep fingernails short 
o	Avoid group activities for 24-48 hours after starting antibiotics to allow time for the antibacterial to work 
o	Monitor for complications
	Poststreptococcal glomerulonephritis
•	Hematuria, hypertension, edema 
	Rheumatic fever
•	Arthritis, carditis, chorea (unintentional jerking movements of the extremities)
	Subcutaneous nodules 
	Erythema marginatum

Hemolytic Uremic Syndrome
-	Patho: caused by e coli 
-	Life threatening condition 
-	Most commonly seen in children less than 5
-	After diarrheal illness results in triad of:
o	Hemolytic anemia
o	Thrombocytopenia
o	Acute kidney injury 
-	Features:
o	Hemolytic anemia: tired, irritable, pallor
o	Thrombocytopenia: decreased platelets = petechiae, purpura (larger bleeds), bruising
o	AKI: decreased urine output, edema, hypertension (cannot filter blood so fluid overload)
-	Nursing:
o	Decrease the spread by implementing contact precautions
o	Provide fluids to maintain fluid or electrolyte balance 
o	Monitor for worsening AKI  anuria = red flag, communicate to HCP
	Strict intake and output 
	Treat hypertension with antihypertensives
	Prepare for dialysis
o	Administer blood products for anemia and thrombocytopenia
o	Inform caregivers that most children resolve in 2-3 weeks 
Hydrocephalus
-	Buildup of CSF in brain
-	Overproduction or impaired drainage
-	Accumulation = dilated ventricles = compression = increased ICP
-	Can progress to brain damage or death if left untreated 
-	Features:
o	Infant:
	Bulging fontanel
	Prominent scalp veins
	Sunset eyes (late sign  cannot look upwards, has a continuous downward gaze)
	High pitched shrill cry
	Poor feeding or vomiting
	Developmental delays
	Increased head circumference 
	Wide spaced suture lines 
o	Older children/adults:
	Signs of increased ICP
	Headache, vomiting, drowsiness
	Vision problems
	Ataxia (poor muscle coordination)
	Cushing’s triad: hypertension, bradycardia, hypoventilation 
-	Dx:
o	Infant: ultrasound (fontanel)
o	Older children and adults: MRI or CT
-	Management:
o	Surgical intervention 
o	Preop:
	Seizure precaution
	Daily head circumference managements
	Intake and output 
	Reposition head frequently to decrease pressure injuries 
o	Surgery:
	Temporaru external ventricular drainage device
•	Drains excess CSF int a drainage bag
	Ventriculoperitoneal shunt
•	Inserted into ventricle and drains into peritoneum which reabsorbs fluid into the bloodstream 
o	After surgery:	
	Maintain flat or supine position for 24 hours then lay on nonoperative side to decrease pressure on VP shunt to prevent CSF overdrainage, ventricular collapse, and sudden drop in ICP.
	Monitor for shunt malfunction  increase in head circumference and ICP (same as before shunt) 
	Monitor for infection  fever
	Monitor for abdominal tenderness  can lead to peritonitis 
	Test surgical site drainage for glucose (halo sign)  immediately intervene, no glucose = normal (serosang)
o	Discharge: 
	Shunt will require revision surgeries 
	Child should be active (swimming) 
	Avoid contact sports (boxing)
	Enroll in early childhood development programs 
o	Monitor for shunt malfunction (teaching)
	Fever, poor feeding, irritability 
	Redness at surgical site 
	Abdominal tenderness 
	Teach them to report to HCP
Pinworms
-	Most common parasitic worm infection of intestines and rectum
-	Transmitted to hands and under fingernails during scratching and then spread to common objects which are swallowed or inhaled and hatch in the intestine 
-	Worms come out at night and lay eggs outside the rectum 
-	Causes extreme itching 
-	Common in daycare and classroom settings
-	Features:
o	Extreme perianal itching at night  excoriations
o	Disturbed sleep
o	Irritability
o	Restlessness
o	Nocturnal enuresis
-	Management:
o	Tape test – apply tape to rectum before they go to bed and take off in morning, put in glass jar and see if eggs hatch
o	Antihelmintic medications if the kid is over 2
	Pyrantel pamoate and albendazole
	All members of the household should be treated
o	Take initial dose and repeat in 2 weeks to complete eradication
-	Teaching:
o	Prevent reinfection
o	Short fingernails, avoid nail biting 
o	Wash hands thoroughly after using the toilet and before eating
o	Shower rather than bathe every morning 
o	One piece sleeping outfits to reduce scratching
o	Wash linens, clothes, toys, blankets in hot water
o	Eggs can live up to 2 weeks on surfaces 

Conjunctivitis
-	Inflammation 
-	If newborns have it it needs to be reported immediately, can lead to blindness
-	Children have three major causes:
o	Allergies, viral infection, bacterial infection
-	Features: 
o	Inflamed conjunctiva
o	Sandy sensation in eye
o	Swollen lid
o	Purulent drainage ie bacterial 
o	Serous drainage (viral)
o	Intense itching in both eyes (allergic)
-	Treatment
o	Treat the underlying cause 
	Bacterial  administer antibiotics 
	Viral  cold compress, saline eye drops
	Allergic  allergen avoidance and antihistamines 
o	Remove the accumulation of secretions 
	Wipe from the inner canthus downward and outward away from the other eye
	Separate linens, towels, washcloths 
	Refrain from rubbing the eye
	Apply mittens
	Good handwashing 
	Length of infection
•	Bacterial  up to 24 hours after initiation of antibiotics
•	Viral  until symptoms resolve (3-7 days)
Metabolic Acidosis vs Alkalosis 
-	Metabolic acidosis = kidneys are too acidic, too much acid or loss of base
-	Metabolic alkalosis = too much base or loss of acid
-	Metabolic acidosis
o	Increase in acid from: 
	Poor perfusion, carbon monoxide poisoning
	Keto acids in DKA
	Exogenous ingestion (aspirin, ethylene glycol)
	Impaired renal excretion (kidney disease) 
o	Losing base
	Diarrhea 
o	Features: 
	Signs of hyperkalemia (potassium and acid love each other)
	ECG monitoring
	Tall tented T waves
	Kussmaul respirations (fast deep breathing) to get rid of acid 
	Diarrhea
o	Nursing: 
	Monitor labs (lactic acid)
	Replace fluids and electrolytes
	Medications
•	Antidiarrheals 
•	Sodium bicarb 
-	Metabolic alkalosis
o	Large amounts of GI loss 
	Vomiting, NG tube suctioning 
	Losing hydrochloric acid 
	Chronic OTC antacids 
	Medication like diuretics, thiazides, excess hydrogen loss
o	Features:
	No real symptoms, just unwell
	Symptoms are due to underlying causes 
	Low resps to make acid 
o	Nursing:
	Replace fluids and electrolytes
	Avoid diuretics to hold onto hydrogen
	Antiemetics 
	Avoid suctioning to keep acid in the gut 

Administering Eye Drops
-	Place client in supine position with chin lifted
-	Place cotton ball or tissue below eyelid and press downward to expose conjunctival sac
-	Have client look up
-	Hold medication in dominant hand and rest side of hand on forehead 
-	Administer prescribed drops 
-	If ointment, apply from inner lower eyelid to outer and then close eyes and rub eyelid with a cotton ball
-	Nursing: 
o	Observe eyes prior to administering medications 
o	If administering ointment and drops do drops first, wait five mins, then ointment


Blood Transfusions
-	Replace lost blood or cellular components 
-	Increase RBCs or hemoglobin 
-	Types: 
o	PRBC – symptomatic anemia, raise hgb and hematocrit
o	FFP: DIC 
o	Frozen RBC: autologous transfusions
o	Platelets: thrombocytopenia 
o	Albumin: expands blood volume – shelf stable, lasts for 5 years, does not need to follow blood tubing 
o	Clotting factors: cryoprecipitate if client is lacking clotting factors
-	Nursing:
o	Verify ABO and Rh blood group compatibility 
o	Large bore IV
o	Filtered Y tubing 
o	Use an infusion pump 
o	Start at a slower rate in case person has a reaction 
o	Normal saline is the only fluid compatible 
o	Medications are never added to blood products 
o	Tubing set should be changed for each unit of blood 
o	Check the blood bag for the date of expiration 
o	Inspect bag for leaks, color, clots, and bubbles
o	If compromised, do not give, return to blood bank 
o	Administer within 20-30 mins after receiving from bank 
o	Return unused blood promptly 
o	Do not put in the refrigerator unless it’s the specific one in the blood bank
o	Verify consent and 2 client identifiers with nurse 
o	Insert large gauge iv catheter
o	Administer over 2-4 hours 
o	Baseline vitals and 15 mins after initiation 
-	Complications
o	Circulatory overload
o	Transfusion reactions
o	Sepsis
o	Infection transmission (HIV, hepatitis B/C)
o	Hypocalcemia due to citrate in the blood which also releases potassium 
o	Hyperkalemia  
-	Transfusion reactions
o	Assessment
	Chills, fever, itching, rash
	SOB, chest tightness
•	Intervention: immediately stop transfusion, do not flush the blood in the tubing 
•	Disconnect tubing and use new tubing and infuse NS and stay with patient, monitoring VS every 5 mins
•	Administer emergency medications as prescribed 
•	Collect urine and blood specimens 
•	Do not throw away the bag or tubing and return to blood bank for further testing 
•	Document appropriately and accordingly 
•	Notify the HCP and charge nurse
Circulatory Overload
-	Often caused by rapid infusion of blood products
o	Assessment:
	Cough, dyspnea
	Orthopnea, SOB, crackles, distended neck veins and bounding pulses
	High BP, high heart rate
	Do not stop infusion, slow the rate 
	Place in high fowler, keep feet in a dependent position 
	May need oxygen and diuretic, furosemide




Dehydration
-	Causes: 
o	Fluid loss from GI tract (vomiting, diarrhea)
o	Decreased oral intake 
o	Sensible losses (skin or respiratory tract with hyperventilation)
o	Burns/trauma
o	Can rapidly progress to hypovolemic shock 
o	Infants and younger children have a higher percentage of body water  dehydration occurs more quickly
-	Types
o	Serum sodium level determines the type of dehydration
	Isotonic: losing equal amount of water and electrolytes 
•	Greatest threat to life with shock
•	Primary form of dehydration in children
	Hypotonic:
•	Losing more electrolytes than fluids (marathon runner)
•	Sodium osmolality will be low 
	Hypertonic:
•	Water loss exceeds electrolyte loss 
•	Diuretic use/misuse
•	Patient will have hypernatremia
-	Features:
o	Based on degree of dehydration 
o	Mild: 3-5% of weight loss – increased thirst, decreased intake, increased losses
	Minimal abnormalities on exam
o	Moderate (6-9%)
	Decreased skin turgor, dry mucous membranes 
	Sunken eyes/fontanelles
	Body compensates with tachycardia decreased urine output 
o	Severe: (>10% loss)
	Emergency
	Heading to hypovolemic shock
	Listless, lethargic, unresponsive 
	Weak pulse, hypotension, tachypnea
	Decreased cap refill >2 seconds
	Skin tenting oliguria/anuria
-	Nursing: 
o	Check cap refill, skin turgor, mucous membranes, fontanelles
o	Daily weights 
o	Rehydration therapy 
	Mild to moderate: oral rehydration solution 
	Severe: IV fluids 
o	Advance diet as tolerated strict intake and output
o	Monitoring serum electrolytes and replacing as lost
o	Monitor urine output 

Clean Catch Urine Specimen
-	Urinalysis and cns
-	Evaluate for UTI
-	Only 30-60ml is required 
-	Females:
o	Perform hand hygiene and open the container, do not touch the inside 
o	Spread the labia using the index finger and thumb of non dominant hand, cleanse vulva in front to back motion

o	Pass container into urine stream 
o	Remove before flow ends 
o	Replace cap and do not touch inner lid
o	Repeat hand hygiene
-	Males
o	Hand hygiene and open, do not touch inside
o	If uncircumcised, retract foreskin, cleanse urinary meatus
o	Intiate the urine stream 
o	Pass container into the stream and remove before flow ends
o	Replace sterile cap 
o	Repeat hand hygiene 
-	Toddlers/Infants 
o	Urine collection bag
o	Nonsterile 
o	Cleanse the perineum and the tip of the penis 
o	CNS – sterile intermittent catheterization 
-	Nursing:
o	After collection – label per policy 
o	Do not put the label on the lid 
o	Send specimen to laboratory immediately 
o	Document the collection of the specimen 
o	Report findings to HCP 

Hypercalcemia
-	Calcium helps regulate nerve and muscle function 
-	High levels of calcium in the blood (>10.5mg/dl or 2.62 mmol/L)
-	Calcium is absorbed within GI tract and stored in bones and is eliminated by the kidneys 
-	Any change in the process affects levels
o	Causes:
	Increased calcium absorption
	Excessive intake of calcium and vitamin D
	Decreased calcium excretion
	Thiazide diuretics
	Increased bone resorption of calcium 
•	Parathyroid is responsible for increasing serum calcium levels expulsion and it also affects kidneys and tells it to reabsorb
•	Calcitonin regulates calcium levels – opposite of parathyroid – helps to decrease levels of calcium 
	Hyperparathyroidism 
	Malignancy (bone cancer) 
	Hemoconcentration from dehydration 
-	Features:
o	Symptomatic when calcium is over 3 
o	Acts like a diuretic and sedative
o	Symptoms: 
	Polyuria  dehydration  polydipsia
o	Calcium helps regulate nerve and muscle function 
o	Decreased neuromuscular excitability – muscle weakness  paralysis  decreased reflexes
o	Decreased intestinal paralysis  constipation
o	Cardiac dysrhythmias
o	Severe and rapid hypercalcemia  lethargy, confusion, stupor, coma 
o	Monitor calcium and report over 2.62
o	Treat underlying cause 
o	Increase oral fluid intake 
o	Administer normal saline, bisphosphonates and calcitonin 
o	Discontinue calcium containing medications and thiazide diuretics 
o	Continuous cardiac monitoring 
o	Monitor LOC 

Hyperkalemia
-	High serum potassium over 5
-	Major intracellular ion and maintains intracellular fluid volume 
o	Causes:
	Creates action potential on the cell membrane 
	Large intake of potassium (oral or IV)
	Reduced renal excretion of potassium in AKI or CKD, spironolactone, ACE inhibitors 
	Metabolic acidosis (DKA)
	Insulin deficiency/hyperglycemia 
	Muscle injury (rhabdomyolysis)
	Tumor lysis syndromes
	Pseudohyperkalemia – during the process of storing blood the RBC may rupture and can lead to excessive levels of potassium inside of the sample so maintaining proper techniques is important 
-	Features:
o	Baseline ECG – tall peaked T waves and absence of p waves 
o	Cardiac conduction problems – heart block
o	Ascending muscle weakness  paralysis
o	Lethal cardiac dysrhythmias
	V- fib, asystole
	Widened qrs complex 
	St segment depression 
o	If untreated or severe – leads to widened qrs and then asystole 
o	Monitor serum electrolytes all throughout treatment 
o	Continuous cardiac monitoring 
o	Interventions to promote excretion 
o	Potassium binders (patiromir, sodium polystyrene sulfonate (kayexalate))
o	Dialysis for severe renal impairment
o	Medications to drive potassium from blood into cells 
	Insulin drives potassium into cells (regular insulin and iv dextrose)
	Be careful with this as it can cause hypoglycemia 
	Betaadrenergic agonists (albuterol)  not as effective 
o	ECG changes  medications to stabilize myocardial cell membranes such as IV calcium gluconate 
o	Encourage patient to avoid foods with high potassium
	Fast  fish, avocado, spinach and strawberries, tomato
	Bunny can carry many oranges  bananas cantaloupe, carrots, mushrooms, oranges
	Roast potatoes, pork, beef: raisins, potatoes pork, beef
Hypermagnesemia
-	Normal range is 1.5-2.5 mEq/L 
-	Second most abundant ICF electrolyte
-	Usual role: muscle contraction at neuromuscular junction using acetylcholine 
-	Inverse relationship with calcium 
-	Works with potassium (if potassium goes up, so does magnesium)
-	Excess intake – not dietary – magnesium containing antacids
-	IV mag sulfate for preeclampsia
-	Impaired excretion  kidney disease 
-	Findings:
o	Magnesium toxicity
	Mild: nausea, flushing, headache, diminished DTR
	Moderate: hypocalcemia, somnolence, absent DTR, hypotension, bradycardia 
	Severe: flaccid quadriplegia, respiratory failure, cardiac arrest 
-	Nursing:
o	Address underlying cause
o	Monitor serum magnesium
o	Avoiding more intake
	Magnesium rich-foods: spinach, broccoli, avocados, potatoes, whole grains
	Magnesium containing meds
o	Reverse, remove magnesium
	Isotonic fluids
	IV calcium gluconate
o	Dialysis
o	Cardiac monitor
	Heart blocks 

Hypernatremia
-	Major extracellular cation outside of body cells
-	Cell membrane function and action potential 
-	Controls fluid movement 
-	Sodium over 145 
-	Pulls water from brain into cerebral veins  veins enlarge and rupture 
-	Causes:
o	Too much salt or losing too much water
o	Excess sodium intake 
o	Hypertonic nacl, IV sodium bicarb
o	Near drowning in salt water
o	Decreased sodium excretion  hyperaldosteronism, kidney disease
o	Inadequate water intake 
	Unconscious or cognitively impaired 
	Prolonged fasting, NPO status
	Excess water loss  diarrhea, vomiting, sweating, hyperventilating
	Diabetes insipidus
-	Features:
o	Too much salt, not enough water
o	Neurologic: early: lethargy, weakness, irritability 
	Late: Twitching, seizures, coma 
	Signs of dehydration: dry mucous membranes, increased thirst, decreased urination, increased heart rate 
-	Nursing:
o	Monitor serum sodium levels and report over 145
o	Increase oral fluid intake and decrease dietary sodium intake
o	Isotonic or hypotonic iv fluids (0.45 NACL)
o	Monitor response to treatment
o	Decrease sodium level slowly  rapidly decrease in sodium levels  cerebral edema and permanent neurologic complications
o	Seizure precautions
o	Monitor intake and output

Hyperphosphatemia
-	Normal range 2.7-4.5 
-	Located in ICF
-	Metabolizes calcium (inverse relationship)
-	Muscle contraction 
-	RBC function  holds cell membranes together
-	Increased cellular release from tumor lysis syndrome 
-	Rhabdomyolysis
-	Decreased renal excretion
-	Increased renal reabsorption
-	Hypoparathyroidism (decreased pth)
-	Features:
o	Binds to calcium and lowers it so if its high, hypocalcemia
o	Numbness and tinging 
o	Muscle cramps, tetany (medical emergency)
o	Laryngospasm  stridor (emergency)
o	Chvosteks sign, trousseau sign 
-	Nursing:
o	Treat underlying cause
o	Monitor serum levels
o	Hypocalcemia  correct this as well (calcium gluconate IV)
o	Monitor IV site
o	Give phosphate binders
	Calcium carbonate  always dissolved, never chew
	Take with 8 oz of water  with meals or right after
	Can cause constipation  hypoactive bowel sounds

Hypocalcemia
-	Calcium less than 2.25
-	Pth: secreted by parathyroid gland and leads to increased serum calcium levels
-	Calcitonin: decreased serum calcium 
-	Causes: parathyroidectomy, thyroidectomy, radical neck surgery, vitamin d deficiency, intestinal malabsorption (celiac), phosphate binding calcium (CKD, tumor lysis syndrome)
-	Features:
o	Acts like a sedative normally  nerves and muscles are irritable when levels are low
o	Peripheral neuromuscular excitability (tetany)  Chvostek and trousseau
	Chvostek: tap facial nerve, spasm of facial muscles = positive response
	Trousseau: venous stasis for three minutes, carpal spasm = positive response 
o	Parasthesia (tingling of lips and fingers)
o	Hyperreflexia (3+) normal is 2+
o	Muscle cramps 
o	Seizures
o	Cardiac dysrhythmias 
o	Severe  respiratory depression, laryngeal stridor (both life threatening)
-	Nursing:
o	Notify HCP
o	IV calcium gluconate or chloride
o	Seizure precautions
o	ECG
o	Continuous cardiac monitoring
o	Respiratory status
o	Monitor for adventitious breath sounds 
o	Encourage calcium rich foods
	Don’t let fish and tofu spoil
	D: dairy
	L: leafy greens
	F: fish
	A: almonds
	T: tofu
	S: soy products

Enema Administration
-	Promote intestinal peristalsis  evacuates feces from colon
-	Indications:
o	Endoscopic procedures (colonoscopy)
o	Relief for constipation
-	2 types:
o	Cleansing – used to clean the bowel out 
	Tap water: water intoxication and hyponatremia 
	Soap suds: stimulate peristalsis by creating irritation/inflammation 
	Normal saline – isotonic solution – less fluid absorption

Foreign Body Aspiration
o	Causes obstruction
o	Commonly caused by small and round objects
o	Hot dogs, candy, peanuts, popcorn, grapes
o	Burst latex balloons, plastic beads, batteries, and coins
o	Batteries may cause esophageal or tracheal corrosion
o	Most common in children 1-3
o	Medical emergency
o	Nasal foreign body: unilateral purulent drainage that does not improve
o	Features: 
	Initially: universal choking sign – hands clutching neck, gagging, coughing, retractions, inability to talk, sweating, tachycardia
	Later: cyanosis  unconsciousness
o	Laryngotracheal obstruction:
	Stridor and hoarseness
o	Bronchial obstruction:
	Paroxysmal cough, wheezing, asymmetrical breath sounds 
	Other lung will be clear
-	Nursing:
o	Heimlich under age of 1
o	Position baby face down with head lower than body and support the baby’s head with forearm against thigh, provide 5 back thrusts and turn baby up if still choking and give five chest thrusts on sternum with 2-3 fingers, repeat until needed or perform cpr if baby becomes unconscious 
o	Adult: stand or kneel behind person and wrap arms around, place fist against abdomen above navel and give a quick upward thrust into the abdomen, repeat until necessary. 
o	Never do a blind finger sweep (if you don’t see it) 
o	If you see the object, take it out 
o	Endoscopy and bronchoscopy may be needed to remove 
o	Monitor for at least an hour afterwards for laryngeal edema 
o	Prevention: keep small objects out of reach, educate about avoiding small round foods and cut longitudinally

-	Long acting beta agonists – salmeterol

Negative Pressure Wound Therapy
-	uses an active suction that removes drainage and infectious organisms, increases circulation and helps to bring the edges of the wound together
-	only for wounds with healthy granulation tissue
-	surgically debride necrotic tissue prior to initiating therapy 
-	requires routine sterile dressing therapy 
-	administer analgesics prophylactically
-	apply a skin protectant to the intact skin around the wound to help maintain an airtight seal
-	sterile foam dressing should be cut to the shape and placed in the wound bed 
-	cover with occlusive film dressing that extends 1-2 inches past wound bed
-	attach vacuum tubing and then connect it to the closed unit
-	observe compression of the foam dressing

Submersion Injury
-	occurs when a client is underwater and unable to breath for a long time
-	initial period of panic – loss of normal breathing pattern  holding breath, air hunger, struggling to stay above water
-	children from ages 1-4 have the highest rates of submersion injury
-	life threatening complications – 
-	leading cause of accidental death in children
-	risk:
o	children less than 5
o	minimal supervision around water
o	inability to swim or overestimation of swimming abilities
o	risk-taking behaviour
o	substance abuse
o	developmental or behavioural disorders
o	seizure disorders
-	Features:	
o	Length of submersion dictates severity of impairment
o	More than five minutes the prognosis Is poor
o	Onset up to 24 hours after incident 
o	Fatal event  due to respiratory and cardiac impairment
o	Nonfatal event 
	Mild impairment: breathing, paroxysmal coughing, fully alert
	Moderate: difficulty breathing and or disoriented but conscious
	Severe: not breathing or unconscious
o	Hypoxia  hypoxic-ischemic brain injury 
o	May initially be cognitively intact but then suffer from hypoxia due to edema
o	Cardiac arrest
o	Apnea
o	Hypothermia: organ failure – weak thready pulse, cold water  ventricular dysrhythmia/PEA
o	Aspiration  ARDS 
	Reflex inspiratory efforts
	Coughing when water contacts lower respiratory tract 
-	Nursing:
o	Obtaining length of submersion is critical
o	First interventions:
	Focus on ABCs 
	Rescue and immediately resuscitate  administer rescue breaths first, then compressions  remove wet clothing
	Emergency dept interventions: 
•	Priority  airway management
•	Monitor for signs of respiratory distress
•	Administer oxygen
•	Prepare for intubation and mechanical ventilation
•	Monitor neurological status
•	Initial and frequent neurological checks 
•	If gcs is less than 8 = medical emergency = intubate and ventilate
o	Hypothermia:
	Monitor body temperature  slowly increase
•	Passive rewarming  remove wet clothing and apply warm blankets and dry clothing
	Active external rewarming
•	Heating devices
•	Warm water immersion
	Severe cases: active internal rewarming
•	Warm IV fluids
•	Warmed humidified oxygen 
-	Anticipatory guidance
o	Properly fence swimming pools
o	Supervise children closely around or near water 
o	Wear a properly fitted floatation device 
o	Teach children how to swim
o	Avoid substance abuse while swimming or boating
o	Children can drown in shallow water, even buckets and bathtubs 

Cleft Lip and Palate
-	Incomplete fusion of the palatine bones during fetal development = uncoordinated suck and swallow efforts
-	Increased risk for aspiration
-	Features:
o	Poor suction
o	Uncoordinated suck and swallow
o	Complications:
	Aspiration pneumonia
	Risk for ear infections and hearing loss
	Speech or language difficulties
	Dental problems
-	Nursing:
o	Encourage bonding 
o	Cannot breastfeed successfully
o	Feed in upright position
o	Burp after each ounce of formula 
o	Require a cross cut nipple
o	Position the nipple toward the back or side of the newborns mouth
-	Management:
o	Surgical repair
o	6-12 months after birth 
o	Postop care: 
	Elbow restraints
	Observe for excessive swallowing = fresh bleed
	Do not offer straws, pacifiers, rigid eating utensils
	Diet: soft foods and modified liquids
