Pyrosis (heartburn)
-	Common in pregnancy due to increase in progesterone and uterine enlargement 
-	Progesterone relaxes smooth muscles which results in esophageal sphincter relaxation
-	Lifestyle changes:
o	Keep HOB elevated
o	Sit upright after meals
o	Eat small and frequent meals
o	Avoid tight fitting clothing
o	Eliminate dietary triggers: milk, fat, caffeine, citrus, spicy foods, tomatoes, carbonated drinks, peppermint

Cirrhosis
-	Inflammation and formation of scar tissue secondary to chronic disease
-	Biliary structures such as the gallbladder and bile ducts become impaired
-	Pruritis may occur when bile ducts become obstructed and bile acid accumulates in the body
-	Cholestyramine is a bile acid sequestrant used to block the reabsorption of bile acid in the intestine, decreasing itching. It should be taken one hour after other medications because it can affect the absorption of many medications
-	Encourage client to cut nails short and use knucles to alleviate itching 
-	Promote moisturization to soothe irritated skin
-	Bathe in tepid water

ECT: electroconvulsive therapy
-	Induces a generalized seizure by passing an electrical current through electrodes applied to the scalp
-	15-20 second seizures are proven effective in treating mood disorders and schizophrenia
-	Teaching:
o	NPO 6-8 hours prior to treatment
o	Anesthesia and a muscle relaxant will be administered, clients are unconscious and feel no pain during the procedure
o	Driving is not permitted
o	Temporary memory loss and confusion in the immediate recovery period are common side effects of ECT
o	Post care treatment: monitor VS, ensure a patent airway, assess mental status, provide frequent reorientation during periods of postictal confusion 

COPD
-	Etiologies associated with the development of COPD:
o	Tobacco smoke: co2 decreases o2 carrying capacity and increases demand
o	Causes hyperplasia and increased mucus production
o	Causes alveolar wall destruction
o	Decreases ciliary action
o	Related to 80-90 percent of COPD deaths
-	Occupational exposure to chemicals and dust 
o	Prolonged exposure causes inflammation of airways
-	Air pollution
o	Inflammation of airways
-	Genetics (a1 antitrypsin deficiency)
o	Premature emphysema
-	COPD refers to emphysema and chronic bronchitis. Characterized by persistent airflow obstruction and is associated with chronic airway inflammation

Phenytoin
-	Anticonvulsant used to treat tonic-clonic and partial seizures. Has a narrow therapeutic range and requires regular monitoring of serum blood levels for early signs of toxicity. Abrupt cessation can put the patient at risk for increased seizures
-	Adverse effects:
o	Dose independent adverse effects:
	Hypertrichosis
	Gingival hyperplasia
	Folic acid deficiency
	Osteomalacia/vitamin d deficiency
	Drug induced lupus
	Anticonvulsant hypersensitivity syndrome
o	Dose dependent adverse effects
	Rash
	Horizongal nystagmus
	Ataxia
	Slurred speech
	Lethargy/confusion
	Coma
-	Important to maintain good oral hygiene with a soft bristle tooth brush and to visit the dentist regularly as phenytoin can cause gingival hyperplasia 
-	Folic acid supplementation can also reduce this side effect

Hypertensive encephalopathy
-	Medical emergency caused by a sudden elevation in BP (hypertensive crisis) creating cerebral edema and ICP. Triggers include acute exacerbation of preexisting hypertension, drug use, head injury, and pheochromocytoma
-	Client may report severe headache, visual impairment, anxiety, confusion, and observed epistaxis, seizures, coma
-	May precipitate MI, hemorrhagic stroke, AKI. 
-	Patient with known hypertension and signs of ICP need immediate medical attention

Cystic Fibrosis
-	Exacerbation
o	Greasy, bulky stools. Viscous secretions block pancreatic ducts preventing the release of the pancreatic enzymes necessary for fat absorption by the intestines. Clients should take replacement pancreatic enzymes with food to promote fat absorption and normal stool formation. Steatorrhea occurs when unabsorbed fat is released in the stool. 
o	Blood tinged sputum: pulmonary capillaries can be chronically damaged from repeated infections
o	Paroxysmal cough- violent coughing
o	Hypoxia- sudden drop in o2 signifies possible mucus plug in the airway

Cauda Equina Syndrome
-	Injury to the lumbosacral nerve (L4-L5) causing motor and sensory deficits
-	Cauda equina syndrome is a medical emergency that requires urgent reduction of pressure on the spinal nerves to prevent permanent damage 
o	Symptoms:
	Inability to walk
	Acute, severe lower back pain
	Bowel and bladder incontinence or retention
	Saddle anesthesia (motor weakness to innter thighs and buttocks)
-	A herniated disc can cause cauda equina syndrome because of spinal compression from a bulging disc. 
-	A medical emergency to prevent permanent damage

Grapefruit juice: acts on the same pathway used to metabolize calcium channel blockers and HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors (statins). Grapefruit juice increases levels of CCBs and HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors which can lead to serious adverse effects like hypotension or organ dysfunction. 

NSAIDS can cause cardiovascular side effects (MI, stroke, heart failure) and decrease the effectiveness of diuretics and antihypertensives. 
-	Exert anti-inflammatory, analgesic and antipyretic effects through the inhibition of COX. When blood circulation is compromised, the kidneys compensate by synthesizing prostaglandins. Prostaglandins help maintain renal perfusion by dilating the blood vessels that supply the nephrons, particularly in clients with CKD. Increased PG synthesis is necessary to preserve renal blood flow and maintain GFR. Inhibition of PG synthesis with NSAIDs results in reduced GFR. Ibuprofen should not be administered to a patient to prevent nephrotoxicity.

Hirschsprung disease
-	Characterized by a lack of specialized nerve cells in portions of the distal large intestine resulting in the inability of the internal anal sphincter to relax. There is an absence of peristalsis and bowel evacuation including the absence of meconium passage which is expected within 24-48 hours of birth. There will be a backup of intestinal contensts into the mouth, 
-	Symptoms:
o	Bilious vomiting
o	Abdominal distension
o	Failure to pass meconium
o	Failure of internal anal sphincter relaxation

Increased ICP
-	Results form cerebral swelling or hematoma and can continue to increase without treatment which may decrease cerebral perfusion and cause cerebrovascular ischemia, cranial nerve compression, seizure, coma, death
-	An acute alteration in LOC is the earliest sign of increased ICP
-	Cranial nerve functions will become impaired as icp increases, as evidenced by fixed or dilated pupils (cranial nerve 3). Brainstem and motor complex impairment may also be suspected if muscle flaccidity or posturing (decerebrate or decorticate) is present 
-	Projectile vomiting is an urgent sign of increased ICP 
-	Late signs are cushing’s triad: widening pulse pressure, bradycardia, hypertension

Albumin: 
-	Plasma protein synthesized by the liver that maintains intravascular oncotic pressure ( holds fluid inside the blood vessels)
-	- in clients with cirrhosis, albumin synthesis is decreased due to altered hepatic function. Decreased serum albumin results in decreased oncotic pressure causing fluid to shift form the vascular space into the interstitial space 
-	Effectiveness of treatment is indicated by blood pressure and heart rate being within normal limits

Uterine rupture
-	Signs: abnormal fetal heart rate patterns and constant abdominal pain, loss of fetal station, and sudden cessation of uterine contractions. 
-	Hemorrhage, hypovolemic shock, and maternal tachycardia may occur 

Memantine: 
-	Used to ease the symptoms of moderate to severe Alzheimer disease and improves QOL for clients 
-	It is an NMDA antagonist that works by binding to NMDA receptors, blocking the brain’s NMDA glutamate pathways and protecting brain cells from overexposure to glutamate 
-	May experience improvement in 
o	Cognition: memory, thinking, language
o	Daily functioning: dressing, bathing, grooming, eating
o	Behavioural problems: agitation, depression, hallucinations

Eclampsia
(severe preeclampsia and seizures)
-	Clinical features: hypertension, proteinuria, severe headaches, visual disturbances, RUQ pain, 3-4 minutes of tonic-clonic seizure, usually self-limites
-	Management: magnesium sulfate, antihypertensive, deliver the fetus 
-	Preeclampsia is a characterized by hypertension and proteinuria after 20 weeks gestation. 
-	Eclampsia is the onset of convulsions during pregnancy that cannot be attributed to other causes
-	Magnesium sulfate is a CNS depressant used to prevent and control seizure activity in clients with preeclampsia and eclampsia and if the client begins convulsing the nurse should anticipate administration of magnesium sulfate to stop the client’s seizures 
o	Magnesium toxicity: mild: nausea, flushing, headache, hyporeflexia
	Moderate: areflexia, hypocalcemia, somnolence
	Severe: respiratory paralysis, cardiac arrest
	Treatment: stop magnesium therapy
	Give calcium gluconate bolus
-	Proteinuria: signifies end-organ damage to the kidneys 
-	Giving birth is the cure for preeclampsia/eclampsia. Magnesium sulfate may cause a temporary tocolytic effect and stop contractions but this is not the desired clinical effect

Cleft Palate
-	Malformation of the secondary palate that occurs from the incomplete fusion of the palatine bones and maxilla 
-	Treatment: surgical repair 
-	Infants have an issue creating a seal resulting in poor suction during feedings 

Botulism
-	Food poisoning that occurs after consuming clostridium botulinum 
-	Can cause life-threatening paralysis 
-	Early manifestations: constipation, difficulty feeding, decreased head control, diminished deep-tendon reflexes 
-	Progresses rapidly to respiratory failure and arrest
-	Management: IV botulism immune globulin (BIG-IV) which reduces severity and duration of symptoms by improving immune response 
-	Mechanical ventilation and enteral feedings may be provided

Ziprasidone hydrochloride: atypical antipsychotic that is used for acute bipolar mania, acute psychosis, and agitation. 
-	Carries a risk of QT prolongation leading to torsades de pointes. Baseline ECG and potassium are usually checked. Patient should be placed on a cardiac monitor. Client should be monitored for seizures and hypotension. 
-	Risk for effects is increased with alcohol. 

Atrial fibrillation: common cardiac dysrhythmia following CABG. Characterized by absence of p waves, presence of fibrillatory waves, irregularly irregular rhythm. Results in incomplete atrial emptying, decreased CO, and increased risk for atrial thrombi formation due to blood stasis. 
-	May require antiarrhythmic medications such as beta blockers or digoxin 

CABG
-	Harvests healthy blood vessels from different parts of the body (great saphenous vein) to reroute myocardial blood flow around the blocked coronary artery. Blood is temporarily diverted away from the heart and circulated back to the client using a cardiopulmonary bypass machine. 
-	Following CABG clients are at risk for bleeding and fluid accumulation within the pericardial sac (pericardial effusion). Hypotension may indicate progression to cardiac tamponade. This impairs contractility of the heart and requires immediate pericardiocentesis, emergency sternotomy to restore function. If untreated, the graft can collapse. 

SLE
-	Autoimmune disorder where the body produces antibodies that attack the body’s tissues and cells. Characterized by alternating periods of exacerbation and remission. The skin is one of the target organs affected by the disease. 
-	Characteristics: flat or raised red rash that forms a butterfly shape on the nose and cheeks. Related to sunlight exposure. Recurrent oral ulcers are also common.

Mononucleosis
-	Caused by Epstein barr virus
-	Spleen rupture is a serious complication that can occur spontaneously and present with sudden onset of left upper quadrant pain 
-	Should monitor HGB levels and provide supportive care to prevent hemorrhagic shock, possible surgery

Correcting hyperkalemia
-	Albuterol nebulizer and IV insulin
o	Drives potassium from blood into cells 
o	Works temporarily 
o	Dextrose is administered in combination with insulin to prevent hypoglycemia 
o	Albuterol and insulin are used together 
-	IV furosemide
o	Promotes potassium excretion 
o	Increases the amount of potassium excreted into the urine at the loop of henle
	Can cause ototoxicity if administered too quickly (sensorineural hearing loss that is irreversible)
-	IV calcium gluconate
o	Stabilizes myocardial cell membranes 
o	Antagonizes potassium and protects the myocardium from potassium induced dysrhythmias by blocking potassiums effect and reducing MI 
o	Does not alter serum potassium levels and is indicated if there are ECG changes with hyperkalemia
-	Severe hyperkalemia can be treated with sodium polystyrene sulfonate 
o	Works in the GI tract to exchange sodium for potassium, thereby eliminating excess potassium through the stool and reducing the serum potassium levels 
o	Nurse should assess bowel sounds and review the medical record for frequency of stools prior to administering the medication because there is a risk for intestinal necrosis if sodium polystyrene sulfate is administered to clients without normal bowel function (postoperative, constipation, fecal impaction)

Bacterial meningitis
-	Inflammation of the meninges of the brain and spinal cord caused by infection 
-	Manifestations: early: fever, headache, vomiting, poor suck/feeding
o	Late: neck stiffness, rash, bulging fontanels 
-	Hydrocephalus = increased ICP resulting from obstruction of CSF
-	Bulging fontanels and increasing head circumference are early indicators of increased ICP 
-	Use a surgical mask, face shield, gown and gloves 
o	Life threatening inflammation of meninges
o	Caused by strep pneumonia
-	Nursing: 
o	Lumbar puncture- monitor site
o	Pathogen specific antibiotics
o	Droplet precautions
o	Decrease environmental stimuli for ICP 
o	Frequent neuro assessments 
o	HOB 30 degrees
o	Neutral neck 
o	Seizure precautions – suction available, side rails up, pillow to protect head 
Autonomic Dysreflexia
-	Life threatening response to noxious stimuli which clients with spinal cord injuries above T6 are unable to feel
-	Signs and symptoms: hypertension, bradycardia, pounding headache, diaphoresis, nausea
-	Assess for noxious stimuli
o	Bladder distention: obstructed urinary catheter, neurogenic bladder
o	Fecal impaction
o	Tight clothing

Hypglycemia
-	Occurs when the proportion of insulin exceeds the glucose levels in the blood
-	Counterregulatory hormones like epinephrine are released and the ANS is activated, causing multiple hypoglycemia associated symptoms including increased HR, shakiness, sweating, hunger, anxiety, and pallor
-	Lack of glucose in the brain is also responsible for other symptoms, including disorientation, impaired vision and speech, seizures, and coma

Macrolide antibiotics
-	Can cause prolonged QT interval which may cause sudden cardiac death due to torsades the pointes
-	ECG should be monitored
-	Amiodarone, sotalol, haloperidol, ziprasidone, azole antifungals 
-	Macrolides also cause hepatotoxicity when taken in high doses in combination with other hepatotoxic medications such as acetaminophen, phenothiazines,a nd sulfonamides 
-	Elevations of aspartate transaminase and alanine transaminase levels may indicate that hepatotoxicity is occurring, report to HCP

Nephrotic syndrome
-	Characterized by increased permeability of the glomerulus to proteins (albumin, immunoglobulins) and most commonly affects children ages 2-7. This results in hypoalbuminemia, lowering the plasma oncotic pressure, therefore allowing fluids to exit the vascular space more easily ad remain in the tissues. 
-	Loss of immunoglobulins causes increased susceptibility to infection. Parents should minimize the risk for infection by limiting the client’s physical contact with others during periods of relapse
-	4 classic manifestations
o	Massive proteinuria caused by increased glomerular permeability
o	Hypoalbuminemia from excess protein loss in the urine
o	Edema: periorbital and peripheral edema and ascites caused by low serum protein and albumin as fluid is pulled into interstitial spaces and body cavities
o	Hyperlipidemia related to increased compensatory protein and lipid production by the liver
o	Additional symptoms are decreased urine output, fatigue, pallor, weight gain

Newborn hypoglycemia
-	Serum glucose of 2.2-2.5 
-	In womb they receive continuous supply of glucose and a drop 1-2 hours after birth of cbg
-	Feeding helps to increase bg level and the body will save it and use it later
-	Risk factors: maternal history gestational diabetes, small or large for gestational age, cold stress
-	Clinical manifestations: hypothermia (axillary temp <36.5) poor feeding, irritability, tachypnea, respirations over 60/min, tremors
-	Symptomatic hypoglycemia requires buccal and IV dextrose to prevent seizures and neurological damage 
-	Priority actions = initiating feeding with formula or breast milk
o	Can cause seizures and neurological injury if not treated
o	Preterm babies are at risk 
o	Small or large for gestational age 
o	Maternal diabetes mellitus
	Mother produces less insulin so baby will overproduce after birth
o	Any type of stress response increases cortisol and increases insulin 
-	Symptoms:
o	Can be asymptomatic 
o	Hypothermia
o	Poor feeding
o	Irritability
o	Tremors
o	Light headed, clammy, shaky
o	Moro reflex will be exaggerated 
o	High resp rate
o	Tremors
o	Lethargy
-	Interventions
o	Screen newborn with risk factors or symptoms 
o	Check blood on the heel on the lateral side because the middle is full of nerves 
o	Early feeding and skin to skin contact
o	Monitor temperature and respiratory rate
o	If initial feeding doesn’t help you can administer dextrose in the mouth or through IV

Neonatal abstinence syndrome
-	Withdrawal is caused by in utero exposure to maternal use of substances (opioids)
-	Severity depends on the type of medication and the length of exposure as well as the last time the mother took the medication
-	Features:
o	Presents hours to days after birth 
o	Central nervous system -body will be on alarm mode and they will be irritable, high pitched cry, sweaty, short sleep cycles, sneezing, yawning, uncoordinated swallowing 
o	Respiratory: tachypnea, increased risk for aspiration
o	GI: vomiting and diarrhea, feeding difficulties
-	Nursing
o	Know what medication the baby was exposed to and use a newborn drug screen through urine or meconium 
o	We will know what to expect 
o	Monitor daily weight 
o	Feed in small, frequent amounts 
o	Minimize environmental stimuli
o	Promote swaddling tightly in flexed position
o	Pacifier for self calming 
o	Administer prescribed medications and evaluate response 
	Opioid agonists (morphine and methadone)
