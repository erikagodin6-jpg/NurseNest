Accelerated Idioventricular Rhythm

 Accelerated Idioventricular Rhythm ECG tracing
Accelerated idioventricular rhythm occurs when three or more ventricular escape beats appear in a sequence. Heart rate will be 50-100 bpm. The QRS complex will be wide (0.12 sec. or more).

Asystole

 Asystole ECG tracing
Asystole is the state of no cardiac electrical activity and no cardiac output. Immediate action is required.

Idioventricular Rhythm
 Idioventricular Rhythm ECG tracing
Idioventricular rhythm is a slow rhythm of under 50 bpm. It indicates that then ventricles are producing escape beats.

Premature Ventricular Complex
 Premature Ventricular Complex ECG tracing
Premature ventricular complexes (PVCs) occur when the ventricule creates the ECG impulse. This happens before the next regular sinus beat. Look for a wide QRS complex, equal or greater than 0.12 sec. The QRS complex shape can be bizarre. The P wave will be absent.

Ventricular Fibrillation
 Ventricular Fibrillation ECG tracing
Ventricular fibrillation originates in the ventricles, and it is chaotic. No normal ECG waves are present. No heart rate can be observed. Ventricular fibrillation is an emergency condition requiring immediate action.

Ventricular Tachycardia
 Ventricular Tachycardia ECG tracing
A sequence of three PVCs in a row is ventricular tachycardia. The rate will be 120-200 bpm. Ventricular Tachycardia has two variations, monomorphic and polymorphic. These variations are discussed separately.

Ventricular Tachycardia Monomorphic
 Ventricular Tachycardia Monomorphic ECG tracing
Monomorphic ventricular tachycardia occurs when the electrical impulse originates in one of the ventricles. The QRS complex is wide. Rate is above 100 bpm.

Ventricular Tachycardia Polymorphic
 Ventricular Tachycardia Polymorphic ECG tracing
Polymorphic ventricular tachycardia has QRS complexes that vary in shape and size. If a polymorphic ventricular tachycardia has a long QT Interval, it could be Torsade de Pointes.

Torsade de Pointes
 Torsade de Pointes ECG tracing
Torsade de Pointes is a special form of ventricular tachycardia. The QRS complexes vary in shape and amplitude and appear to wind around the baseline.
Ventricular Rhythm ECGs – Comprehensive Clinical Guide
Ventricular rhythms occur when the primary pacemaker activity originates in the ventricles rather than the sinoatrial (SA) node. Because ventricular depolarization spreads cell-to-cell instead of through the His-Purkinje system, these rhythms characteristically produce wide QRS complexes (≥ 0.12 seconds) and are often hemodynamically unstable.
From a clinical standpoint (especially in acute care, telemetry, ED, or ICU settings), rapid recognition is essential because several ventricular rhythms are immediately life-threatening.

1. Accelerated Idioventricular Rhythm (AIVR)






4
Definition
A ventricular rhythm of 3 or more consecutive ventricular beats with a rate between 50–100 bpm.
ECG Characteristics
Rate: 50–100 bpm
Rhythm: Usually regular
P waves: Absent or dissociated
PR interval: Not measurable
QRS: Wide (≥ 0.12 sec)
Clinical Context
Common during reperfusion after MI
Seen post-thrombolysis or PCI
Often transient and benign
Nursing Implications
Usually does not require treatment
Monitor hemodynamic stability
Assess perfusion and BP
Notify provider if symptomatic
2. Idioventricular Rhythm (Ventricular Escape Rhythm)






4
Definition
A ventricular escape rhythm with a rate < 50 bpm.
ECG Characteristics
Rate: 20–40 bpm (typically)
Regular rhythm
No P waves
Wide QRS
Clinical Significance
Occurs when:
SA node fails
AV block present
Severe bradycardia
Nursing Actions
Assess level of consciousness
Monitor BP
Prepare for atropine, pacing, or dopamine if unstable
Follow ACLS bradycardia algorithm if symptomatic
3. Premature Ventricular Complexes (PVCs)






4
Definition
An early ventricular beat occurring before the next sinus beat.
ECG Characteristics
Early beat
Wide bizarre QRS (≥ 0.12 sec)
No preceding P wave
Compensatory pause often present
Patterns
Bigeminy – every other beat is PVC
Trigeminy – every third beat is PVC
Quadrigeminy – every fourth beat is PVC
Clinical Causes
Hypoxia
Electrolyte imbalance (↓K⁺, ↓Mg²⁺)
Caffeine/stimulants
Ischemia
Digoxin toxicity
Nursing Priorities
Assess frequency and pattern
Check potassium and magnesium
Monitor for progression to VT
Treat underlying cause
4. Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)






4
Definition
Three or more consecutive PVCs at a rate > 100 bpm (often 120–200 bpm).
ECG Characteristics
Wide QRS
Regular (monomorphic) or irregular (polymorphic)
P waves absent or dissociated
Monomorphic VT
QRS complexes look identical
Usually originates from one ventricular focus
Often seen post-MI (scar tissue)
Polymorphic VT
QRS shape varies
Often associated with ischemia or electrolyte imbalance
May be unstable
Clinical Assessment: PULSE vs NO PULSE
Pulseless VT
Treat like VF
Immediate defibrillation
CPR + ACLS protocol
VT with Pulse
Stable → Amiodarone
Unstable → Synchronized cardioversion
5. Torsade de Pointes






4
Definition
A specific form of polymorphic VT associated with prolonged QT interval.
ECG Appearance
Twisting QRS complexes
Amplitude varies
Appears to “rotate” around baseline
Causes
Prolonged QT
Hypomagnesemia
Hypokalemia
Certain medications (e.g., antiarrhythmics, antipsychotics)
Treatment
IV Magnesium sulfate
Correct electrolytes
Defibrillate if pulseless
6. Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)






4
Definition
Chaotic ventricular electrical activity with no organized contraction.
ECG Characteristics
No identifiable P waves
No QRS complexes
No measurable heart rate
Chaotic waveform
Clinical Significance
No cardiac output
Immediate cardiac arrest
Most common arrest rhythm
Management
Immediate CPR
Defibrillation
Epinephrine
Amiodarone per ACLS
7. Asystole






4
Definition
Complete absence of ventricular electrical activity.
ECG Characteristics
Flatline
No P waves
No QRS
No T waves
Clinical Significance
No cardiac output
Non-shockable rhythm
Management
Confirm in 2 leads
CPR immediately
Epinephrine
Treat reversible causes (H’s and T’s)

Exam & Clinical Pearls (RPN/RN/NP)
Wide + Fast = Think VT until proven otherwise
AIVR is common post-MI reperfusion and usually benign
Torsades = long QT + magnesium deficiency
VF and pulseless VT are shockable
Asystole is NOT shockable
Always assess patient, not just monitor
