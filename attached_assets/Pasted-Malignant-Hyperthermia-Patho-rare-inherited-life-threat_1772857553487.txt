Malignant Hyperthermia
Patho: rare, inherited life threatening disorder, autosomal dominant trait, geneticmutation
-	Skeletal muscles react with triggering agent which causes muscles to contract, causes increased intracellular calcium levels  contraction and hypermetabolism in the muscle
-	Usually seen in the OR
-	End tidal CO2 is the first manifestation
Features:
-	Rapid and progressive rise in body temperature
o	Can be a late sign, 
o	Fever can exceed 107 F
o	Muscle rigidity (in jaw and extremities)
o	Damaged muscle releases proteins causing a dark red urine
o	Tachy cardia
o	Tachypnea
o	Unstable blood pressure
o	Diaphoresis
Triggers: certain anesthetics: succinylcholine (primary trigger)
-	Stress
-	Trauma
-	Excessive heat
Complications: 
-	Rhabdomyolysis: hyperkalemia
o	Due to muscles being in hypercontraction 
o	Muscles release proteins and potassium 
o	Cardiac arrhythmias
o	Cardiac arrest, death
Nursing: 
-	Discontinue anesthetic
-	Administer dantrolene  slows metabolism. Reduces muscle contractions
-	Respiratory/ventilatory support with 100% o2
-	Lower body temp
o	Cooling blanket
o	Ice packs
o	Chilled iv solutions 
o	Dantrolene can be given as well
o	To prevent future events: anesthetic team will ask questions 

Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome
-	Patho: life threatening adverse reaction to antipsychotic medications
-	Haloperidol, risperidone 
-	Usually associated with first class medications but can happen with any antipsych meds
-	Typically occurs 1-3 days after initiation or rapid dose escalation
-	May be caused by dysfunction in areas of central nervous system
o	Thermoregulation
o	Regulation of muscle tone and movement
-	When they have a new dose or dose adjustment
o	Alterations in mental status (early sign)
o	As it progresses: high fever (temp above 103)
o	Muscle rigidity leading to resistance
o	Autonomic dysfunction – diaphoresis, tachypnea, hypertension, tachycardia, dysrhythmia
-	Nursing
o	Early recognition is critical
o	Discontinue the antipsychotic immediately
o	Initiate seizure precautions
o	Monitor for rhabdomyolysis – can affect kidneys and heart
o	Supportive treatment: cooling blankets, fluid and electrolyte replacement 
o	Severe cases: muscle relaxant (dantrolene), dopamine agonist (bromocriptine), muscle relaxants
Peptic Ulcer Disease
-	Occurs when there is a breakdown of the stomach mucosa
-	Affects stomach and duodenum
-	Erosion and ulceration of protective mucosa  breakdown of underlying tissue by digestive enzymes
-	Risks:
o	H pylori
o	NSAIDs and corticosteroids
o	Smoking
o	Alcohol
o	Excessive caffeine
o	Stress
o	Trauma/critical illness
-	Features:
o	Hallmark: abdominal pain in left or midepigastric pain 1-2 hours after meal (gastric ulcer)
o	Pain 2-5 hours after meals if it’s a duodenal ulcer
o	Back pain with posterior ulcers 
o	Pain relief with antacids
-	Complications:
o	GI bleeding (melena)
	Hematemesis
o	Perforation
	Peritonitis: diffuse abdominal pain, rigidity, guarding (life threatening)
-	Diagnosis:
o	EGD (esophagogastroduodenoscopy) Upper scope
-	Nursing:
o	Nonbleeding:
	Antibiotics for H pylori
	Oral PPI 
	Sucralfate for GI mucosa protection 
•	Works differently
•	Has to bind to stomach acid to become effective then forms a protective lining 
•	If stomach acid isn’t present with a PPI, then the sucralfate won’t bind or work  time 2 hours after a meal or one hour before to make sure it is effective
o	Bleeding
	NPO status 
•	Prevents irritation of ulcer
•	Anticipate endoscopy
	Monitor for hemoglobin and hematocrit levels 
•	Transfuse packed RBC as necessary
•	Monitor for hypovolemic shock 
-	Patient teaching
o	Remove triggers that can lead to injury
o	Avoid spicy or acidic foods 
o	Avoid substances that stimulate acid secretion 
	NSAIDs
	Alcohol
	Tobacco
	Caffeine
	Chocolate
o	Monitor for signs of bleeding
o	Monitor for signs of perforation
	Increase in abdominal or back pain, high fever
