Sinus Tachycardia ECG Interpretation – Comprehensive Clinical Lesson
Learning Objectives
At the completion of this lesson, the learner will be able to:
Define sinus tachycardia using strict ECG criteria
Apply the five-step rhythm analysis method to confirm sinus origin
Differentiate sinus tachycardia from supraventricular tachycardia (SVT)
Explain physiologic and pathologic causes
Recognize when sinus tachycardia signals clinical instability
Outline appropriate clinical management priorities
1. Definition of Sinus Tachycardia
Sinus tachycardia is a sinus rhythm with a heart rate greater than 100 beats per minute.
The electrical impulse originates from the sinoatrial (SA) node, just as in normal sinus rhythm.
The only defining difference between normal sinus rhythm and sinus tachycardia is the rate.
All other components of rhythm analysis remain normal.
Typical rate range:
100–160 bpm (occasionally higher in young patients)
2. Electrophysiology Review






4
The SA node normally fires at 60–100 bpm.
In sinus tachycardia:
SA node automaticity increases
Sympathetic tone rises
Parasympathetic tone decreases
This results in faster impulse formation but normal conduction pathways.
3. Five-Step Rhythm Analysis Applied to Sinus Tachycardia
Step 1: Rhythm Regularity
R–R intervals are regular.
Beats occur at equal intervals.
Step 2: Heart Rate
Using the 1500 method for regular rhythms:
Heart Rate = 1500 ÷ number of small boxes between R waves
If result > 100 bpm → tachycardia confirmed.
Step 3: P Wave Morphology






4
P waves must be:
Upright in lead II
Uniform in morphology
Present before every QRS
1:1 P:QRS ratio
As the rate increases, P waves may merge with the preceding T wave.
This can make PR interval measurement difficult.
Step 4: PR Interval
Normal range:
0.12 – 0.20 seconds
PR interval remains normal but may be difficult to measure if the P wave becomes partially buried in the preceding T wave.
If needed, increase ECG paper speed to 50 mm/sec to better separate complexes.
Step 5: QRS Complex






4
QRS duration:
0.06 – 0.10 seconds
QRS complexes are:
Narrow
Uniform
Consistent

Only the rate is abnormal.
5. Causes of Sinus Tachycardia
Sinus tachycardia is usually a physiologic response, not a primary arrhythmia.
Physiologic Causes
Exercise
Anxiety
Emotional stress
Pain
Caffeine
Nicotine
Pathologic Causes
Fever
Hypovolemia
Hemorrhage
Shock
Sepsis
Pulmonary embolism
Heart failure
Hyperthyroidism
Anemia
In most clinical settings, sinus tachycardia is a compensatory mechanism.
6. Pathophysiology
Cardiac Output = Heart Rate × Stroke Volume
When stroke volume falls (e.g., dehydration, hemorrhage), the body increases heart rate to maintain cardiac output.
Persistent tachycardia:
Increases myocardial oxygen demand
Decreases diastolic filling time
Can worsen ischemia
7. Differentiating Sinus Tachycardia from SVT
Sinus tachycardia:
Gradual onset
Gradual termination
Visible upright P waves
Rate usually < 160 bpm
Supraventricular tachycardia (SVT):
Sudden onset
Sudden termination
P waves often absent or hidden
Rate typically 150–250 bpm
Key distinguishing feature: visible, normal P waves preceding each QRS.
8. Clinical Presentation
Symptoms depend on cause and rate.
Mild tachycardia:
Palpitations
Mild anxiety
Severe tachycardia:
Hypotension
Chest pain
Dyspnea
Lightheadedness
Reduced perfusion
Always evaluate underlying cause.
9. When Is Sinus Tachycardia Concerning?
Sinus tachycardia becomes dangerous when:
Rate persistently > 130–140 bpm at rest
Hypotension present
Chest pain develops
Signs of shock appear
Myocardial ischemia suspected
Tachycardia is often the first sign of clinical deterioration.
10. Clinical Management Principles
Key concept:
Treat the cause, not the rate.
Initial approach:
Assess airway, breathing, circulation
Obtain vital signs
Establish IV access
Perform 12-lead ECG
Identify precipitating factors
Examples:
Fever → antipyretics
Hypovolemia → IV fluids
Sepsis → antibiotics
Anemia → transfusion
Rate-control medications are rarely indicated unless tachycardia is inappropriate or causing instability.
11. Practice Strip Interpretation Example
Rhythm: Regular
Rate: 118 bpm

QRS: 0.08 sec
Final Interpretation: Sinus Tachycardia
12. Clinical Pearls
• Sinus tachycardia is usually compensatory
• P waves remain normal and upright
• Rate rarely exceeds 160 bpm in adults
• Always search for underlying cause
• Persistent tachycardia increases myocardial oxygen demand
• Treat physiology, not just the monitor
13. Key Takeaway
Sinus tachycardia is a sinus rhythm originating from the SA node with a rate greater than 100 bpm. Diagnosis requires systematic application of the five-step rhythm analysis method. Clinical importance depends on the underlying cause and patient stability.
Mastering sinus tachycardia ensures accurate differentiation from pathologic tachyarrhythmias and supports appropriate intervention.
