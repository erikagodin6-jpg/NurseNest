Sinus Bradycardia ECG Interpretation – Comprehensive Clinical Lesson

1. Definition of Sinus Bradycardia
Sinus bradycardia is a sinus rhythm with a heart rate less than 60 beats per minute.
The rhythm originates in the sinoatrial (SA) node, just like normal sinus rhythm. The only distinguishing feature is the slower rate.
“Brady” means slow.
“Cardia” refers to the heart.
All other components of the five-step rhythm analysis remain normal.
2. Electrophysiology Review






4
The SA node normally fires at 60–100 bpm. In sinus bradycardia, the SA node is still the pacemaker, but its firing rate is decreased.
Intrinsic pacemaker rates:
SA node: 60–100 bpm
AV node: 40–60 bpm
Ventricular pacemaker: 20–40 bpm
In sinus bradycardia, the SA node rate slows but still overrides lower pacemakers.
3. Five-Step Rhythm Analysis Applied to Sinus Bradycardia
Step 1: Rhythm Regularity
R–R intervals are regular.
The spacing between beats is constant.
Step 2: Heart Rate
Using the 1500 method (for regular rhythms):
Heart Rate = 1500 ÷ number of small boxes between R waves
If result < 60 bpm → bradycardia confirmed.
Step 3: P Wave Morphology






4
P waves must be:
Upright in lead II
Uniform in morphology
Present before every QRS
There must be a 1:1 P:QRS relationship.
Step 4: PR Interval
Normal range:
0.12 – 0.20 seconds
PR interval should:
Be constant
Fall within normal limits
Step 5: QRS Complex






4
QRS duration:
0.06 – 0.10 seconds
QRS complexes:
Narrow
Uniform
Consistent morphology
4. ECG Criteria Summary Table
Feature	Sinus Bradycardia Finding
Rate	< 60 bpm
Rhythm	Regular
P waves	Upright, uniform
P:QRS	1:1
PR interval	0.12–0.20 sec
QRS duration	0.06–0.10 sec
Only the rate is abnormal.
5. Causes of Sinus Bradycardia
Physiologic (Normal Variants)
Athletes (high vagal tone)
Sleep
Relaxation
Meditation
These patients are typically asymptomatic.
Pathologic Causes
Hypothyroidism
Hypothermia
Increased intracranial pressure
Inferior myocardial infarction
Sick sinus syndrome
SA node ischemia
Medication-Induced
Beta blockers
Calcium channel blockers
Digoxin
Amiodarone
6. Clinical Presentation
Symptoms depend on perfusion.
Asymptomatic:
Common in healthy individuals
Symptomatic:
Dizziness
Hypotension
Syncope
Fatigue
Chest pain
Altered level of consciousness
Bradycardia becomes clinically concerning when it reduces cardiac output.
7. Pathophysiology
Cardiac Output = Heart Rate × Stroke Volume
If heart rate drops significantly:
Cardiac output decreases
Blood pressure falls
Organ perfusion decreases
Older adults and patients with heart failure are more vulnerable to low-rate effects.
8. Differentiating Sinus Bradycardia from Other Slow Rhythms
Junctional Rhythm
P waves absent or inverted
Rate 40–60 bpm
May have narrow QRS
Key difference: abnormal or absent P waves.
Second-Degree AV Block
Dropped QRS complexes
PR interval abnormalities
Sinus bradycardia maintains 1:1 conduction.
9. When Is Sinus Bradycardia an Emergency?
Sinus bradycardia is dangerous if:
HR < 40 bpm with symptoms
Hypotension present
Syncope occurs
Acute myocardial infarction suspected
Pauses accompany bradycardia
Severe bradycardia may lead to cardiogenic shock.
10. Clinical Management (ACLS Overview)
If asymptomatic:
Monitor
Identify cause
Review medications
If symptomatic:
Assess airway, breathing, circulation
Administer oxygen
Establish IV access
Obtain 12-lead ECG
Prepare atropine 0.5 mg IV (per ACLS protocol)
Consider transcutaneous pacing if atropine ineffective
Persistent symptomatic bradycardia may require permanent pacemaker placement.

12. Clinical Pearls
• Sinus bradycardia differs from normal sinus rhythm only by rate
• Always assess symptoms before initiating treatment
• Athletes may normally have resting HR in 40s
• Inferior MI commonly causes bradycardia
• Treat the patient, not just the monitor
13. Key Takeaway
Sinus bradycardia is a sinus rhythm originating from the SA node with a rate less than 60 bpm. Diagnosis requires systematic application of the five-step method. Clinical significance depends on patient stability and perfusion status.
Understanding sinus bradycardia ensures accurate differentiation from conduction blocks and escape rhythms and guides appropriate intervention.
