EXAMPLE QUESTION STRUCTURE: FNP

Question:
A 52-year-old patient with type 2 diabetes presents for follow-up. Their A1C is 9.1% despite metformin therapy, BMI is 34, and eGFR is normal. Which medication addition would most appropriately improve glycemic control while also supporting weight loss?

A Glyburide
B Pioglitazone
C Semaglutide
D NPH insulin

Correct Answer: C

Correct Answer Explanation:
Semaglutide is a GLP-1 receptor agonist that lowers A1C and supports weight loss. In an overweight patient with persistent hyperglycemia despite metformin and preserved renal function, it is an appropriate next step.

Incorrect Options:
A: Glyburide may lower glucose but increases risk of hypoglycemia and does not promote weight loss.
B: Pioglitazone can improve insulin sensitivity but often causes weight gain and fluid retention.
D: Insulin may be appropriate in some cases, but a GLP-1 receptor agonist better addresses both glycemic control and obesity here.

Clinical Takeaway:
For many patients with type 2 diabetes and obesity, GLP-1 receptor agonists offer both glycemic benefit and weight reduction.

Exam Pearl:
When two treatments improve glucose control, the one with cardiometabolic benefit and weight reduction is often preferred.

specialtyTrack: "FNP"
examTier: "NP"

EXAMPLE QUESTION STRUCTURE: PMHNP

Question:
A patient presents with decreased need for sleep, pressured speech, grandiosity, and excessive goal-directed activity for 8 days with marked impairment in occupational functioning. Which diagnosis is most appropriate?

A Cyclothymic disorder
B Bipolar II disorder
C Bipolar I disorder, manic episode
D Generalized anxiety disorder

Correct Answer: C

Correct Answer Explanation:
A manic episode lasting at least 1 week or requiring hospitalization, especially with marked impairment, supports bipolar I disorder.

Incorrect Options:
A: Cyclothymic disorder involves fluctuating hypomanic and depressive symptoms that do not meet full episode criteria.
B: Bipolar II disorder involves hypomania, not full mania with marked impairment.
D: Generalized anxiety disorder does not explain grandiosity, decreased need for sleep, and classic manic symptoms.

Clinical Takeaway:
Marked impairment plus classic manic symptoms strongly suggests bipolar I disorder.

Exam Pearl:
Hypomania does not cause marked impairment; mania does.

specialtyTrack: "PMHNP"
examTier: "NP"

EXAMPLE QUESTION STRUCTURE: AGACNP

Question:
A hospitalized patient develops acute shortness of breath, hypotension, jugular venous distention, and muffled heart sounds. Which diagnosis should be suspected first?

A Tension pneumothorax
B Cardiac tamponade
C Acute pulmonary edema
D Massive pleural effusion

Correct Answer: B

Correct Answer Explanation:
Hypotension, JVD, and muffled heart sounds are Beck’s triad and strongly suggest cardiac tamponade.

Incorrect Options:
A: Tension pneumothorax can cause hypotension and JVD, but muffled heart sounds and Beck’s triad specifically point to tamponade.
C: Pulmonary edema causes respiratory distress but not the classic triad seen here.
D: Massive pleural effusion may impair breathing but does not produce Beck’s triad.

Clinical Takeaway:
Beck’s triad is a classic board-style clue for cardiac tamponade.

Exam Pearl:
When hypotension, JVD, and muffled heart sounds occur together, tamponade should be considered immediately.
