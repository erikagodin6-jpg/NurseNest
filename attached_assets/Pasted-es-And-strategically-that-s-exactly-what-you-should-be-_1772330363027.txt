Why This Works (Especially for You)
As an RN in grad school building exam-focused products, your advantage is:
Clinical reasoning
Understanding of REx-PN vs NCLEX differences
Knowing what students actually struggle with
Knowing how exam questions are written
AI can generate:
Structured pathophysiology explanations
Organized medication tables
Basic assessment lists
Draft rationales
Question stems
But AI cannot reliably:
Prioritize content by exam weight
Identify high-yield distractors
Catch subtle Canadian vs U.S. practice differences
Apply true clinical nuance
That’s your layer.
The Ideal AI → Edit Workflow for Study Guides
Step 1: AI Generates Structured Skeleton
Have AI generate in sections:
Learning Objectives
Pathophysiology
Signs & Symptoms
Assessment Findings
Labs & Diagnostics
Medications (with mechanism)
Nursing Interventions
Complications
Patient Teaching
15–30 Practice Questions
Rationales
Keep it modular so you can regenerate sections without rewriting everything.
Step 2: You Do High-Level Editing
You should edit for:
Exam relevance
Remove low-yield textbook fluff.
Canadian vs U.S. clarity
Separate:
REx-PN terminology
NCLEX terminology
Lab value differences
Clinical prioritization
Add:
“Most tested concept”
“Classic exam trap”
“If you see this, think this”
Tone tightening
Remove generic phrases:
“It is important to note…”
“This condition may present with…”
Replace with:
“On exams, hyperkalemia = peaked T waves first.”
“Priority: airway before glucose.”
Step 3: You Add Value AI Cannot Replicate
This is critical for monetization.
Add:
Clinical pearls from practice
“Common student mistake” callouts
Exam-style decision trees
Prioritization flowcharts
Comparison tables (e.g., DKA vs HHS)
That’s what makes it sellable instead of generic.
How to Prompt AI Properly for Rough Drafts
Here’s a prompt structure that works extremely well:
Create a structured rough draft study guide for [topic].

Audience:
- REx-PN and NCLEX-PN students
- Canadian and U.S. context separated clearly

Include:
- Learning objectives
- Pathophysiology (concise but exam-focused)
- Classic signs and symptoms
- Assessment findings
- Labs and diagnostics (include normal ranges CA and US)
- First-line medications with mechanism
- Nursing interventions
- Complications
- 20 exam-style multiple choice questions
- Detailed rationales explaining why each distractor is wrong

Write in clear, direct, exam-prep tone.
Avoid textbook filler language.
Prioritize high-yield concepts.
This produces something usable.
Quality Control Rule
Before publishing, ask yourself:
Would this compete with:
UWorld-style explanations?
Archer rationales?
A structured nursing textbook?
If not, refine.
What You Should NOT Let AI Fully Control
Avoid fully auto-publishing:
Drug dosages without verification
Canadian scope-specific practice statements
Policy-based recommendations
Legal/ethics content
MAiD or regulatory guidance
Always fact-check anything that could vary by jurisdiction.
