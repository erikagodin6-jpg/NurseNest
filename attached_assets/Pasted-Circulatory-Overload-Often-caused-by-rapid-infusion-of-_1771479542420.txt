Circulatory Overload
- Often caused by rapid infusion of blood products
o Assessment:
 Cough, dyspnea

 Orthopnea, SOB, crackles, distended neck veins and bounding pulses
 High BP, high heart rate
 Do not stop infusion, slow the rate
 Place in high fowler, keep feet in a dependent position
 May need oxygen and diuretic, furosemide

Dehydration
- Causes:
o Fluid loss from GI tract (vomiting, diarrhea)
o Decreased oral intake
o Sensible losses (skin or respiratory tract with hyperventilation)
o Burns/trauma
o Can rapidly progress to hypovolemic shock
o Infants and younger children have a higher percentage of body water 
dehydration occurs more quickly

- Types
o Serum sodium level determines the type of dehydration
 Isotonic: losing equal amount of water and electrolytes
 Greatest threat to life with shock
 Primary form of dehydration in children
 Hypotonic:
 Losing more electrolytes than fluids (marathon runner)
 Sodium osmolality will be low
 Hypertonic:
 Water loss exceeds electrolyte loss
 Diuretic use/misuse
 Patient will have hypernatremia

- Features:
o Based on degree of dehydration
o Mild: 3-5% of weight loss – increased thirst, decreased intake, increased losses
 Minimal abnormalities on exam
o Moderate (6-9%)
 Decreased skin turgor, dry mucous membranes
 Sunken eyes/fontanelles
 Body compensates with tachycardia decreased urine output
o Severe: (&gt;10% loss)
 Emergency
 Heading to hypovolemic shock
 Listless, lethargic, unresponsive
 Weak pulse, hypotension, tachypnea
 Decreased cap refill &gt;2 seconds

 Skin tenting oliguria/anuria

- Nursing:
o Check cap refill, skin turgor, mucous membranes, fontanelles
o Daily weights
o Rehydration therapy
 Mild to moderate: oral rehydration solution
 Severe: IV fluids
o Advance diet as tolerated strict intake and output
o Monitoring serum electrolytes and replacing as lost
o Monitor urine output

Hypercalcemia

- Calcium helps regulate nerve and muscle function
- High levels of calcium in the blood (&gt;10.5mg/dl or 2.62 mmol/L)
- Calcium is absorbed within GI tract and stored in bones and is eliminated by the kidneys
- Any change in the process affects levels
o Causes:
 Increased calcium absorption
 Excessive intake of calcium and vitamin D
 Decreased calcium excretion
 Thiazide diuretics
 Increased bone resorption of calcium
 Parathyroid is responsible for increasing serum calcium levels
expulsion and it also affects kidneys and tells it to reabsorb
 Calcitonin regulates calcium levels – opposite of parathyroid –
helps to decrease levels of calcium

 Hyperparathyroidism
 Malignancy (bone cancer)
 Hemoconcentration from dehydration

- Features:
o Symptomatic when calcium is over 3
o Acts like a diuretic and sedative
o Symptoms:
 Polyuria  dehydration  polydipsia
o Calcium helps regulate nerve and muscle function
o Decreased neuromuscular excitability – muscle weakness  paralysis  decreased
reflexes
o Decreased intestinal paralysis  constipation
o Cardiac dysrhythmias
o Severe and rapid hypercalcemia  lethargy, confusion, stupor, coma
o Monitor calcium and report over 2.62
o Treat underlying cause
o Increase oral fluid intake
o Administer normal saline, bisphosphonates and calcitonin
o Discontinue calcium containing medications and thiazide diuretics
o Continuous cardiac monitoring
o Monitor LOC

Hyperkalemia
- High serum potassium over 5
- Major intracellular ion and maintains intracellular fluid volume
o Causes:
 Creates action potential on the cell membrane
 Large intake of potassium (oral or IV)
 Reduced renal excretion of potassium in AKI or CKD, spironolactone, ACE
inhibitors

 Metabolic acidosis (DKA)
 Insulin deficiency/hyperglycemia
 Muscle injury (rhabdomyolysis)
 Tumor lysis syndromes
 Pseudohyperkalemia – during the process of storing blood the RBC may
rupture and can lead to excessive levels of potassium inside of the
sample so maintaining proper techniques is important

- Features:
o Baseline ECG – tall peaked T waves and absence of p waves
o Cardiac conduction problems – heart block
o Ascending muscle weakness  paralysis
o Lethal cardiac dysrhythmias
 V- fib, asystole
 Widened qrs complex
 St segment depression
o If untreated or severe – leads to widened qrs and then asystole
o Monitor serum electrolytes all throughout treatment
o Continuous cardiac monitoring
o Interventions to promote excretion
o Potassium binders (patiromir, sodium polystyrene sulfonate (kayexalate))
o Dialysis for severe renal impairment
o Medications to drive potassium from blood into cells
 Insulin drives potassium into cells (regular insulin and iv dextrose)
 Be careful with this as it can cause hypoglycemia
 Betaadrenergic agonists (albuterol)  not as effective
o ECG changes  medications to stabilize myocardial cell membranes such as IV
calcium gluconate
o Encourage patient to avoid foods with high potassium
 Fast  fish, avocado, spinach and strawberries, tomato
 Bunny can carry many oranges  bananas cantaloupe, carrots,
mushrooms, oranges
 Roast potatoes, pork, beef: raisins, potatoes pork, beef

Hypermagnesemia
- Normal range is 1.5-2.5 mEq/L
- Second most abundant ICF electrolyte
- Usual role: muscle contraction at neuromuscular junction using acetylcholine
- Inverse relationship with calcium
- Works with potassium (if potassium goes up, so does magnesium)
- Excess intake – not dietary – magnesium containing antacids
- IV mag sulfate for preeclampsia
- Impaired excretion  kidney disease
- Findings:
o Magnesium toxicity
 Mild: nausea, flushing, headache, diminished DTR

 Moderate: hypocalcemia, somnolence, absent DTR, hypotension,
bradycardia
 Severe: flaccid quadriplegia, respiratory failure, cardiac arrest

- Nursing:
o Address underlying cause
o Monitor serum magnesium
o Avoiding more intake
 Magnesium rich-foods: spinach, broccoli, avocados, potatoes, whole
grains
 Magnesium containing meds
o Reverse, remove magnesium
 Isotonic fluids
 IV calcium gluconate
o Dialysis
o Cardiac monitor
 Heart blocks

Hypernatremia
- Major extracellular cation outside of body cells
- Cell membrane function and action potential
- Controls fluid movement
- Sodium over 145
- Pulls water from brain into cerebral veins  veins enlarge and rupture
- Causes:
o Too much salt or losing too much water
o Excess sodium intake
o Hypertonic nacl, IV sodium bicarb
o Near drowning in salt water
o Decreased sodium excretion  hyperaldosteronism, kidney disease
o Inadequate water intake
 Unconscious or cognitively impaired
 Prolonged fasting, NPO status
 Excess water loss  diarrhea, vomiting, sweating, hyperventilating
 Diabetes insipidus

- Features:
o Too much salt, not enough water
o Neurologic: early: lethargy, weakness, irritability
 Late: Twitching, seizures, coma
 Signs of dehydration: dry mucous membranes, increased thirst,
decreased urination, increased heart rate

- Nursing:
o Monitor serum sodium levels and report over 145
o Increase oral fluid intake and decrease dietary sodium intake
o Isotonic or hypotonic iv fluids (0.45 NACL)

o Monitor response to treatment
o Decrease sodium level slowly  rapidly decrease in sodium levels  cerebral
edema and permanent neurologic complications
o Seizure precautions
o Monitor intake and output

Hyperphosphatemia
- Normal range 2.7-4.5
- Located in ICF
- Metabolizes calcium (inverse relationship)

- RBC function  holds cell membranes together

- Rhabdomyolysis

- Increased renal reabsorption
- Hypoparathyroidism (decreased pth)
- Features:
o Binds to calcium and lowers it so if its high, hypocalcemia
o Numbness and tinging
o Muscle cramps, tetany (medical emergency)
o Laryngospasm  stridor (emergency)

o Monitor serum levels

o Monitor IV site

 Take with 8 oz of water  with meals or right after

Hypocalcemia

- Pth: secreted by parathyroid gland and leads to increased serum calcium levels
- Calcitonin: decreased serum calcium
- Causes: parathyroidectomy, thyroidectomy, radical neck surgery, vitamin d deficiency,

- Features:

low

 Chvostek: tap facial nerve, spasm of facial muscles = positive response
 Trousseau: venous stasis for three minutes, carpal spasm = positive
response

o Parasthesia (tingling of lips and fingers)
o Hyperreflexia (3+) normal is 2+
o Muscle cramps
o Seizures
o Cardiac dysrhythmias
o Severe  respiratory depression, laryngeal stridor (both life threatening)
- Nursing:
o Notify HCP
o IV calcium gluconate or chloride
o Seizure precautions
o ECG
o Continuous cardiac monitoring
o Respiratory status
o Monitor for adventitious breath sounds
o Encourage calcium rich foods

 D: dairy
 L: leafy greens
 F: fish
 A: almonds
 T: tofu
 S: soy products

Enema Administration
- Promote intestinal peristalsis  evacuates feces from colon
- Indications:
o Endoscopic procedures (colonoscopy)
o Relief for constipation
- 2 types:
o Cleansing – used to clean the bowel out
 Tap water: water intoxication and hyponatremia
 Soap suds: stimulate peristalsis by creating irritation/inflammation
 Normal saline – isotonic solution – less fluid absorption
 Hypertonic, small volume: prepackaged with rectal tip, contains sodium
phosphate – easier to tolerate, helps ease passage of stool. Monitor for
hyperphosphatemia
 To administer: inspect for distension,auscultate bowel sounds.
 Place in sims position left lateral with right knee flexed
 Place waterproof pad under bum
 Hang enema no more than 12 inches above the rectum to prevent
cramping
 Lubricate the tube and insert 3-4 inches (7.5-10 cm) into the
rectum, pointing the tip toward the umbilicus
 Open roller clamp slowly
 Monitor for cramping or discomfort  temporarily stop for 15-30
seconds, encourage deep breathing and relax, resume at a slower
rate
 Retain solution for as long as needed

o Barium
 Contrast medium  visualize the colon using fluoroscopic x-ray
 Before: laxatives until colon is clear, clear liquid diet the day before, NPO
for 8 hours, cramping and urge to defecate are expected
 After: fluids and laxatives to expel barium, observe stool for contrast
medium, stool may be white for up to 72 hours

Foreign Body Aspiration
o Causes obstruction
o Commonly caused by small and round objects
o Hot dogs, candy, peanuts, popcorn, grapes
o Burst latex balloons, plastic beads, batteries, and coins
o Batteries may cause esophageal or tracheal corrosion
o Most common in children 1-3
o Medical emergency
o Nasal foreign body: unilateral purulent drainage that does not improve
o Features:

 Initially: universal choking sign – hands clutching neck, gagging, coughing,
retractions, inability to talk, sweating, tachycardia
 Later: cyanosis  unconsciousness
o Laryngotracheal obstruction:
 Stridor and hoarseness
o Bronchial obstruction:
 Paroxysmal cough, wheezing, asymmetrical breath sounds
 Other lung will be clear

- Nursing:
o Heimlich under age of 1
o Position baby face down with head lower than body and support the baby’s head
with forearm against thigh, provide 5 back thrusts and turn baby up if still
choking and give five chest thrusts on sternum with 2-3 fingers, repeat until
needed or perform cpr if baby becomes unconscious
o Adult: stand or kneel behind person and wrap arms around, place fist against
abdomen above navel and give a quick upward thrust into the abdomen, repeat
until necessary.
o Never do a blind finger sweep (if you don’t see it)
o If you see the object, take it out
o Endoscopy and bronchoscopy may be needed to remove
o Monitor for at least an hour afterwards for laryngeal edema
o Prevention: keep small objects out of reach, educate about avoiding small round
foods and cut longitudinally

Metered Dose Inhaler
- Allows for measured dose administration of inhaled medications
- Short acting beta agonists - albuterol
- Long acting beta agonists – salmeterol
- Inhaled corticosteroids -fluticasone
- Shake canister for 3-5 seconds
- Tilt head back slightly and exhale slowly for 3-5 seconds
- Hold canister about 1.5 inches in front of open mouth
- Compress canister while inhaling slowly for 3-5 seconds
- Hold breath for 10 seconds
- Wait 1-2 mins before taking a second puff
- First puff dilates the bronchioles and allows easy passage of the second puff
- Can add a spacer if patient has a hard time coordinating
- Dry powder inhalers:
o Remove mouthpiece cover, do not shake
o Prepare medication inside inhaler
o Exhale
o Seal lips around mouthpiece
o Hold breath for 5-10 seconds
o Wait 20-30 seconds between puffs and 2-5 mins between medications

Submersion Injury
- occurs when a client is underwater and unable to breath for a long time
- initial period of panic – loss of normal breathing pattern  holding breath, air hunger,
struggling to stay above water
- children from ages 1-4 have the highest rates of submersion injury
- life threatening complications –
- leading cause of accidental death in children
- risk:
o children less than 5
o minimal supervision around water
o inability to swim or overestimation of swimming abilities
o risk-taking behaviour
o substance abuse
o developmental or behavioural disorders
o seizure disorders
- Features:
o Length of submersion dictates severity of impairment
o More than five minutes the prognosis Is poor
o Onset up to 24 hours after incident
o Fatal event  due to respiratory and cardiac impairment
o Nonfatal event
 Mild impairment: breathing, paroxysmal coughing, fully alert
 Moderate: difficulty breathing and or disoriented but conscious
 Severe: not breathing or unconscious
o Hypoxia  hypoxic-ischemic brain injury
o May initially be cognitively intact but then suffer from hypoxia due to edema
o Cardiac arrest
o Apnea
o Hypothermia: organ failure – weak thready pulse, cold water  ventricular
dysrhythmia/PEA
o Aspiration  ARDS
 Reflex inspiratory efforts
 Coughing when water contacts lower respiratory tract

- Nursing:
o Obtaining length of submersion is critical
o First interventions:
 Focus on ABCs

 Rescue and immediately resuscitate  administer rescue breaths first,
then compressions  remove wet clothing
 Emergency dept interventions:
 Priority  airway management
 Monitor for signs of respiratory distress
 Administer oxygen
 Prepare for intubation and mechanical ventilation
 Monitor neurological status
 Initial and frequent neurological checks
 If gcs is less than 8 = medical emergency = intubate and ventilate

o Hypothermia:
 Monitor body temperature  slowly increase
 Passive rewarming  remove wet clothing and apply warm
blankets and dry clothing
 Active external rewarming
 Heating devices
 Warm water immersion
 Severe cases: active internal rewarming
 Warm IV fluids
 Warmed humidified oxygen

- Anticipatory guidance
o Properly fence swimming pools
o Supervise children closely around or near water
o Wear a properly fitted floatation device
o Teach children how to swim
o Avoid substance abuse while swimming or boating
o Children can drown in shallow water, even buckets and bathtubs

o Feeding intolerance

o Failure to pass meconium

Hyperbilirubinemia
- Bilirubin is a by-product of rbc destruction  mainly excreted in stool
- Excessive level of bilirubin in the bloodstream
- Immature livers for babies
- Jaundice
- Resolves within two weeks
- Highly toxic to neurons  risk for permanent brain damage
- Physiologic (developmental/newborn)
o Onset after 24 hours after birth
o Immature hepatic function and increased rbc lysis  increased bilirubin
o Prolonged onset in premature infants
- Pathologic
o Onset first 24 hours of life

DM type 1
- Chronic endocrine disorder
- Absolute deficiency in insulin production due to destruction of pancreatic beta cells
- Insulin required
- Typically seen in children and young adults
- Features:
o Thin
o Hyperglycemia
o Polyuria, polydipsia, polyphagia
o Weight loss
o Ketoacidosis (can be initial presentation)
o Breathing fast, deep
o Blurred vision, nocturnal enuresis
o Vaginal candidiasis
- Management:
o Consistent meal and snack with portions and compositions
o Frequent glucose monitoring
o Keep diary of glucose levels
o Lifelong insulin administration – requirements change with age – younger needs
more insulin
o Rotate insulin sites

o Avoid inflammation, cellulitis, infection
o Carry glucose tablets at all times
o Wear medical alert bracelet
o Monitor for hypoglycemia and dka
o Extra food prior to exercise, 10-15 grams of carbs for every 30-45 mins

o Ha1c every three months

o Test urine for ketones
- Hypoglycemia
o Blood glucose under 3.9
o Tx: oral glucose
o IM glucagon if unconscious
o Medical emergency

DM Type 2
- Not making enough insulin or the insulin isn’t working properly
- Pancreas is still making some insulin
- Insulin resistance- insulin receptors aren’t working, not enough of them, combination of
both
- Insulin cannot take glucose into cells = hyperglycemia
- Liver releases too much or not enough glucose
- Adipose tissue releases cytokines that cause inflammation and leads to DM
- Risks:
o Nonmodifiable: age above 45, family history, history of gestational diabetes
o Modifiable: obesity, inactivity, hyperlipidemia
o Cigarette smoking
- Manifestations:
o Gradual onset, asymptomatic hyperglycemia
o Polyuria, polyphagia, polydipsia
o Blurry vision
- Dx:
o Hgba1c above or equal to 6.5
o Fasting glucose above or equal to 7 mmol/L
 Nothing to eat or drink for 8 hours
o Random glucose above 11.1
o Oral glucose tolerance test for pregnant clients
- Complications:
o Hyperlipidemia, hypertension
o Macrovascular: atherosclerosis, CAD, stroke, TIA, peripheral artery disease
o Microvascular: peripheral neuropathy, sensory neuropathies, retinopathy,
nephropathy
o Delayed wound healing

o Recurrent infections = bacteria loves sugar
o Hyperosmolar hyperglycemia
- Nursing:
o How to use a glucometer
o Teach moderate activity, diet low in fat, high in fiber
o Weight loss
o Avoid alcohol (hypoglycemia risk)
o Oral antidiabetic medications or insulin therapy
o Treat hyperlipidemia – statins
o Monitor for hyperglycemia, HHS
o Proper foot care, inspect foot, report infection immediately, no open sandals, no
overlapping toes, protective shows, wash with lukewarm water daily
o Avoid heating pads
o Sick day management – they tend not to take insulin but they can become
hyperglycemic, continue as directed, monitor blood glucose frequently
o Routine eye exams
o Stress reduction techniques
o Prevent infections

Hemodialysis
- Mimics function of kidneys
- Removes waste and water
- Corrects electrolyte imbalances
- Restores acid base balance
- Requires:
o Access to the clints circulation
o Semipermeable membrane
o Dialysis solution
o Highly purified water
- Access:
o Three ways:
 Central venous double lumen catheter (temp)
 Arteriovenous fistula

 Arteriovenous graft – clients own system is not intact or fragile – uses
synthetic graft

- Procedure
o Access the clients circulation
o Administer heparin to the client through the line to prevent or reduce the risk of
clots
o Flows through membrane in one direction
o Dialysis solution flows in opposite direction
o Wastes are removed and electrolytes are corrected via diffusion
o Ultrafiltration removes excess water from the blood
o Blood returns to the client
- Nursing:
o Administer anticoagulants
o Monitor for bleeding
o Assess VS frequently predialysis and every 15 mins and at the end
o Hypotension may occur due to excess fluid removal
o Withhold antihypertensives prior to hemodialysis
o Obtain blood pressure on the arm opposite to the arm accessed
o Provide nutrition since they can eat before and during, especially if diabetic
- Complications:
o Air embolism – dyspnea and tachypnea, chest pain, anxiety, changes in
consciousness, hypotension, reduced o2 sat
 Immediately clamp tubing, place in Trendelenburg on left side,
administer high flow o2, notify HCP, medical emergency

o Disequilibrium syndrome – sudden change in composition of the ECF, solutes are
removed from the blood too fast, more common in new client
 Cerebral edema, n+v, restlessness, agitation, confusion, seizures, death
 Slow or stop dialysis, notify HCP, reduce environmental stimuli,
prepare to administer hypertonic saline or mannitol, prepare to
dialyze for shorter periods at reduced flow rates

o Infection/sepsis
o Cardiac arrhythmias (electrolyte imbalance)
o Catheter clotting

Clozapine
- Atypical antipsychotic medication to treat schizophrenia that doesn’t respond to other
treatment
- Associated with a risk for agranulocytosis (dangerously low WBC)
- WBC must have WBC and ANC in normal limits before starting clozapine and must be
continuously monitored
Crutch Paralysis

- Reversible condition caused by excessive and prolonged pressure on the radial nerve at
the axilla, due to crutches that are too long or leaning on top of the crutches when
ambulating. Manifestations = weakness, numbness, tingling of the arm, wrist, hand
- Teach client to support body weight on the hands and arms, not the axillae, make sure
there is a 1-2 inch space between the axilla and the crutch pad.
Sjogren Syndrome
- Chronic autoimmune disorder where moisture producing exocrine glands of the body
are attacked by the white blood cells. Most commonly affected glandsa re the salivary
and lacrimal glands, leading to dry eyes, dry mouth.
- Can lead to corneal ulcerations, dental caries, oral thrush.
- Can also have dry skin and rashes, chronic dry cough, vaginal dryness and painful
intercourse.
- Treatment: otc drops, prescription drops, wearing goggles, sugarless
gum/candy/artificial saliva, regular dental appts, lubricants for vaginal dryness. Use
humidifiers. Sip water.
- Take lukewarm showers with mild soap, avoid decongestants.
- Avoid coffee, alcohol, nicotine, acidic drinks.
Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis
- Severe form of Stevens Johnson syndrome – an immune mediated acute skin reaction
triggered by certain medications (lamotrigine, rifampin). Initial manifestations are
nonspecific and flu like (fever, fatigue), followed by a burn like rash and blistered lesions
that often involve the mucous membranes. Care is similar to that of a burn patient.
- Nursing:
o Apply sterile, moist dressings to areas of open skin due to shedding on the face,
trunk, palms
o Ensuring the causative medication is discontinued to prevent further progression
of symptoms
o Providing cotton blankets and maintaining a warm room temperature to prevent
hypothermia
o Administer lubricating eye drops or ointment to relieve dryness and prevent
corneal abrasion and keratoconjunctivitis

Surgical Site Infections
- Develop within days to weeks of a surgical procedure and are caused by introduction of
microorganisms to the open wound, most commonly from skin flora or contamination
of the surgical site. Clients with DM with a history of smoking have an increased risk of
SSI due to delayed wound healing from altered tissue perfusion and collagen synthesis
- Clinical manifestations:
o Fever, purulent discharge, induration, erythema.
o Paresthesia at the wound edges, dusky subq tissue, intense pain = necrotizing SSI
- Nursing:
o Administer broad spectrum abx

o Obtain a prescription for additional insulin if diabetic
o Prepare the client for urgent surgical debridement
o No abdominal binder

Beta Blockers
- Reduce cardiac workload, decrease myocardial oxygen demand by decreasing blood
pressure and decreasing HR
ACE inhibitors
- Alter the RAAS, preventing release of aldosterone
- Promote vasodilation and decrease BP
- Decrease ventricular remodeling
Loop Diuretics
- Increase urine output and fluid excretion
- Decrease BP and reduce pulmonary edema, increase o2 saturation, and decrease
cardiac preload
Autonomic Dysreflexia
- Acute unregulated sympathetic nervous system response to noxious stimuli (bladder
distension, fecal impaction, constrictive clothing) that causes severe lifethreatening
hypertension.
- Blood pressure remains high
- Manifestations:
o HTN, bradycardia, severe headache, profuse diaphoresis, nausea.
o Can lead to stroke, seizures, and MI.
o First elevate the head of the bed 45 degrees or higher to reduce BP
o Then Assess for noxious stimuli

Broca Aphasia
- Nonfluent aphasia resulting from damage to the frontal lobe. Clients can comprehend
speech but demonstrate speech difficulties. Speech pattern often consists of short,
limited phrases that make sense but display great effort and frequent omission of
smaller words
- Can become frustrated easily
- Are aware of impediment
Wernicke Aphasia
- Unaware of speech impairment

Bell Palsy
- Damage to facial nerve VII
- Manifestations = facial asymmetry, droop, inability to close the eyelid, impaired

- Chew on unaffected side to prevent choking
- Use a warm moist compress for pain relief (not cold)

Osteomalacia
- Reversible bone disorder that occurs when the body is unable to use calcium and
phosphorus for bone calcification due to a vitamin D deficiency. Management focuses
on increasing dietary intake of vitamin D, calcium (leafy green vegetables, dairy),
phosphorus (organ meats, nuts, fish, poultry, whole grains)
- Encourage moderate physical activity
- Use assistive devices to prevent injury
External Fixator
- Used to stabilize broken bones, metal pins are placed through the tissue into the bone
and connect to the frame outside the skin
- Monitor for signs of neurovascular compromise and pin site infection which can lead to
osteomyelitis
- Nursing:

o Assess pin sites for new, increased, purulent drainage and check for erythema,
warmth, pain, breakdown
o Assess for compartment syndrome
o Perform pin site care with a sterile cleaning solution (chlorhexidine, sterile
normal saline), gauze
o Monitoring pins and device for loosening and report to hcp
o Promote early mobilization, may begin walking with PT the day after surgery

Thyroid Storm
- Acute thyrotoxicosis
- Complication of hyperthyroidism that occurs when the thyroid gland releases large
amounts of thyroid hormones in response to stress
- Thyroid storm is characterized by severe hypermetabolic symptoms including altered
mentation, agitation, confusion, tachycardia, hyperthermia, cardiac dysthythmias.
- Without treatment, can lead to seizures and coma
Viral Rhinitis
- Highly infectious, mild upper respiratory infection most commonly caused by rhinovirus
- Self-limited and typically resolves within 1-2 weeks

- Risk factors: stress, allergies, spending time in crowds during winter months
- Symptoms:
o Cough, sore throat
o Headache
o Nasal congestion, runny nose, sneezing
o Low-grade fever
o Malaise
Ventricular Fibrillation
- Unorganized ventricular activity, nonperfusing rhythm, causes cardiac arrest
- Often results from degeneration of ventricular tachycardia
- Irregular, chaotic
- Waveforms of varying shape and amplitude
- Absence of organized QRS
- IMMEDIATELY check the pulse, start CPR, and prepare for defib
Hyperthyroidism
- Leads to increased metabolic rate
- Adhere to high calorie diet (4000-5000 calories a day)
- Consumption of approximately 6 full meals and snacks a day. These should be packed
with protein, carbs, and vitamins and minerals
- Avoid high-fiber foods as they may increase GI symptoms
- Avoid stimulants (caffeine, coffee, tea, soft drinks)
- Avoid spicy foods
Premature Ventricular Contractions
- Early contractions of the ventricles that originate from an ectopic focus and result in a
wide distorted QRS complex
- Usually harmless if occasional and can occur as a result of stimulants (caffeine, nicotine),
digoxin, hyperkalemia, hypokalemia, and stress
- Can lead to life threatening dysrhythmias like v-tach and v-fib. Assess physiological
response, including apical and radial pulse
Peritonsillar abscess
- Results from tonsillitis or pharyngitis
- Features: fever, muffled voice, trismus (inability to open the mouth), pooling of saliva,
and deviation of the uvula to one side
- Can lead to life threatening airway obstruction
- Immediately assess and monitor for signs of airway obstruction
Acute Prostatitis
- Inflammation of the prostate that is usually caused by a bacterial infection
- Symptoms: fever, rectogenital pain, burning, urinary hesitancy, and or urinary urgency

- Management includes antimicrobial (ciprofloxacin) and anti inflammatory medications.
Tamsulosin, alfuzosin, help relax the bladder and prostate. Suprapubic catheterization
may be necessary for urinary retention in severe cases of acute prostatitis
- Urethral catheterization is contraindicated due to the risk of additional pain and urethral
inflammation
- Teaching:
o Take stool softeners as prescribed to reduce straining
o Take sitz baths to help relieve symptoms
o Ejaculation may reduce discomfort
o Ensure adequate hydration

- Paralytic
