FOUNDATIONAL PEDIATRIC CARDIAC PRINCIPLES (Most Important)
Before diseases, students must understand:
✅ Children Compensate Differently Than Adults
Children maintain blood pressure until late.
Early deterioration signs:
🚨 Tachycardia (MOST sensitive sign)
🚨 Poor feeding (infants)
🚨 Irritability / lethargy
🚨 Delayed capillary refill
🚨 Cool extremities
Late signs:
🚨 Hypotension
🚨 Bradycardia (VERY ominous)
✅ Perfusion vs Oxygenation Logic
Many pediatric cardiac questions blend:
• Circulation problems
• Oxygenation problems
Must recognize:
Poor perfusion ≠ always hypotension
✅ Signs of Poor Cardiac Output in Children
Very testable.
• Tachycardia
• Fatigue
• Diaphoresis (especially with feeds)
• Poor weight gain
• Decreased urine output
• Cool skin
🫀 HIGH-YIELD PEDIATRIC CARDIAC CONDITIONS FOR REX-PN
Focus on common / exam-friendly / safety-relevant disorders.
✅ Congestive Heart Failure (VERY HIGH YIELD)
Most important pediatric cardiac topic.
Mechanism:
Heart cannot meet metabolic demand → Congestion + low output
Recognition:
⭐ Tachypnea
⭐ Poor feeding
⭐ Sweating with feeds
⭐ Failure to thrive
⭐ Hepatomegaly (if mentioned)
⭐ Edema (less dramatic than adults)
Exam logic:
Feeding intolerance = key clue
✅ Congenital Heart Defects (Conceptual Recognition Only)
REX-PN does not require memorizing dozens of defects.
Students must understand broad categories:
Acyanotic Defects (Left → Right Shunts)
Examples commonly referenced:
• Ventricular Septal Defect (VSD)
• Atrial Septal Defect (ASD)
• Patent Ductus Arteriosus (PDA)
Core concept:
⭐ Increased pulmonary blood flow
⭐ CHF risk patterns
Cyanotic Defects (Right → Left Shunts)
Example most commonly tested conceptually:
• Tetralogy of Fallot (TOF)
Core recognition:
⭐ Hypoxia / cyanosis
⭐ “Tet spells” (if referenced)
Exam focus:
Symptoms & safety, NOT anatomy memorization
✅ Patent Ductus Arteriosus (Common Exam Mention)
Conceptual understanding only.
• Murmur presence
• Pulmonary congestion risk
• Indomethacin / closure logic (basic)
✅ Pediatric Dysrhythmias (Simplified Recognition)
Know implications, not ECG mastery.
• Tachycardia significance
• Bradycardia = often hypoxia sign
• Perfusion consequences
Critical exam rule:
🚨 Bradycardia in child often = deterioration
✅ Cardiomyopathy (Basic Concept Only)
Recognition:
• Poor cardiac output
• Exercise intolerance
• Fatigue
No deep pathology needed.
✅ Rheumatic Fever / Carditis (Occasional Exam Topic)
Pattern recognition:
• Recent strep infection
• Inflammatory cardiac involvement
🫀 VERY IMPORTANT EXAM-STYLE PRESENTATIONS
Questions often describe symptom clusters, not diagnoses.
Students must recognize:
🚨 Signs of Cardiac Distress in Infants
• Poor feeding
• Sweating while feeding
• Tachypnea
• Poor weight gain
🚨 Signs of Reduced Perfusion
• Cool extremities
• Delayed cap refill
• Lethargy
• ↓ Urine output
🚨 Signs of Fluid Overload
• Tachypnea
• Hepatomegaly (if mentioned)
• Edema (mild)
💊 MEDICATION & SAFETY CONCEPTS (Very Testable)
✅ Diuretics
Used frequently in pediatric CHF.
Must know:
• Hypotension risk
• Electrolyte monitoring
• Hydration considerations
✅ Cardiac Medications (General Logic)
• Digoxin safety concepts (if included)
• Narrow therapeutic window idea
• Toxicity risk awareness (conceptual)
