Umbilical Cord Prolapse
-	2 umbilical arteries and one vein
-	Occurs when the umbilical cord slips out before baby is delivered
-	Everytime uterus contracts the blood supply and oxygen isn’t delivered to the baby
-	Risks:
o	Inadequate filling of the maternal pelvis by the fetus
o	Malpresentation (breech)
o	Low birth weight
o	Unengaged fetus
o	Long umbilical cord
o	Polyhydramnios
o	Rupture of membranes
-	Clinical features
o	Obstetric emergency
o	Visualized cord protruding from the vagina
o	Palpation of cord during vaginal examination
o	Sudden fetal heart rate changes in previously normal tracing
	Fetal bradycardia
	Moderate to severe variable decelerations
-	Nursing interventions
o	Call for help and notify health care provider
o	Prepare for c-section
o	Request a neonatal resuscitation team at birth
o	Wrap protruding umbilical cord with sterile towel and warm saline
o	Do not manipulate cord
o	Switch mom to knee-chest position to take babys head off umbilical cord

Stages of Labor:
-	Continuous uterine contractions causing progressive cervical dilation and effacement 
-	False labor vs true labor
o	False: Braxton hicks
	Irregular, without progression, come and go
	No cervical dilation, effacement, or fetal descent
	Activities relieve contractions
o	True:
	Regular, stronger, last longer, frequent
	Cervical dilations and effacement are progressive 
	Fetus is engaged and descends in the pelvis
-	4 stages
o	1: from start of regular contractions to complete dilation
o	Latent phase:
	0-5 cm cervical dilation
	Contractions are mild
o	Active:
	6 cm dilation and above
	Progress at faster rate
	May see increase in vaginal bleeding
o	Transition phase: 8-10 cm
o	Fetal station: how far babys head is down 
o	2: form complete cervical dilation to birth 
	Patients should start pushing
	Cardinal movements of labor start
•	Engagement
•	Neck flexion
•	Internal rotation
•	Neck extension
•	Restitution
•	External rotation
o	3: birth of baby to expulsion of placenta 
	Oxytocin is given 
o	Stage 4: 1-4 hours after birth
	Maternal physiological 
o	Nursing:
	Comfort and pain management
	Intrapartum monitoring 
	Assist with pushing techniques
	Cesarean birth 
	Fetal resuscitation at birth 
Spina Bifida (Myelomeningocele)
-	Defect in vertebrae
-	Vertebrae cannot fully close during fetal development
-	Nerves, spinal fluid protrude through opening and present through closed or open presentation 
-	Neural tube cannot fully form = defect and opening to allow the meninges to protrude
-	This is the most common neural tube defect and occurs in first 28 days of pregnancy
-	2 types: open or closed
o	Closed: spina bifida occulta
	Defect is not able to be seen to naked eye so the skin is intact on the back and you cannot see the defect but it remains under the surface
	Presentation: defect not visible, skin is intact. May see a dimple above anus, tuft of hair. May also see hemangioma, subcutaneous lipoma
o	Open/spina bifida cystica
	Protrusion is evident on assessment
	Myelo = spinal cord
	Meninges within the spine 
	All encompassed within a sack
	Presentation: sac-like protrusion
•	Spinal cord, nerves, fluid
•	Increased risk for infection 
-	Risk factors: 
o	Inadequate maternal folic acid intake
o	Prenatal vitamins 
o	Teratogenic medication during pregnancy including: methotrexate, antifolates, antiepileptics (trimethoprim, sulfasalazine, phenytoin, valproic acid) 
o	Maternal t2dm 
-	Complications: 
o	Can occur anywhere on the spine, anything below the defect will be affected (muscles and nerves)
o	Decreased sensation and relaxed muscles
o	Abnormal hip development due to muscle weakness
o	Tethered spinal cord – due to decreased flexibility 
o	Hydrocephalus – usually occurs after repair because of a disruption of the csf and not being able to drain the fluid effectively 
o	Neurogenic bladder.- muscles in bladder are weak, possible incontinence 
o	Increased risk of infection 
-	Nursing: 
o	Initial: latex precautions – use latex-free gloves due to increased number of surgical procedures so can develop an allergy
o	Preventing infections
o	Keep site moist with sterile, saline soaked nonadherent dressing
o	Large defects: also cover with plastic wrap to prevent heat loss
o	Only neurosurgeon removes dressing 
o	No diaper- can cause friction and tears, can also cause stool and urine to creep into the site 
o	Maintain prone or lateral position even when feeding to prevent them from laying on the defect 
o	Prophylactic IV abx 
o	Surgical repair within 1-2 days after birth because it is a life threatening defect
o	Long term:
	Teach parents how to measure head circumference (hydrocephalus)
	Maintain bladder function – clean intermittent catheterization
	Maintain bowel function and prevent constipation 
	Support musculoskeletal integrity – can they safely ambulate? Use a walker or ROM exercises to prevent contractures 
	Prevent recurrence (subsequent pregnancy) adequate folic acid intake 
Shoulder Dystocia
-	Difficult birth because of the shoulders
-	Obstetrical emergency – worst nightmares
-	When babys head crowns, rest of the head delivers, with traction we can deliver the shoulders and the body 
-	Fetal head emerges but the anterior shoulder becomes wedged behind the maternal symphysis pubis 
-	Not diagnosed until we try traction and find that the shoulder is stuck 
-	There are some signs it may be imminent: mom who had protracted labor, slow fetal descent, large baby, most common sign is when baby’s head emerges and baby is pulled back in toward the perineum (turtle sign) 
-	Scary thing is that baby remains stuck in the birth canal and cannot get enough oxygen or blood to its brain 
-	Risk factors:
o	Majority of patients have no risk factors at all
o	Big baby or big mom
o	Gestational diabetes mellitus 
o	Postterm pregnancy (> 42 weeks)
o	Maternal obesity and excessive weight gain during pregnancy 
o	Protracted labor course
	Unchanged fetal station for several hours 
	Prolonged second stage of labour (pushing longer than average)
-	Nursing:
o	L and d team work together to facilitate rapid vaginal birth 
o	First thing is to call for assistance – many hands will be needed
o	Patient should be positioned by lowering head of the bed and removing anything that might get in the way
o	Assist in maneuvers (mcroberts maneuver, suprapubic pressure) 
	Knees to chest and legs are abducted to allow the sacrum and spine to flatten and create more room for the baby
	Suprapubic pressure: pressure to moms pubic symphysis bone to dislodge the shoulder so it can deliver 
•	Not the same as fundal pressure 
o	This can make things worse
o	Someone needs to document the exact timing of events and call out reminders for how much time has passed 
o	The more time that passes the greater the risk of complications for both mom and baby 
-	Maternal complications 
o	Postpartum hemorrhage 
o	Perineal laceration 
-	Fetal complications
o	Not enough blood and o2 to babys head and can lead to asphyxia and death 
o	Can result in hypoxic ischemic brain injury 
	Eg cerebral palsy
o	Pressure that is applied ot the neck can lead to brachial plexus injuries (shoulder arm nerve injury causing weakness – usually temporary)
o	Fractured clavicle or humerus in order to facilitate its vaginal delivery 

Preterm Labor
-	Baby is born early before 37 weeks gestations
-	Regular painful uterine contractions causing progressive cervical dilation and effacement 
-	Very preterm: <32 weeks
-	Moderate 33 – 34 weeks
-	Late 34-37 weeks
-	Management differs depending on how preterm the baby is 
-	Risk: 
o	Prior preterm birth
o	Short interval pregnancy (less than 18 months)
	Haven’t really fully recovered from last pregnancy
o	Maternal infection
	Urinary or genital tract that can cause contractions
o	Maternal age (less than 17 or more than 35)
o	Uterine overdistension – very big babies or twins
o	Smoking- compromises blood flow
-	Prevents similarly to normal labor 
o	Painful uterine contractions
o	Lower back pain
o	New abdominal pain
-	Complications
o	Significant cause of morbidity and mortality among newborns
o	Low birth weight
o	Respiratory distress syndrome 
o	Patent ductus arteriosis 
	Blood is sent to the wrong place and extremities don’t get proper blood flow
o	Intraventricular hemorrhage
o	Necrotizing enterocolitis due to bacteria that enters the gut – abdominal distension, erythemia of the abdominal wall 
o	Preterm babies can suffer a lot of complications 
-	Nursing:
o	Magnesium sulfate at <32 gestation to reduce babys risk of cerebral palsy
	Fetal neuroprotection
o	Tocolysis
o	Corticosteroids (betamethasone) to accelerate fetal lung maturity 
o	Antibiotics (penicillin) to protect them from group b streptococcus 
o	Notify neonatal resuscitation team 
o	Continuous fetal monitoring 
o	Provide emotional support to client and partner
