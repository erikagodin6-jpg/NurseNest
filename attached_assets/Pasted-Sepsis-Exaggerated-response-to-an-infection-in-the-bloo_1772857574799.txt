Sepsis
-	Exaggerated response to an infection in the bloodstream originating from a local infection that results in life-threatening organ impairment 
-	Older adults are at an increased risk for sepsis due to normal age related decreases in immune and inflammatory response
-	Older adults typically do not develop typical signs of infection like fever and leukocytosis
-	Instead nurses must observe for and immediately report atypical indicators of infection (altered mental status, hypothermia, leukopenia) because early identification and intervention reduce mortality
-	Causes cardiovascular collapse or impairs the body’s ability to maintain normal metabolic and cellular processes. 
-	Manifestations:
o	Fever or hypothermia – fever occurs as a response to infection, low body temp occurs as shock worsens to metabolic alterations and inadequate tissue perfusion
o	Hypotension: systolic less than 90 or MAP less than 65 due to lactic acid accumulation and metabolic acidosis
o	Prolonged capillary refill: refill more than 3 or 4 indicates inadequate tissue perfusion as a result of altered peripheral circulation and hypotension
o	Tachycardia: compensates for decreased systemic vascular tone 
o	WBC: immature neutrophils (bands) more than 8% 
o	Clients may have decreased urine output due to decreased perfusion


ARDS
-	Refractory hypoxemia is the hallmark of acute respiratory distress
-	Has a high mortality rate
-	Can develop following a pulmonary insult (aspiration, pneumonia) or nonpulmonary insult (sepsis, multiple blood transfutions, trauma)
-	Insult triggers massive inflammatory response that causes the lung tissue to release inflammatory mediators (leukotrienes, proteases) that cause damage to the alveolar capillary membrane 
-	As a result the membrane becomes more permeable, and intravascular fluid then leaks into the alveolar space, resulting in noncardiogenic pulmonary edema
-	Lungs become stiff and noncompliant, which makes ventilation and oxygenation less than optimal and results in increased work of breathing, tachypnea, and alkalosis, atelectasis, refractory hypoxemia
-	Profound hypoxemia despite high concentrations of oxygen is a key sign of ARDs and is the most important assessment finding to report to the HCP 

Allopurinol
-	Prevention of gout
-	Gout is the buildup of uric acid deposited in the joints that causes pain and inflammation
-	Allopurinol helps to prevent uric acid deposits in the joints and the formation of uric acid kidney stones
-	Any rash in the client taking allopurinol, even if mild, should be reported to the HCP
-	Nurse should direct the patient to stop taking the medication immediately, schedule an appointment, and notify the HCP
-	Rash may be followed by more severe hypersensitivity reactions that can be fatal including stevens, johnson syndrome and toxic epidermal necrolysis
-	Avoid taking anticonvulsants (carbamazepine, phenytoin, lamotrigine) and sulfa antibiotics
-	 Take with a full glass of water – fluid intake is important to prevent renal stones

Pertussis
-	Whooping cough
-	Highly contagious, caused by Bordetella pertussis
-	Spread through close contact, coughing, sneezing
-	Once attached to client’s cilia in the upper respiratory tract, this bacterium releases a toxin that causes irritation and swelling
-	To prevent transmission, implement standard and droplet precautions 
-	Characterized by violent, spasmodic cough and a distinctive high-pitched whooping sound heard during inhalation 
-	Coughing may continue until the client expectorates a thick mucus plug or vomits (posttussive emesis)
-	Therefore the nurse should monitor for airway obstruction during coughing episodes, place clients on side if vomiting, and suction the airway and provide o2 as needed
-	Treatment consists of antibiotics and other supportive measures such as humidified oxygen and oral fluids 
-	small amounts of oral fluids help loosen mucus so it can be expectorated

Guaiac fecal occult blood test
-	used to assess for microscopic blood in the stool as a screening tool for colorectal cancer
-	Steps:
o	1. Assess for recent ingestion (within 3 days) of red meat or medications (vitamin C, aspirin, anticoagulants, iron, ibuprofen, corticosteroids) that may interfere with false test results
o	Obtain supplies (hemoccult test paper, wooden applicator, hemoccult developer) wash hands, and apply nonsterile gloves
o	Open the slides flap and use the wooden applicator to apply to separate stool samples to the boxes on the slide
o	Collect from 2 different areas of the specimen as some portions of the stool may not contain microscopic blood
o	Close the slide cover and allow the stool specimen to dry for 3-5 mins
o	Open the back of the slide and apply two drops of developing solution to the boxes on the slide
o	Assess the color within 30-60 seconds
o	Positive result turns the test blue, indicating presence of microscopic blood in the stool
o	Dispose of gloves
o	Document

Major Depressive Dissorder
-	5 or more symptoms must be present almost every day for at least 2 weeks and one symptom must be depressed mood or loss of interest or pleasure
-	Signs: SIG-E-CAPS
o	Sleep (increased or decreased)
o	Interest deficit (anhedonia)
o	Guilt (worthless, hopeless)
o	Energy deficit 
o	Concentration deficit
o	Appetite (increased or decreased)
o	Psychomotor retardation or agitation
o	Suicidality
Codeine 
-	Narcotic analgesic used for acute pain or cough suppressant
-	Depressing the cough reflex can cause accumulation of secretions in the presence of COPD leading to respiratory difficulty
-	Can also depress the respiratory center and effort, therefore they should not be given to clients with respiratory disease (asthma, COPD)
