Trisomy 21
-	Extra copy of 21st chromosome
-	Genetic disorder
-	Most common cause of intellectual disability
-	Diagnosed during pregnancy via amniocentesis, ultrasound, blood test, chorionic villus sampling
-	Physical assessment: (bluesss child)
o	Brushfield spots (white specks on iris)
o	Low set small ears
o	Upslanting palpebral fissures
o	Epicanthal folds 
o	Short neck with excess skin
o	Single palmar transverse crease
o	Sandal toe deformity (larger space after big toe)
-	Congenital heart defects 
-	Hearing and vision impairment
-	Intellectual disability
-	Lung problems or language problems
-	Developmental delay
-	Decreased muscle tone 
-	Nursing:
o	Swaddle infant 
o	Monitor respiratory function and sounds 
o	Suction nose with bulb syringe before feedings 
o	Rest periods during feeding  prone to respiratory issues 
o	Check ability to eat solid food
o	Ensuring the client is doing activities according to their developmental age
o	Increase fiber and fluids 
o	Monitor height and weight for trends
o	Family support 
o	Physical therapy, speech and language therapy, occupational therapy 
o	Routine vision and hearing testing 

Ectopic Pregnancy
-	Happens usually in the fallopian tubes
-	Egg grows outside the uterus, usually happens due to fallopian tube damage or PID
-	Features:
o	Unilateral lower abdominal pain
o	Abnormal vaginal bleeding or spotting
o	Delayed menstrual period
o	Positive pregnancy test
-	Ruptured ectopic pregnancy:
o	Unilateral abdominal pain
o	Hypotension (dizziness, tachycardia)
o	Referred shoulder pain 
o	Blood pools inside the belly = hemoperitoneum = referred shoulder pain 
o	If bleeding is severe enough they will develop hypovolemia 
	Medical emergency 
-	Treat: 
o	Methotrexate 
	Chemo drug that stops division of rapidly dividing cells 
	Urine is toxic up to 72 hours after administration
	Avoid analgesics stronger than acetaminophen to not mask other abdominal pain 
o	Surgery may be required
o	Rupture is a medical emergency

Fetal Alcohol Syndrome
-	Can cause brain damage and growth problems
-	Permanent physical, developmental, and cognitive abnormalities
-	Leading cause 
-	Features:
o	Alcohol withdrawal at birth
o	Jitteriness/irritability, increased tone and reflexes
o	Abnormal facial features
o	Microcephaly
o	Small eyes
o	Flat midface
o	Smooth philtrum 
o	Thin upper lip 
o	Weak suck reflex and difficulty feeding
o	Sensitive to sound as an infant
o	Hyperactive and inattentive
o	Poor social skills
o	Learning disabilities
o	Hearing impairment
o	Fine and gross motor delays
o	Poor handwriting and coordination
-	Nursing:
o	Daily weights
o	Intake and output
o	Monitor for hypoglycemia
o	Seizure precautions
o	Measure head circumference
o	Check suck and swallow reflex
o	Decrease environmental stimuli 
o	Small frequent feeds 
-	Teaching:
o	No amount of alcohol is safe during pregnancy
o	Early speech therapy
o	Breastfeeding is not contraindicated if alcohol intake has been discontinued

Hirschsprung Disease
-	Bowel cannot pass stool 
-	Absence of specialized nerve cells in distal large intestine
-	Impaired peristalsis
-	Inability of the interior sphincter to relax
-	Prevention of stool passage
-	Increased risk for life threatening bowel perforation or enterocolitis
-	Features:
o	Abdominal distension
o	Feeding intolerance
o	Bilious vomiting
o	Failure to pass meconium
o	Ribbon like stools
o	Poor weight gain or growth failure
o	Delayed meconium passage (over 48 hours)
o	Diagnose by barium enema or rectal biopsy 
-	Nursing:
o	Surgical is the only way
o	Check abdo girth
o	Prepare for surgery (removal of aganglionic portion of the bowel)
o	Maintain NPO
o	Administer IV fluids
o	Older children may require bowel cleanout prior to the procedure (enema) 
o	Postoperative:
	Assess for infection (pus, erythema)
	If a colostomy was performed, provide teaching for skin and stoma care
	Assess the stoma if a colostomy was performed should be moist and red in colour 

Hyperbilirubinemia
-	Bilirubin is a by-product of rbc destruction  mainly excreted in stool
-	Excessive level of bilirubin in the bloodstream
-	Immature livers for babies 
-	Jaundice 
-	Resolves within two weeks 
-	Highly toxic to neurons  risk for permanent brain damage
-	Physiologic (developmental/newborn)
o	Onset after 24 hours after birth 
o	Immature hepatic function and increased rbc lysis  increased bilirubin
o	Prolonged onset in premature infants
-	Pathologic
o	Onset first 24 hours of life 
o	Blood antigen incompatibility  hemolysis
	When baby is rh positive and mom is rh negative  triggers an immune response, mom attacks baby = hemolytic anemia = tested via coombs test 
	Risks: positive coombs test, excessive red blood cell breakdown, hemolytic disease, gestational age 35-36 weeks, genetic predisposition, cephalohematoma or significant bruising from delivery (bruises are broken capillaries = hemolysis)
o	Polycythemia
o	Excessive bruising 
-	Breastfeeding
o	Onset 3-4 days of life
o	Decreased oral intake/decreased milk supply = less stooling
-	Features
o	Jaundice  develops from head to toe, resolves from toe to head
o	Hepatosplenomegaly 
o	Edema in lower extremities
o	Decreased oral intake
o	Decreased stooling 
-	Complications: 
o	Bilirubin encephalopathy 	
	Bilirubin toxicity
•	Lethargy, decreased activity
•	Irritability, high pitched shrill cry = neurological issues 
•	Poor feeding 
•	Hypotonia – more floppy, loose
•	Temperature instability 
•	Can lead to seizures and a coma
•	Intervene asap due to damaged neurons 
•	Temporary and reversible 
	Kernicterus: permanent
•	Bilirubin induced neurological dysfunction
•	Brain cell necrosis causing chronic, permanent brain damage
•	Lethargy
•	Poor feeding
•	Hypotonia
•	Apnea (20 seconds, hypoxia)
•	Absent startle reflex (moro) 
•	High pitched, shrill cry
-	Nursing: 
o	Phototherapy
o	Put on sunglasses
o	Decrease risk of retinal damage
o	Fluorescent blue lights regulate temperature, do not add blankets
o	Do not clothe baby
o	Frequent temperature checks
o	Frequent turning and skin assessments 
o	Avoid lotions = risk of skin breakdown
o	Increased frequency of feedings
o	Avoid supplements (glucose water)
o	Monitor stooling pattern 
o	Daily weights 
o	Regular bilirubin levels 
o	Encourage skin to skin whenever theyre feeding to facilitate bonding 

Hyperemesis Gravidarum
-	Severe, persistent nausea and vomiting
-	Exact cause unknown
o	Pregnancy related increases in hormone levels may contribute 
-	Features:
o	Weight loss > 5% of prepregancy weight
o	Dehydration
	Dry mucous membranes
	Poor skin turgor
	Hypotension
	Tachycardia
	Increased urine specific gravity 
	Increased hematocrit (highly concentrated blood)
	Electrolyte imbalance 
•	Hypokalemia 
•	Metabolic alkalosis
	Ketonuria
-	Nursing
o	Alleviate vomiting
o	Fluid replacement
o	Correct imbalances
o	Iv fluids, antiemetics, thiamine
o	Check NPO for gut rest  slowly advance once asymptomatic 
o	Small frequent meals every 2-3 hours, avoid an empty stomach
o	Avoid greasy foods
o	Avoid strong odors
o	Sit upright after meals
o	Drink fluids in between meals
o	Ginger containing beverages
o	High carbohydrate foods like crackers
o	Cold foods, rather than hot 

Hypertension in Pregnancy
-	Types:
o	Chronic hypertension
	Elevated BP present before pregnancy
	Over 140 
	Persistent after postpartum period
	Can lead to superimposed preeclampsia 
o	Gestational hypertension
	High BP caused by pregnancy 
	New onset at over 20 weeks gestation 
	No other signs of end organ damage
	Resolves with delivery of the baby 
•	Features:
o	BP over 140 or 90 diastolic
o	2 separate measurements at least 4-6 hours apart
•	Complications:
o	Disease progression to preeclampsia 
o	Preeclampsia
o	HELLP syndrome
	Blood pressure is so severe that most organ systems fail 
	Low platelets and elevated liver function tests 
	Restricted fetal growth 
	Placental insufficiency or abruption 
	Maternal stroke, pulmonary edema, baby’s death 
-	Nursing:
o	Monitor BP and weight 
o	Teach client to monitor BP at home
o	Report symptoms 
o	Report vision changes
o	Review kick counts and report decreased movement
o	Anticipate induction of labor 
o	Test for proteinuria
o	Monitor intake and output
