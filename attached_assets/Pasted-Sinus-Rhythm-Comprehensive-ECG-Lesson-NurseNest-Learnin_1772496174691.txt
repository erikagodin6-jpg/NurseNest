1. Electrophysiologic Basis of Sinus Rhythm






4
The Sinoatrial (SA) Node
Sinus rhythm originates from the sinoatrial (SA) node, located in the high right atrium near the superior vena cava. The SA node is the heart’s primary pacemaker due to its highest intrinsic automaticity.
Intrinsic Firing Rate
SA node: 60–100 bpm
AV node: 40–60 bpm
Ventricular pacemakers: 20–40 bpm
Because the SA node fires fastest under normal conditions, it suppresses slower pacemakers via overdrive suppression.
Sequence of Depolarization
SA node generates impulse
Atrial depolarization → produces the P wave
Impulse reaches AV node (physiologic delay)
Conducts through Bundle of His
Travels down right and left bundle branches
Purkinje fibers depolarize ventricles → QRS complex
2. Definition of Sinus Rhythm
A rhythm is sinus in origin if all of the following criteria are met:
Criterion	Requirement
P wave morphology	Upright in lead II, uniform shape
P before QRS	Yes, every QRS preceded by a P
P:QRS ratio	1:1
PR interval	Constant (0.12–0.20 sec)
Rhythm	Regular
Pacemaker origin	SA node
Sinus rhythm refers to origin, not rate. A rhythm can be sinus and still be slow or fast.
3. ECG Characteristics of Normal Sinus Rhythm (NSR)






4
Diagnostic Criteria for NSR
Feature	Normal Range
Rate	60–100 bpm
Rhythm	Regular
PR interval	0.12–0.20 sec
QRS duration	< 0.12 sec
QT interval	Appropriate for rate
Morphology Details
P Wave
Rounded, upright in lead II
Consistent shape throughout strip
Duration < 0.12 sec
Amplitude < 2.5 mm
PR Interval
Represents atrial depolarization + AV nodal delay
Must remain constant if rhythm is sinus and stable.
QRS Complex
Narrow (< 120 ms)
Indicates normal ventricular conduction
4. Physiologic Regulation of Sinus Rhythm
Sinus rhythm is modulated by the autonomic nervous system.
Sympathetic Stimulation
Increases SA node firing rate
Increases conduction velocity
Shortens PR interval
Increases heart rate
Triggered by:
Exercise
Anxiety
Hypotension
Pain
Parasympathetic (Vagal) Stimulation
Decreases SA node firing
Slows AV conduction
Prolongs PR interval
Triggered by:
Sleep
Carotid massage
Valsalva maneuver
5. Rate Variability Within Sinus Rhythm
Sinus rhythm does not always mean exactly 75 bpm.
Normal physiologic variation includes:
• Mild respiratory variation (respiratory sinus arrhythmia)
• Rate increase with inspiration
• Rate decrease with expiration
• Transient rate changes with movement
These changes reflect intact autonomic balance and are often normal.
6. Distinguishing Sinus Rhythm from Other Rhythms
A rhythm may appear regular, but not be sinus in origin.
Key Differentiators
Atrial Ectopic Rhythm
Abnormal P wave morphology
May be inverted or peaked
Origin outside SA node
Junctional Rhythm
P waves absent, inverted, or after QRS
Rate typically 40–60 bpm
Atrial Fibrillation
No discrete P waves
Irregularly irregular rhythm
Atrial Flutter
Sawtooth flutter waves
Multiple atrial depolarizations per QRS
The presence of consistent upright P waves in lead II is the strongest indicator of sinus origin.
7. Sinus Rhythm in 12-Lead ECG
In a 12-lead ECG:
Sinus rhythm is confirmed if:
P waves upright in lead II
P waves upright in aVF
P waves negative in aVR
Uniform P morphology across beats
These findings confirm right atrial SA node origin.
8. Clinical Significance of Sinus Rhythm
Sinus rhythm indicates:
• Intact SA node automaticity
• Functional AV conduction
• Organized atrial depolarization
• Coordinated ventricular contraction
Hemodynamically, sinus rhythm supports:
Effective atrial kick
Optimal ventricular filling
Adequate cardiac output
Loss of sinus rhythm may reduce stroke volume by up to 20–30%, especially in older adults.
9. Systematic Interpretation Framework
When evaluating any rhythm:
Calculate rate
Assess rhythm regularity
Identify P waves
Confirm P before every QRS
Measure PR interval
Measure QRS width
Confirm sinus origin
If all criteria are satisfied → sinus rhythm confirmed.
10. Common Exam Pitfalls
• Assuming all regular rhythms are sinus
• Ignoring P wave morphology
• Missing inverted P waves
• Failing to check PR interval consistency
• Confusing sinus rhythm with junctional rhythm
Always analyze P waves first.
11. Clinical Case Example
A 65-year-old patient presents for routine ECG.
Findings:
Rate: 78 bpm
Rhythm: Regular
Upright P waves in lead II
PR interval: 0.16 sec
QRS: 0.08 sec
Interpretation: Normal sinus rhythm.
No intervention required.
12. Advanced Concepts
Sinus Rhythm and Cardiac Output
Cardiac output = Heart rate × Stroke volume
In sinus rhythm:
Atrial contraction contributes to ventricular filling
Known as “atrial kick”
Especially important in diastolic dysfunction
Loss of sinus rhythm (e.g., AFib) reduces preload and may worsen heart failure.
Sinus Node Dysfunction
Even if sinus rhythm is present intermittently, dysfunction may include:
Sinus pauses
Chronotropic incompetence
Bradycardia-tachycardia syndrome
Persistent abnormal sinus activity requires further evaluation.
13. Key Clinical Pearls
• Sinus rhythm refers to origin, not rate
• Upright P wave in lead II confirms sinus origin
• Every QRS must have a preceding P wave
• PR interval must be constant
• Sinus rhythm supports optimal cardiac output
• Always assess symptoms, not just ECG
14. Summary
Sinus rhythm represents the heart’s normal pacemaking mechanism, originating from the SA node and conducted through an intact atrioventricular system. It is defined by consistent P waves, 1:1 conduction, regular rhythm, and stable intervals.
Recognition of sinus rhythm is foundational to ECG interpretation. Every other arrhythmia is compared against this baseline pattern.
Mastering sinus rhythm identification ensures accurate detection of deviations and appropriate clinical decision-making.
