Tricyclic antidepressant effects and nursing
-	CV:
o	Dizziness, orthostatic hypotension, tachycardia
	Teach to change positions slowly, avoid hot baths, showers, avoid alcohol, sedatives
	Obtain baseline ECG 
	Monitor BP and pulse
o	Anticholinergic
	Dry mouth, constipation, urinary retention or difficult initiating a urinary stream, blurred vision
	Teach patient to drink sips of water, suck on ice chips, chew sugarless gum
	Teach client to consume a high-fiber diet and large amounts of fluids 
	Contact HCP if urinary problems persist or cause pain
	Report changes in vision to HCP, reassure client that blurred vision is temporary
o	Neuro: 
	Drowsy, confusion
•	Teach to use caution when driving or engaging in other activities requiring close concentration
•	Ask HCP if medication can be administered prior to bedtime rather than morning
o	Dermatological
	Photosensitivity
•	Teach client to wear sunscreen, sunglasses and protective clothing when outdoors 

Acute Pediatric Epiglottitis
-	Swelling of epiglottis and surrounding tissue
-	Usually caused by haemophilus influenza type B
-	Vaccination can prevent
-	Medical EMERGENCY – swelling obstructs the airway
-	Hits fast and hard – kids can act fine then have a sudden onset of symptoms
-	Features:
o	Fever, sore throat, extreme agitation, restlessness, anxiety
o	Airway obstruction
o	Tripod position- opens airway and helps airflow 
o	4 Ds: drooling (cannot swallow secretions), muffled voice (dysphonia), dysphagia, distress (respiratory), tachypnea, retractions, stridor
o	Accessory muscle usage, nasal flaring
-	Interventions: maintain airway
o	Prepare for intubation
o	Tracheostomy if intubation is unsuccessful 
o	Initiate cardiac monitoring and pulse oximetry
o	Avoid supine and allow the child to remain in tripod
o	Administer iv antibiotics and fluids, keep NPO
o	Administer oxygen via mask or blow by to avoid further agitation
o	Avoid placing foreign objects in mouth, no throat swab, no tongue temp probe due to risk of laryngeal spasm- can also be life threatening 
Asthma
-	Obstructive lung disease due to hyperactive airways, chronic inflammation, hypersecretion of mucus
-	Lining becomes thickened 
-	Airways are very sensitive and hyperreactive 
-	Bronchioles contract and spasm leading to obstruction
-	Exacerbating factors:
o	Allergens
o	Medications: beta blockers, aspirin
o	Environmenta
o	Exercise
o	Stress, reflux
-	Symptoms: mild classic symptoms which can be managed at home
o	Chest tightness, difficulty breathing, wheezing, often audible
o	Cough – dry or productive
o	Severe symptoms: increased RR, retractions, accessory muscle usage 
o	Hypoxemia
o	Wheezing diminished or absent lung sounds – air is not moving through the airways and this is an emergency
-	Diagnosed: spirometry – not a good measure of the obstruction in an acute exacerbation
o	X-ray: hyperinflation, flattened diaphragm
o	Reduced peak expiratory flow rate
o	Arterial blood gas – increase in CO2 
	Low pa02 and high paco2 indicates respiratory failure
-	Nursing:
o	High fowler
o	Supplemental o2 if o2 is not over 90
o	Bronchodilators
o	Short acting beta 2 agonist (albuterol)
o	Anticholinergic: ipratropium: opens bronchioles and dries up mucus 
o	Corticosteroids to decrease inflammation 
o	Auscultate before and after treatment 
o	Monitor vital signs, pulse ox, and peak flow meter
o	Peak flow meter:
	Helps measure air they can push out of their lungs 
	Green is good zone 
	Yellow = mild symptoms
	Red: severe obstruction – indicates acute exacerbation 
o	Develop a maintenance asthma action plan 
	Corticosteroid inhalers
	Long acting beta agonist inhalers 
	Use of bronchodilator before steroid inhaler 
	SABA can be used as needed 
Retinoblastoma
-	Retinal tumor that can be unilateral or bilateral
-	Most common eye malignancy
-	Diagnosed in kids less than two and is first recognied when parents report that the pupil has a white glow (leukocoria)
-	Light reflecting off the tumor causes the pupil to look white and can be visualized when taking a photograph with flash
-	Treatment: depends on disease severity and may include radiation therapy or enucleation (removal of the eye) and fitting for a prosthesis
-	Siblings should undergo regular ocular screening because some forms are hereditary
