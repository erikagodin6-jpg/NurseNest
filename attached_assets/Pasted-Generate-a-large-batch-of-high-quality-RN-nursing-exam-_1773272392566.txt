EXAMPLE QUESTION STRUCTURE

Question:
A nurse is caring for a client with heart failure who reports increasing dyspnea when lying flat and a weight gain of 2.5 kg in 3 days. Which assessment finding is most concerning?

A Dry mucous membranes
B Bilateral crackles at the lung bases
C Decreased bowel sounds
D Warm flushed skin

Correct Answer: B

Correct Answer Explanation:
Bilateral crackles suggest fluid accumulation in the lungs, which is concerning for worsening pulmonary congestion in heart failure. Orthopnea and rapid weight gain further support fluid overload.

Incorrect Options:
A: Dry mucous membranes suggest dehydration rather than fluid overload.
C: Decreased bowel sounds are not the most urgent indicator in this heart failure scenario.
D: Warm flushed skin does not specifically indicate decompensated heart failure.

Clinical Takeaway:
Orthopnea, rapid weight gain, and crackles strongly suggest worsening fluid overload in heart failure.

Exam Pearl:
In heart failure, sudden weight gain is often one of the earliest signs of fluid retention.

SECOND EXAMPLE

Question:
A nurse reviews the telemetry strip of a client reporting palpitations and dizziness. Which intervention is the priority if the strip shows a narrow-complex tachycardia and the client is unstable?

A Encourage oral fluids
B Prepare for synchronized cardioversion
C Reassess in 30 minutes
D Administer subcutaneous insulin

Correct Answer: B

Correct Answer Explanation:
An unstable client with symptomatic narrow-complex tachycardia requires immediate synchronized cardioversion according to ACLS principles.

Incorrect Options:
A: Oral fluids do not address unstable tachyarrhythmia.
C: Waiting 30 minutes is unsafe in an unstable client.
D: Insulin is unrelated to the rhythm emergency.

Clinical Takeaway:
Unstable tachyarrhythmias require immediate intervention, often synchronized cardioversion.

Exam Pearl:
Always assess stability first when interpreting tachyarrhythmias.
