Trigeminal Neuralgia
- Sudden, sharp pain along the distribution of the trigeminal nerve
- Symptoms are usually unilateral and primarily in the maxillary and mandibular branches.
Clients may experience chronic pain with periods of less severe pain or cluster attacks of
pain between long periods without pain. Triggers can include washing the face, chewing
food, brushing teeth, yawning, or talking. Pain is severe, intense, burning, or electric
shock-like. The primary intervention for trigeminal neuralgia is consistent pain control
with medications and lifestyle changes. The drug of choice is carbamazepine. It is a
seizure medication but it is highly effective for neuropathic pain. Associated with
agranulocytosis and infection risk. Advise clients to report any fever or sort throat.
- Behavioural interventions: oral care, using a soft bristled toothbrush, use lukewarm
water, room should be kept at an even or moderate temp, have a soft diet, avoid
rubbing or facial massage, use cotton pads to wash the face, avoid foods that are
difficult to chew. Chew on the unaffected side of the mouth.

Rh alloimmunization
- Occurs when a pregnant client with Rh negative blood type is exposed to rh positive
fetal blood via trauma or birth
- The maternal immune system can produce antibodies because it does not recognize the
rh antigen on the fetal rbcs
- Prevents the maternal antibody formation by suppressing the maternal immune
response to rh positive fetal blood. During the postpartum period clients with rh
negative blood require an additional dose of rh immune globulin if the newborn has rh

Vancomycin infusion reaction
- Can occur with rapid IV vancomycin administration characterized by flushing, erythema,
pruritis, typically on the face, neck, chest. Muscle pain, spasms, dyspnea, hypotension
may also occur. VIR is usually a rate related infusion reaction and not an allergic
reaction. It can be reduced by infusing vancomycin over a minimum of 60 minutes. It
can be difficult to differentiate severe VIR from anaphylaxis as flushing and hypotension
can occur in both conditions. Hives, angioedema (lip swelling), wheezing, and
respiratory distress are more suggestive of anaphylaxis.
- Stop infusion immediately, treat with IM epinephrine.

ALS
- Also known as lou Gehrig disease and is a debilitating progressive neurodegenerative
disease with no cure. Clients develop fatigue and muscle weakness that progresses to

- Riluzole is the only medication approved for als treatment. Riluzole is a glutamate
antagonist thought to slow neuron degeneration by decreasing the production and
activity of the neurotransmitter glutamate in the brain and spinal cord and may slow
disease progression and prolong survival by 3-6 months.

Impetigo
- Highly contagious bacterial skin infection, most commonly occurring in children during
hot, humid weather. Characterized by itchy, burning, red pustules that rupture to form
honey coloured crusts. When treated with antibiotic ointment or oral antibiotics, lesions
are no longer contagious after 24-48 hours and typically heal within a week. Usually
resolves within 2-3 weeks but remains highly contagious until lesions heal
- To care for: perform handwashing before and after touching, isolate infected persons
clothing and linens, wash in hot water, keep infected persons fingernails short and
clean, avoid close contact with others for 24-48 hours after initiation of abx therapy,
keeping the infected area covered with gauze when in contact with others. Clean with a
mild antibacterial soap and avoid alcohol as it is irritative.
Acne Vulgaris
- Skin disorder characterized by obstructed sebaceous glands which form comedones
(blackheads and whiteheads). Bacteria consume and metabolize the obstructed sebum
and this causes inflammation, pustules, papules, and nodules.
- Treatment: topical and oral medications such as tretinoin, benzoyl peroxide,
isotretinoin, and oral contraceptives. Antibacterial soaps should be avoided.
- Use noncomedogenic skin care products
- Maintain a healthy lifestyle
- Refrain from squeezing, picking, and vigorously scrubbing lesions
Scarlet Fever
- Complication of group a streptococcal infection commonly seen in early childhood and is
characterized by a red rash. The rash begins on the neck and chest and spreads to the
extremities, resembles a bad sunburn, blanches with pressure, and has find bumps like

sandpaper. Additional manifestations include pharyngitis, fever, swollen anterior
cervical lymph nodes
- Clinical presentation is not diagnostic so a rapid streptococcal antigen test will confirm
etiology
- Swabbing the pharynx and tonsils provides test results in minutes
Splenic Sequestration Crisis
- Complication of painful episodes that occurs when a large number of sickle shaped cells
get trapped in the spleen causing splenomegaly
- This is life threatening because pooling in the spleen can lead to severe hypovolemia
and shock
- Classic assessment finding is rapidly enlarging spleen and hypotension
Varicella
- Lesions begin as a maculopapular rash, progress to weeping vesicular lesions, and
typically crust over within approx. one week. The lesions are often pruritic or painful,
and clients frequently have an accompanying fever
- Treatment includes cool oatmeal baths, calamine lotion, antihistamines,
diphenhydramine, Tylenol for pain or fever
- Clients whoa re immunocompromised are at risk for severe varicella and require
aggressive therapy including an antiviral like acyclovir. This should be continued until all
lesions have crusted over
- Varicella is airborne and contact and clients are most infectious in the days leading up to
the rash and continue to be infectious until the entire rash reaches the crusting stage
- If a client is immunocompromised they should not receive live attenuated vaccines or if
they have had a previous infection from varicella as they already have immunity
Erythema Infectiosum (Fifths)
- Viral illness caused by human parvovirus b10 which affects mainly school aged children.
Initial symptoms are nonspecific and include fever, headache, diarrhea, lethargy, and
itching. The client develops a distinctive red, slapped face rash that appears on the
cheeks, once the rash appears, the client is no longer contagious. Following
development of the rash on the race, a maculopapular rash develops on the trunk,
extremities, hands and feet.
- Clients typically recover in 7-10 days but the rash may remain for 3 weeks. Anti-
inflammatory and antipyretic agents may be used to relieve fever and joint pain.
Hemophilia A and B
- Inheritance is x-linked recessive
- Features: delayed and prolonged bleeding after mild trauma, hemarthrosis,
intramuscular hematomas, GI or GU bleeding, intracranial hemorrhage
- Complications: hemophilic arthroplasty
- Lab findings: increased aPTT, normal platelet count and PT, absent or decreased factor 8
(hemophilia A) or factor 9 (hemophilia B) activity

- Treatment: factor replacement and desmopressin for mild hemophilia A
- Cover corners of furniture with soft padding, have the child wear a medic alert bracelet,
have ice packs readily available
- Avoid contact sports, recognize signs of bleeding including tingling in the joints.
Supportive management includes rest, ice, compression, elevation, and pain control
Shaken Baby Syndrome
- Impact of shakin causes bleeding within the brain or the eyes
- Not uncommon for the diagnosis of SBS to be missed as the clinical findings are often
vague and nonspecific – vomiting, irritability, lethargy, inability to suck or eat, seizures,
and inconsolable crying
- No external signs of trauma except for occasional small bruises on the chest or upper
arms where the child was held during the shaking episode
- Most common reasons that caregivers seek medical attention for children with sbs are
breathing difficulty, apnea, seizures, and lifelessness. Caregivers typically do not offer a
history of trauma nor do they report the episodes of shaking. By contrast, children who
have sustained unintentional head injury are typically brought for treatment out of
concern by their caregivers even when the children are asymptomatic
Phenylketonuria
- Autosomal recessive mutation in gene for phenylalanine hydroxylase
- Impaired metabolism of phenylalanine to tyrosine – leads to increased phenylalanine
causes neurological injury
- Features:
o Neuro: microcephaly, developmental delay, seizures, severe intellectual
disability
o Nonneurologic: musty body odor, hypopigmentation, eczema
- Diagnosis: increased phenylalanine levels – universal newborn screening, serum amino
acid analysis
- Treatment: phenylalanine restricted diet – avoidance of protein rich foods, dietary
supplemental tyrosine
- Prognosis: preventable disease manifestations with early treatment
- Delayed diagnosis treatment = irreversible neurologic injury
- Restrict meat, eggs, milk

Operative Vaginal Birth

- Uses a vacuum extractor or forceps to shorten the pushing stage of labor
- Indications may be maternal or fetal
- In a forceps assited birth the HCP gently applies the blades to the sides of the fetal head

Anencephaly
- Severe neural tube defect that results in little to no brain tissue or skull formation in
utero. Most are stillborn but those born alive are not compatible with life. Comfort care
for the newborn and emotional support for the family is priority. Drying, bundling, and
placing the newborn skin to skin provides warmth, and administering oxygen may
decrease discomfort to the newborn. Allow the family to hold the newborn.
- Risks: low folic acid intake, methotrexate, antiepileptics, diabetes mellitus, prior
pregnancy with neural tube defect

Molar pregnancy
- Type of gestational trophoblastic disease that results from abnormal fertilization. Causes
rapidly growing trophoblastic tissue that is initially benign but may lead to gestational
trophoblastic neoplasia
- If it continues to grow or metastasize after evacuation of a molar pregnancy, levels of
hcg will continue to increase. Nurse should emphasize the importance of avoiding
pregnancy during follow up care to allow health care provider to monitor rising hcg
levels which may indicate malignant GTN. Weekly monitoring of hcg levels is required at
first followed by continued monitoring for 6-12 months post partum.
- Uterine evacuation is the recommended treatment option
Placenta Accreta

- Condition of abnormal placental adherence in which the placenta implants directly in
the myometrium rather than the endometrium
- Prenatal ultrasound usually detects placenta accrete although detection can rarely occur
after birth when the placenta is adherent
- A cesarean birth before term gestation at a facility with adequate resources is
recommended for clients with placenta accrete
- The major complication of placenta accrete is life threatening hemorrhage which occurs
during attempted placental separation
- At least two large bore IVs and a blood type and crossmatch are priority concerns in
case blood transfusions are necessary
- Risk factor is previous c-section
- A hysterectomy may be necessary to reduce blood loss

Newborns who are Large for Gestational Age
- Weight must be in or over the 90 th percentile and is commonly over 8lbs 13oz.
- Risk factors include maternal gestational diabetes, excessive gestational weight gain or
elevated prepregnancy BMI, history of a prior newborn who was LGA, postterm
gestation, and genetics
- Nursing care:
o Documenting age assessment, weight, length, and head circumference to
identify newborns who are LGA
o Assessing the newborn for birth related injuries (cephalohematoma, clavicular
fracture) and reviewing the birth record to determine if an operative vaginal
birth occurred
o Monitoring capillary blood glucose via heel stick to assess for hypoglycemia and
notifying the HCP when a BG is less than 2.2
o Assist the mother to feed the newborn soon after birth and every 2-3 hours
thereafter to prevent hypoglycemia

Trisomy 18 (Edwards Syndrome)
- Life threatening chromosomal abnormality that affects multiple organ systems. Many
fetuses affected will die in utero. Of the newborns that survice birth, half will die in the
first week of life and most do not make it to the first birthday
- Before withdrawal of ventilator support, it is appropriate for the nurse to request a
collaborative meeting between the health care provider and the palliative care team to
help the parents understand their child’s condition as well as make decisions about
interventions and the potential need for end-of-life care
- There is no cure

Right Occiput Posterior position
- Common fetal malposition that occurs when the fetal occiput rotates and faces the
maternal posterior or sacrum. Can cause increased back pain during labor. Fetuses in OP
position during first stage of labor often spontaneously rotate to occiput anterior. The

nurse can apply counterpressure to the clients sacrum during contractions to help
alleviate back pain associated with OP fetal positioning. Firm, continuous pressure is
applied with a closed fist, heel of the hand, or other firm object like a tennis ball
- Clients should not be placed in the supine position
- Clients should be urged to ambulate and change positions frequently to increase
comfort and promote fetal rotation and descent
Preterm Labor
- Progressive cervical dilation and or effacement resulting from uterine contractions
before term gestation. The nurse should anticipate the following interventions before
34 weeks gestation
o Administer antenatal glucocorticoids (betamethasone, dexamethasone) to
stimulate fetal lung maturation and promote surfactant development
o Administer antibiotics to prevent gbs
o Initiate an iv magnesium sulfate infusion for fetal neuroprotection if at &lt;32
weeks gestation
o Giving tocolytic medications like nifedipine or indomethacin to suppress uterine
activity which allows antenatal glucocorticoids time to have a therapeutic effect
o Monitor lab results for strep b
o Clients should be placed on continuous fetal monitoring

Acrocyanosis
- Peripheral cyanosis of the hands and feet is a benign finding during a newborns
transition to extrauterine life. It is especially common during the first 24 hours of life or
in the first week if the newborn is cold. Manifestations include a bluish discolouration of
the hands and feet and sometimes the skin around the mouth
- Results from poor perfusion to the periphery of the body, an initial mechanism to
reduce heat loss and stabilize temperature. Initial nursing management includes
promoting thermoregulation by placing the newborn skin to skin with the parent or
under a radiant warmer and assessing axillary temperature
Neonatal Absinence Syndrome
- Potentially life-threatening condition affecting newborns exposed in utero to
physiologically addictive substances. NAS manifests hours to days after birth as the
newborns blood level of the substance decreases after separation from maternal
circulation.
- Manifestations:
o Irritability, inconsolability, high pitched shrill cry, neuromuscular irritability,
tremors, hypertonia

- Nursing
o Environmental stimuli should be minimized, and the nurse should swaddle the
newborn in a flexed position and gently rock the newborn to provide comfort
and decrease irritability

o Promote non-nutritive sucking
o Regurgitation is common in newborns with NAS.
o Keep the baby in a quiet, dimly lit environment and organize care to minimize
stimulation and prevent seizure activity

Peritoneal Dialysis
- Mimics the function of the kidneys by using the abdominal lining as a semipermeable
membrane to filter blood and remove excess fluid, electrolytes, and waste products. PD
involves the surgical placement of a catheter into the peritoneal cavity, through which
dialysiate is infused and drained
- Clients are at a risk for peritonitis, a life-threatening inflammation of the peritoneum
that occurs when bacteria (staph aureus) enter the sterile peritoneal cavity.
- Manifestations:
o Cloudy effluent – life threatening
o Severe abdominal pain
o Rigid or board like abdomen
o Possible signs of infection (fever, tachycardia, leukocytosis)
- Dialysate drainage amount should be greater than that of the dialysate infused due to
the removal of waste products and excess fluid from the body
Carbon Monoxide
- Colorless, odorless gas produced by burning fuel in a poorly ventilated setting. CO
toxicity is most often associated with smoke inhalation from structure fires but is also
generated by furnaces/water heaters fueled by natural gas or oil, coal or wood stoves,
fireplaces, and engine exhaust
- Clients with CO toxicity often have nonspecific symptoms (headache, nausea, dizziness)
and the diagnosis can be missed.
- It is important to initiate appropriate emergency care and prevent neurologic
impairment
- Nursing:
o Ask about similar symptoms in other family members or an illness in an indoor
pet that developed at the same time
o Fuel burning heating/cooking appliances

Dumping syndrome
- Removes part of the stomach and shortens the upper GI tract
- After a partial gastrectomy, many clients experience dumping syndrome which occurs
when rapid gastric emptying causes a fluid shift into the small intestine
- Symptoms include dizziness, swearing, nausea, abdominal cramping, tachycardia, and
darrhea shortly after meal consumption
- To reduce the occurrence of symptoms, clients should recline after eating meals to slow
the emptying of gastric contents. An upright or seated position increases the force of
gravity, which increases the rate of gastric emptying

Polycythemia Vera
- Too many RBCs (and often WBC and platelets) are produced, causing increased blood
viscosity, venous stasis, and increased risk for thrombus formation.
- Nursing interventions:
o Encourage fluid intake to reduce viscosity of the blood
o Administer low-dose aspirin to reduce thrombus formation and alleviate
symptoms of sluggish blood flow (itching)
o Request a prescription for antihistamine cream to relieve itching. Pruritis is a
common occurrence in clients with PV, often after bathing. Clients should bathe
with cool water and pat not rub themselves dry with a towel to avoid histamine
release
o Implementing measures to prevent thrombus formation (compression stockings,
elevating legs while sitting)
o Prepare for therapeutic phlebotomy to reduce blood volume and viscosity

Atrial Pacing
- The expected ecg strip finding would be a single pacemaker stripe before the p wave
indicating electrical stimulation of the atria by a pacemaker lead.
Emergent phase of burn injuries
- First 24-72 hours, burn injuries cause increased capillary permeability, fluid electrolyte
shidts, and decreased intravascular volume.

o nephron is the functional cell of the kidney whos primary job is to remove waste

o requires adequate blood flow to bring waste products and excess fluid to the

o gfr can be calculated by egfr

 hypertension

 anorexia, malaise, weakness

AKI
- abrupt decline in kidney functions that results in impaired elimination of wastes and
leads to electrolyte imbalances
- Prerenal – decreased blood flow to kidneys due to dehydration or blood loss and can
lead to tissue impairment
- Intrarenal – damage to the kidneys  glomerulonephritis or tubular necrosis

- Postrenal – backflow of urine into the kidneys  obstruction will cause a decrease in
blood flow  usually BPH or ureteral stones
- Stages:
o First: initial rise in serum BUN and creatinine which are waste products
eliminated by the kidneys
o Second: oliguric and anuric phase – urine output is less than 500 ml in 24,
progressive increase in BUN, creatinine and urea = pruritis, anorexia, nausea,
vomiting. Metabolic acidosis = kussmaul respirations
 Hypervolumia, hypertension, pulmonary edema and increase in
electrolyte levels like hyperkalemia and hyperphosphatemia
o Last phase: diuretic phase – glomerular function returns but tubules are
damaged so urine cant be concentrated = fluid and electrolyte losses =
hypovolemia and hypokalemia, BUN and creatinine decrease

- Interventions:
o Correct underlying cause
o Avoid nephrotoxic medications
o Strict intake and output
o Important to know whether they are experience oliguric vs diuretic phase
o Potassium restricted diet in oliguric phase
o Daily weights
o Monitor renal function
o Hemodialysis if appropriate

- Patho:

Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome
- Progressive respiratory failure after a lung injury or MODS (sepsis)
- Alveolar capillary membrane becomes more permeable to fluids which impairs gas
exchange, decreased lung compliance
- High mortality rate
- Cytokine storm: acute systemic event triggers a rapid release of inflammatory cells 
target the alveoli and pulmonary capillary system
- Causes: severe swelling of and destruction of the alveoli and capillaries
- Surfactant destruction
- Fluid accumulation into the lungs and surrounding tissue
- Risks: bacterial or viral pneumonia, aspiration of gastric contents, sepsis, acute
pancreatitis, submersion injury, severe burns and trauma
- Features:
o SOB, refractory hypoxemia (no matter how much o2 I give, pulse ox drops)
o Compensatory tachypnea
o Intercostal retractions, accessory muscle use
o Anxiety
o Tachycardia
o Fever if infection
o Crackles on auscultation
- Diagnosis: ABG = low po2 and resp acidosis
o CXR, diffuse infiltrates (fluid everywhere)
- Nursing
o Administer oxygen
o Place in high fowlers
o Prepare for ventilation with PEEP to open up alveoli
o Restrict fluids as appropriate
o Provide and monitor sedation and paralytic support
o Provide enteral nutritional support
o May have to prone
o Anticipate ECMO

o Support family (high mortality)
o Once intubated  high risk for clots so use compression devices and
anticoagulants
o Anticipate PEEP and prone
o Monitor for pneumothorax, hypotension, delirium, stress ulcers in stomach, AKI
o Check ABG frequently
o Monitor hourly intake and output and report if under 30ml an hour
o Temporarily increase o2 to 100% before suctioning

 Exudative: week one, hyaline membranes, acute inflammation
 Proliferative: week 2: interstitial inflammation, proliferation of
pneumocytes, endothelial cells and fibroblasts
 Fibrotic: does not occur in all patients

Sickle Cell Disease
- Autosomal recessive (both parents have to have mutation)
- Alterations in hemoglobin due to deoxygenation or dehydration
- RBC forced into stiffened and elongated crescents and clump and occlude (especially
hands and feet or organs)
- Features:
o Vasoocclusive: painful distal ischemia (hands and feet)
o Sequestration: pooling of blood in liver and spleen
o Hyperhemolytic crisis: marked anemia due to massive destruction of RBCs
- Assessment findings:
o Tachycardia: due to hypoxemia, pain, infection
o Leukocytosis: occurs with inflammation of tissues injured by ischemia
o Anemia: rbc destruction
o Increased reticulocytes – indicative of RBC destruction
- Nursing
o Diet – high protein, high calorie in addition to multivitamin without iron (due to
rbcs breaking down and releasing iron)
o Folic acid – stimulation of erythropoiesis  needed due to increased hemolysis
o Hydration: to reduce blood viscosity
o Infection prevention: vaccination
o Oxygenation: prevent pulmonary complications
o Pain control
o Tissue perfusion
 Monitor hemoglobin levels and administer blood products as indicated
