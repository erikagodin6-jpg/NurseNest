RATIONALE LENGTH STANDARD

The rationale is still too short. Expand it significantly.

For every question, generate or display rationale in this structure:

A. Correct Answer
- clearly state the correct answer

B. Why this is correct
- minimum 4 to 8 sentences when clinically appropriate
- explain the nursing priority
- explain the pathophysiology or clinical reasoning
- explain what the nurse is really assessing for
- explain exam logic when relevant
- make it educational, not just factual

C. Why the other answers are incorrect
- provide a separate explanation for A, B, C, D as appropriate
- for incorrect options, explain:
  - why it is less priority
  - why it may be partially true but not the best answer
  - why timing/frequency/clinical focus is wrong
- each incorrect option should get its own short paragraph or bullet-style explanation

D. Clinical pearl / exam tip
- one concise but useful takeaway

E. Related lesson
- link to the most appropriate lesson

EXAMPLE QUALITY TARGET

For a question like:
What is the priority assessment post-op after AAA repair?

The rationale should be more like:
- after abdominal aortic aneurysm repair, the priority is assessing graft patency, distal perfusion, renal perfusion, and hemodynamic stability
- distal pulses help confirm that blood flow is reaching the lower extremities beyond the surgical repair
- hourly urine output is a key indicator of renal perfusion and overall circulating volume
- decreased urine output may signal hypoperfusion, shock, renal compromise, or post-operative complications
- bowel sounds matter, but they are not the top priority immediately after major vascular surgery
- early ambulation is not the immediate first concern until the patient is stable
- measuring abdominal girth once every 24 hours is too infrequent and does not address the most urgent circulation-related complications

Use this level of reasoning consistently.
