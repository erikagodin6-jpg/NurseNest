RATIONALE CONTENT STANDARD

The explanation is currently too shallow. Expand it substantially.

For every answered question, the rationale should follow this structure:

- Correct Answer
- Why this is correct:
  2 to 5 sentences minimum, clinically useful, exam-oriented, and specific
- Why the other options are incorrect:
  brief explanation for each distractor, ideally 1 to 3 sentences each
- Clinical pearl / priority point:
  one short practical takeaway
- Related lesson:
  link to the matching lesson page if available

Example format:

Correct Answer: B. Monitoring distal peripheral pulses and hourly urine output

Why this is correct:
After abdominal aortic aneurysm repair, the priority is assessing graft patency and end-organ perfusion. Distal pulses help evaluate whether blood flow is reaching the lower extremities past the repair site, while hourly urine output helps assess renal perfusion and overall hemodynamic stability. Reduced urine output may indicate hypoperfusion, shock, renal compromise, or graft-related complications. These are immediate post-operative priorities and are more urgent than routine GI recovery assessments.

Why the other options are incorrect:
A. Bowel sounds are important, but they are not the first priority immediately after AAA repair when circulation and perfusion must be monitored closely.
C. Early ambulation may eventually be appropriate, but not within 2 hours as a primary priority before confirming hemodynamic stability and graft perfusion.
D. Measuring abdominal girth can help monitor bleeding or distention, but once every 24 hours is not an adequate priority compared with ongoing perfusion assessment.

Clinical pearl:
In major vascular surgery, think perfusion first: pulses, urine output, blood pressure, bleeding, and signs of shock.

Related lesson:
Post-Operative Vascular Surgery Priorities

Use this depth and structure consistently.
