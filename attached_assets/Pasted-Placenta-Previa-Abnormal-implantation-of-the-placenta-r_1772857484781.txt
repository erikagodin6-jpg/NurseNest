Placenta Previa
-	Abnormal implantation of the placenta resulting in partial or complete covering of the cervical os. Causes placental blood vessels to thbe disrupted during cervical dilation and effacement, which may result in massive blood loss and maternal/fetal compromise. As pregnancy progresses, the placenta increases in size and can protentially migrate away from the cervical opening, resulting in complete resolution of the previa
-	An additional ultrasound will be performed around 36 weeks gestation to assess placental location 
-	A stable client with no active bleeding and reassuring fetal status may be discharged home and managed in an outpatient setting 
-	Client must be closely monitored and instructed to return to the hospital immediately if bleeding recurs
-	Cesarean birth is planned for after 36 weeks and prior to the onset of labor 
Vaginal exams are contraindicated, so are douching, vaginal intervourse due to risk of disruption of the placental vesels and subsequent hemorrhage
-	Diagnosed by ultrasound
-	Painless vaginal bleeding after 20 weeks 
-	May resolve by the third trimester
-	Type and screen is needed 
-	Large bore IV access
-	Continuous electronic fetal monitoring 
-	Count pads
-	Modified bed rest is recommended

Placental abruption
-	Complication of preeclampsia related to hypertension that can be life threatening for the fetus 
-	Causes premature detachment of the placenta for the uterine wall, resulting in bleeding from uterine blood vessels
-	Common manifestations: abdominal pain, dark red vaginal bleeding, rigid uterus, abnormal fetal heart rate patterns, uterine tachysystole
-	Fetal distress and maternal hypovolemia can develop quickly
-	The nurse should report vaginal bleeding to the HCP immediately because emergency cesarean birth is very common 
-	
-	Black Box Warning
-	- warfarin
-	ARBs
-	- ACE inhibitors


Menopause
-	SS: 
o	CNS: vasomotor symptoms, sleep disturbance, cognitive changes, depression, dementia
o	GU: amenorrhea, vulvovaginal atrophy, dyspareunia, decreased libido
o	Systemic: decreased skin thickness and elasticity, hair loss, weight gain
o	CVS: increased risk for cardiovascular disease
o	MSK: bone loss, joint pain
o	Diagnosed when menses has been absent for 12 months
o	Interventions:
	Calcium supplement associated with decreased estrogen levels, avoiding spicy foods to decrease vasomotor symptoms, use water based lubricants.

Hepatitis B
-	Transmitted by blood, semen, vaginal secretions
-	Clients can be asymptomatic carriers
-	Early symptoms: malaise, nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain
-	Jaundice, weight loss, clay coloured stools. Thrombocytopenia 

Ruptured ectopic pregnancy
-	Unilateral abdominal pain
-	Hypotension
-	Referred shoulder pain due to pooling of blood under the diaphragm which places pressure on the phrenic nerve

Submersion Injury
-	Airway management due to potential aspiration, pulmonary edema, bronchospasm 
-	Intubation and mechanical ventilation
-	Rewarming methods such as removing clients wet clothing, dry clothing, warm blankets
-	Warmed IV fluids and humidified oxygen
-	

Chest Tubes
-	Drain fluid or air and helps reestablish negative pressure which allows for lung expansion 
-	Hemothorax: dark red drainage of 60ml/hr is an expected finding for a patient with a hemothorax. This should be monitored and recorded
-	Give analgesic 30-60 mins prior to procedure 
-	Instruct client to hold breath and bear down when removing 
-	Clamping the chest tube should only be done when changing the drainage system or checking for air leaks as it increases pressure within the tube and pleural cavity and can cause tension pneumothorax
-	If the drainage exceeds 100 ml an hour or changes in quality should be reported to an HCP
-	Monitor for additional signs of bleeding such as hypotension, tachycardia, cool and pale skin 

Hemophilia
-	Bleeding disorder caused by deficiency in coagulation proteins
-	If injured patient needs to be monitored closely for bleeding. 
-	Intracranial bleeds can be lethal so factor 8 needs to be administered

Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome
-	Life threatening adverse reation to antipsychotic medications
-	Characterized by high fever, muscular rigidity, altered mental status and autonomic dysfunction
-	Treatment includes supportive care (rehydration, cooling, and discontinuation of the medication) 

Milrinone/Primacor
-	Phosphodiesterase-3 inhibitor given via IV infusion to increase contractility and promote vasodilation 
-	Inotropic agent prescribed to patients with heart failure unresponsive to other pharmacologic therapies 
-	Usually infused over 48-72 hours in a hospital setting but home infusion through a central line is more common as a palliative measure for end stage heart failure
-	Milrinone requires central venous access as the medication is a vesicant and can cause extravasion if infused through a PIV
o	Home health nurse:
	Ensure that an infusion pump is used to control the rate, instruct the family on basic troubleshooting
	Evaluate medication effectiveness and possible side effects
	Monitor the central line insertion site for infection
	Change the central line dressing as prescribed
	Monitor daily weight
	Monitor blood pressure for possible hypotension
	Implement safety precautions as hypotension increases the patients risk of falling
