Conduct Disorder
-	Persistent pattern of aggressive behavior (>6 months) during childhood/adolescence that willingly violates rules, norms, rights of others
-	Causes: structural, functional, chemical changes in the brain 
o	Genetics
o	Adverse child experiences 
-	Features:
o	Behaviour disorder – behavioural defiance
o	Theft, shoplifting 
o	Behaviour builds
o	Property damage, arson, graffiti
o	Progresses into harm against people or animals
o	Bullying, fights, weapons, sexual coercion. Behaviour is seen at home and at school
o	More serious violation of rules
o	Lack of trust with others, lack of emotional capacity to bond with others 
o	Low self esteem, easily frustrated, anger outbursts, lack of empathy 
-	Complications:
o	Craving excitement and lack of disregard for consequences can lead to 
o	Juvenile delinquency/legal implications, recreational drug or alcohol use, suspension and expulsion
o	Without therapy can develop antisocial personality disorder
-	Management:
o	Improve behaviour at home  caregiver management techniques, redirect inappropriate behaviour, positive reinforcement 
o	Help them build up bond and relationships, love and cared for so they can develop self esteem
o	CBT
o	Social skills training 
-	Nursing:
o	Promote safety
o	Check for substance use, suicidal or homicidal ideation
o	Firm limits, boundaries, structure, expectations 
o	Structures routine 
o	Behavioural goals 
o	Positive reinforcement
o	
Elder Mistreatment
-	Hidden problem
-	Usually involves family members, sometimes staff
-	Patients or witnesses minimize or deny due to fear of retaliation 
-	Unwilling to pursue legal action
-	Risks:
o	Abuser: 
	Dependent for housing and financial support, hx of violence, unemployed, substance use or mental illness,
o	clients risk
	inability to perform ADL
	neurologic (dementia)
	psychiatric (depression)
	lack of support
	alcohol disorder
-	Nursing
o	Atypical abrasions, lacerations, contusions, fractures
o	Poorly explained injuries
o	Sexual: genital injuries, STI
o	Neglect: dehydration, malnutrition, poor personal hygiene, pressure injuries, deteriorating health
o	Financial: failure to take or purchase medications, multiple missed appt
o	Screen for suicide and depression, ask if they feel safe, social service referral 
o	Report to appropriate authorities 
o	Take photographs and document specific findings (wounds and bruises)
o	Document statements by client or accompanying individual 
o	Prevent financial abuse
	Encourage client to appoint a trusted individual to oversee financial decisions 

Opioid Withdrawal
-	Opioids are stopped, the loss of their inhibitory effect leads to a sudden increase in norepinephrine release
-	Sudden release of norepinephrine that causes fight or flight reaction
-	Depressant effect of the opioid wanes  sympathetic nervous system activity increases  withdrawal
-	Symptoms start 4-12 hours after last use and peak within 24-48 hours
-	Craving is apparent
-	Symptoms:
o	Diaphoresis
o	Yawning, sneezing
o	Restlessness
o	Agitation
o	Muscle cramps
o	Tremor
o	Diarrhea, vomiting, abdominal cramps
o	Tachycardia, hypertension 
o	Runny nose, teary eyes, 
o	Shiver, goosebumps
o	Mydriasis (pupil dilation)
o	HTN
o	Insomnia
-	Medication assisted treatment: slowly tapers down opioid 
o	Methadone
o	Buprenorphine – verify client is not taking other opioids because they will experience withdrawal symptoms
o	Helps them avoid using dirty needles, local infection, endocarditis, sepsis
o	Adjunctive medications: 
	Loperamide, promethazine 
o	Watch fluid intake – risk for dehydration
o	Evaluate client behaviour and severity of withdrawal 
	COWS scale 
o	Monitor for suicidal ideation 
o	Long term sobriety: support groups, residential treatment programs, family therapy 
Panic Disorder
-	Dx:
o	Panic attacks
o	Followed by: persistent worry about when the next attack happens
o	Avoid situations that may trigger another panic attack
o	Can last 5-30 mins
o	May or may not be in response to a stressor
o	Some may have one, some may have repeated panic attacks
o	May be related to an imbalance of neurotransmitters or overactive amygdala 
o	Heart palpitations, chest pain, SOB
o	Trembling, fear of dying
o	Nursing:
	CBT
	SSRI – first line of treatment
•	Sertraline, fluoxetine, escitalopram
	Benzo:
•	Lorazepam, alprazolam
•	Given only until SSRI start to work
o	During a panic attack:
	Stay with client
	Reassure they are safe, decrease stimuli
	Speak in short simple sentences
	Controlled breathing exercises
o	After:
	Identify triggers
	Reassure
	Teach positive self talk 

PTSD
-	Exposure to traumatic event
-	Symptoms can present up to several years after the event
-	Body keeps releasing stress hormones 
-	Features:
o	Reliving the event constantly, intrusive thoughts, flashbacks, nightmares
o	Avoidance
o	Avoid social interactions
o	Amnesia about the complete event
o	State of hyper arousal 
o	Sleep disturbances
o	Outbursts of anger
o	Difficulty concentrating
o	Exaggerated startle response 
o	Negative condition and mood
o	Patient feels damaged, detached from others, self-isolate 
o	Negative emotions – fear, guilt, shame, hopelessness 
-	Complications:
o	Depression
o	Anxiety
o	Substance use 
o	Somatic symptoms – headaches or migraines
o	Job issues 
o	Difficulty with intimate relationships 
o	Trauma focused psychotherapy 
	Exposure and desensitization 
	Coping skills
	Encourage to speak about the event
o	Drugs: 
	SSRI
	Establish trust 
o	Nursing: silence and listening, establish trust, verbalize, don’t rush, teach coping strategies, breathing, guided imagery, journaling, exercise, music therapy
o	Address maladaptive coping 
o	Build relationships

Postmortem Care
-	Can be delayed in accordance with government agency policiies like when its unnatural, traumatic, criminal activity
-	Families may have cultural or religious beliefs
-	Nursing:
o	Contact family members immediately after death
o	Verify whether autopsy has been requested
o	Check organ or tissue donor status
o	Standard precautions unless they have an infection
o	Ask families if they want to be present or participate
o	Provide privacy and time with the deceased client
o	Leave body identification tags on body for transport to morgue or funeral home 
o	Wash and straighten body 
o	Clean linens and a pad under the perineum 
o	Place a pillow under head and put dentures in 
o	Close mouth and eyes

o	Keep surgical incisions intact 
o	Give all belongings to the family or transfer with the body 
o	Place in a body bag 

Tracheostomy
-	Surgical opening in the trachea for insertion of tracheostomy tube
-	Indications:
o	Unstable airway (swelling or edema – angioedema), prolonged need for mechanical ventilation 
-	Complicatioons:
o	Aspiration 
o	Infection
o	Bleeding
o	Obstruction
o	Dysphagia
o	Tracheal stenosis/necrosis 
-	Features
o	Inner and outer cannula
o	Inner is good because it can be removed to be cleaned 
o	Obturator: used to guide it into the throat then comes out
o	Cuffed or cuffless – inflated ensures air enters lungs with mechanical/manual ventilation 
o	Deflated cuff – swallowing is easier, air can move over vocal cords – speech is possible 
-	Nursing:
o	Communication board
o	Manage:
	Anxiety 
	Secretions
	Oxygenation/humidification 
	Support communication 
	Tube patency 
	Cuff
	Skin integrity 
	Nutrition – need an ng tube or a g tube, or iv feeds
	Monitor respiratory status
-	Teaching: 
o	Always keep a spare trach tube and one that’s a size smaller 
o	Maintain airway patency – suctioning, clean inner cannula 
o	Avoid water in the stoma (shower)
o	Maintain humidification to thin secretions  prevent mucus plugs 
o	Speak by briefly covering tube or using specialized cap if appropriate 
-	Dislodgement: 
o	Emergency: call for help
o	Newly placed: cannot replace because the track isn’t healed, putting a new one in can cause a fistula or other issues
o	Put client in semi fowler
o	Cover stoma with occlusive dressing
o	Ventilate with BVM over nose and mouth
o	Notify HCP
o	Mature: more than 7 days after insertion, replace with curved hemostat, insert new tube with an obturator 
o	Secure with new ties 
Laryngectomy:
-	Cannot ventilate over nose and mouth – larynx is removed
-	Ventilate over neck 

Trach Care
-	Inner cannula
-	Clean 2-3 times per day 
-	Soak in saline, scrub out, dry, put back in and twist to lock 
-	Disposable = toss out and replace if disposable 
-	Trach tapes/Velcro straps – prevents accidental dislodgement, especially when coughing
o	Apply minimal pressure to flange 
o	Ensure two fingers can fit between skin on neck and tie 


Cardiogenic Shock
-	Inability of heart muscle to properly contract and pump blood
-	Decreased CO and impaired tissue perfusion = activation of sympathetic nervous system
-	Causes: damage to heart muscle
o	MI
o	Decompensated heart failure
o	Cardiomyopathies
o	Valvular heart disease 
o	Arrhythmias 
o	Trauma to the heart
-	Features:
o	Impaired perfusion 
o	Hypotension
o	Clammy skin
o	Weak pulse
o	Tachycardia
o	Pallor, delayed cap refill 
o	Decreased urine output 
o	Impaired cerebral perfusion  changes in LOC, anxiety, agitation
o	Pulmonary congestion – blood is backed up in the lungs 
	Dyspnea, orthopnea, tachypnea, hypoxia, crackles
	Jugular vein distension, peripheral edema
-	Dx:
o	ECG
o	CXR
o	Echo
o	BNP
o	CK-MB, troponin 
-	Complications
o	Multi organ failure
o	MI
o	Cardiac death
-	Nursing:
o	Increase o2 delivery and decrease demand
o	Elevate HOB
o	Supplemental o2
o	Monitor intake and output 
o	Cardiac monitoring 
o	Treat dysrhythmias
o	Vasopressors
o	Prepare for intubation and mechanical ventilation 
o	Prepare for possible surgery (PCI), valve surgery 

Disaster Triage
-	Clients sorted rapidly from highest to lowest priority
-	Color coded
-	Best use of limited resources  helps the greatest number of people 
-	Emergent: red
o	Life-threatening with high probability of survival if interventions are received
	Shock, compromised airway, unstable wounds, chest trauma
o	Urgent (yellow)
	Serious injuries requiring treatment within 30 mins to 2 hours 
•	Open fractures with pulses felt distally 
•	Large wounds that are not actively bleeding
o	Nonurgent (green)
	Can wait at least 2 hours
	Infections, minor burns and lacerations, closed fractures
o	Expectant 
	Extensive injuries with poor prognosis, regardless of treatment
•	Pulselessness
•	Apnea
•	Severe neuro trauma
•	Full thickness burns more than 72 percent
-	Nursing:
o	Transfer stable clients to lower acuity units
o	Discharge stable clients
o	Prioritize and triage clients 
o	Supplemental oxygen and potential emergency intubation for clients with inhalation injuries 
o	Remove residual hazardous materials that could potentiate injuries like chemical agents, bioterrorism
o	Remove clothing and jewelry 
o	Place client in showering station outside of hospital before they enter

Neurogenic Shock
-	Distributive vasodilatory shock
-	Redistribution of intravascular fluids due to loss of sympathetic tone = hypovolemia
-	Massive vasodilation and blood pools in periphery 
-	Hypovolemia, decreased venous return 
-	Causes:
o	Spinal cord injuries 
o	Severe brain injuries
o	Spinal anesthesia
o	Opioids
-	Manifestations (parasympathetic)
o	Massive vasodilation 
o	Hypotension
o	Warm dry skin initially, warmth then dissipates and client becomes hypothermic 
o	Bradycardia
o	Altered mental status 
o	Weakness or paralysis below level of injury
o	Loss of bladder and bowel control 
-	Nursing:
o	Stop shock 
o	Stabilize spinal cord – cervical collar
o	Treat hypotension and bradycardia
	Elevate legs, IV fluid bolus, vasopressors (norepi)
	Atropine 
	Supplemental o2
o	Maintain core body temp with warming blankets
o	Cardiac monitors – arrhythmias
o	Monitor LOC with GCS

Obstructive Shock
-	Physical obstruction to blood flow in around and out from the heart
-	Decrease in CO and a decrease in perfusion
-	Causes: 
o	Cardiac tamponade 
	Distant muffled heart sounds
o	Pulmonary embolism
	Chest pain, sob
o	Tension pneumothorax
-	Features:
o	Hypotension
o	Tachycardia
o	Rapid, shallow breathing, tachypnea
o	Impaired perfusion
o	Weak pulse
o	Cool, clammy skin
o	Delayed cap refil
o	Cyanosis
o	Impaired cerebral perfusion – aloc
-	Complications
o	Organ failure
o	Arrhythmias
o	Cardiac arrest
-	Nursing:
o	Dx: cxr, echo
o	Treat cause
	Cardiac tamponade: pericardiocentesis
	Tension pneumothorax  needle decompression and chest tube
	Pulmonary embolism: embolectomy
o	Cardiac monitoring
o	Supplemental O2
o	Potential intubation
o	Fluid resuscitation
o	Intake and output

Burn Injuries
-	Chemical – caused by caustic substances 
-	Electrical – caused by strong electrical currents
-	Thermal 
-	Features:
o	1st: superficial burn, do not extend past the epidermis, blanchable, painful
o	2nd: partial thickness, extends into the dermis, painful blisters, edema, weeping (cap permeability)
o	3rd: full thickness: extends past the dermis into the subcutaneous tissue, severe edema, eschar, pain is reduced due to nerve damage 
o	4th degree: full thickness and muscle and bone, severe edema, eschar, pain may be absent due to nerve damage
-	Complications:
o	Inhalation injury caused by inhalation of smoke, hot air, or aerosolized toxins
o	Laryngeal (can irreversibly affect airway) and pulmonary edema (impaired gas exchange) 
	Singed nasal hair
	Soot tinged sputum
	Burns on face and neck
	Oropharyngeal edema or blisters
	Altered mental status
o	Carbon monoxide poisoning – can give falsely high spo2 readings
	Administer 100% oxygen and prepare for intubation via nonrebreather
o	Systemic inflammation can lead to respiratory failure 
o	Hypovolemic shock – cellular damage and increased capillary permeability  massive fluid and electrolyte loss  third spacing  hypovolemia  burn shock 
o	Organ hypoperfusion 
o	Decreased perfusion to kidneys = AKI
o	Compartment syndrome – skin becomes nonelastic, circumferential eschar  decreased perfusion to extremities 
	Respiratory complications if it happens to chest – skin becomes tight so lungs cannot expand 
o	Infection, sepsis
o	Stress induced hyperglycemia 
o	Malnutrition 
o	Mobility issues (contractures)
-	Determine TBSA by rule of nines
-	Nursing:
o	High fowler position for inhalation injury  if not intubated 
o	Remove clothing and jewelry around burn area especially with chemical burns 
o	If adhered to the wound bed, do not remove it 
o	Flush with cool water to stop additional burning, not COLD water
o	IV fluid resuscitation is very important 
o	Indwelling catheter to monitor output (genital burns  place quickly)
o	Obtain an ECG due to potential hyperkalemia
o	Pain management  IV opioids 
o	Minimize heat loss (warm environment, warm blankets)
o	Tetanus immunization 
o	High protein, high carb diet
o	Emotional support 
-	Invasive procedures
o	Debridement
o	Surgical excision or graft
o	Escharotomy if compartment syndrome develops 
o	Wound care – gentle cleansing, topical agents (antimicrobial creams, collagen), dressing  premedicate
o	Monitor skin grafts and donor sites 
-	Teaching: 
o	Rehabilitation 
o	Physical therapy
o	Splints or compression garments
o	Wound care as prescribed
o	Antihistamines to alleviate the itching associated with healing
o	Moisturizers and sunscreen should be applied liberally
o	Psychological needs and mental health resources 

Breast Cancer
-	Malignancy of the breast
-	Risk:
o	Female
o	Certain genetic alterations (BRCA1 and BRCA2 mutations)
o	Family hx breast cancer
o	Increasing age of 50 or older
o	Obesity
o	Increased exposure to estrogen in the body, prolonged menopausal hormone therapy
o	Early menarche, late menopause
o	First full term pregnancy occurring at age greater than 35
o	Smoking, alcohol use
-	Features:
o	Most often occurs in upper outer quadrant of the breast
o	Unilateral
o	Palpable mass is usually firm, asymmetrical, nonmobile, nontender 
o	May have discharge at the nipple – clear or bloody
o	Presence of inflammation 
o	Enlarged lymph nodes 
o	Erythema, peau d’orange, edema
o	Retracted nipple
o	May first be suspected to be mastitis
-	Nursing:
o	Thorough assessment of the breast
o	Check for asymmetry, lumps, discharge
o	Teach: modifiable risk factors
o	Screening guidelines
o	Reportable breast changes
o	Prepare for mastectomy, reconstruction, radiation
o	Meds:
	Chemo, hormone therapy, estrogen receptor blockers, monoclonal antibodies 
o	Emotional support and resources
o	Prepare for imagine, biopsy, axillary lymph node dissection
Mastectomy
-	Removal of all or part of the breast tissue
-	Procedure performed depends on extent of cancer and may include
o	Lumpectomy: tumor removal and small amount of surrounding normal tissue
o	Partial mastectomy: partial removal
o	Total or simple mastectomy: removal of whole breast, nipple, areola
o	Modified radical: removal of whole breast, all of the lymph nodes
-	Nursing:
o	Assess pain
o	Prevent lymphedema
	Elevate arm on the affected side
	Encourage supine sleep or sleeping on unaffected side
	No BP, injections, and venipuncture on affected side
	Put a sign above door or use a wristband
	Report unilateral increase in arm size, heaviness, numbness = lymphedema
o	Wound and drain care
	JP 
o	frequently use incentive spirometer
o	ROM exercises
o	Emotional support, encourage verbilization of feelings 

Diaper Candidiasis

-	Topical fungicide to affected diaper areas
-	Clean and dry diaper area; change diapers as soon as they become soiled, check skin folds frequently (inguinal folds)
-	Skin protectant ointment 
-	Avoid prolonged contact, wet or damp clothing
Cataracts
-	Clouding of the lenses, impaired visual acuity
-	Can be unilateral or bilateral
-	Mainly age related
-	Risks:
o	Trauma, smoking, alcohol, DM
o	Radiation, UV light
o	Corticosteroid use
-	Features:
o	Painless, progressive decline in vision
o	Problems with night driving, reading road signs
o	Glare, halo vision
o	Opacified lens
o	Loss of red reflex
-	Dx:
o	Ophthalmoscopy, slit-lamp microscopy
-	Treatment
o	Surgery
o	Outpatient procedure
o	Eyedrops dilate the pupil
o	Cataract is removed and an artificial intraocular lens is placed
o	Surgery is performed on one eye at a time, other eye is done a few months later
-	Surgical complications:
o	Hyphema: blood in eye
o	Intraocular infection
o	Retinal detachment
o	Lens malposition/dislocation
-	Nursing:
o	Postop:
	Notify if vision acuity worsens
	First couple of hours after surgery, vision might be blurry but gets better every day
	Notify if improvement does not happen or suddenly gets worse = retinal detachment
	Elevate HOB 30-45 to decrease pressure
	Avoid activities that increase pressure (straining)
	Monitor for infection 
	Wear eye patch, especially at bedtime to protect the eye
	Observe that the patient can put in eye drops correctly 
•	Rheumatoid arthritis may need abx drops
	Expected findings: might feel like there is sand in the eyes for 6-8 weeks, photophobia for months, might need sunglasses

Celiac disease
-	Autoimmune disorder
-	Gluten causes immune mediated small intestine inflammation and chronic malabsorption
-	Ingestion of gluten leads to an autoimmune response 
-	Gluten comes into contact with the wall and the epithelial wall is affected which leads to smaller surface area 
-	Cannot process gluten 
-	Manifestations:
o	Malabsorption – abdominal discomfort, diarrhea, flatulence, bloating
o	Steatorrhea
o	Malnutrition – younger = delayed growth, failure to thrive
	Older: iron deficiency anemia, vitamin deficiency, A, D, E, B12
-	Management:
o	Diet: gluten free diet 
o	BROW: barley, rye, oats, wheat (avoid)
o	Diet high in calories and proteins 
o	Read food labels carefully 
o	Take supplements, iron (ferrous sulfate), vitamins
