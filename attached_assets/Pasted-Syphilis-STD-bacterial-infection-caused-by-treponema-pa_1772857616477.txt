Syphilis
-	STD bacterial infection caused by treponema pallidum
-	Clients deelop painless sores that resolve spontaneously (primary syphilis)
-	Without treatment, clients can develop systemic symptoms, lymphadenopathy and diffuse maculopapular rash (secondary syphilis)
-	TORCH: toxoplasmosis, other (syphilis) rubella, cytomegalovirus, herpes simplex can cause fetal abnormalities during pregnancy 
-	Penicillin is the only adequate treatment for syphilis during pregnancy 
-	If patient has an allergy, penicillin desensitization is necessary

Tonsillectomy
-	Post operative bleeding is a primary concern after a tonsillectomy because the surgical site is not easily visualized and is vulnerable to irritation and trauma from swallowing and coughing 
-	Nurse should observe for signs of postoperative bleeding (frequent, increased swallowing or clearing of the throat, vomiting bright red blood) and notify the health care provider 
-	Expected post op findings: ear pain when swallowing, low grade fever – administer Tylenol 
-	Superficial infection at the surgical site is common and causes white, fluid filled exudate in the throat with halitosis and is not concerning because it usually resolves spontaneously after 5-10 days
-	Do not drink through a straw
-	Do not suction routinely

Preterm birth
-	Birth before 37 weeks and 0 days
-	Infection (periodontal disease, UTI) is strongly associated with preterm labor, particularly when untreated 
-	Infection causes release of inflammatory mediators such as prostaglandins which are uterotonic and contribute to cervical softening 

Meniere Disease
-	Excess fluid accumulation inside the inner ear
-	Symptoms include vertigo, tinnitus, hearing loss, aural fullness
-	Can be associated with nausea and vomiting 
-	Clients can have drop attacks
-	During an attack, the client is treated with vestibular suppressants including sedatives, antihistamines, anticholinergics, and antiemetics. The nurses priority is to plan for client safety with fall precautions given the severe vertigo and use of sedating medications
-	Restrict salt to void fluid buildup in the ear

Chest tube removal
-	Indicated when drainage is minimal or absent, air leak is resolved, and the lung has reexpanded. The general steps for removal include:
o	Premedicate with analgesic 30-60 mins before 
o	Make sure patient is in semi-fowlers position or on the unaffected side 
o	Provide the HCP with sterile suture removal equipment 
o	Instruct the patient to breathe in, hold it, bear down Valsalva maneuver) while the tube is removed to decrease the risk for pneumothorax and increase intrathoracic pressure and prevent air from entering the pleural space
o	Apply a sterile airtight occlusive dressing to the site immediately
o	Perform a chest xray within 2-24 hours
