The peripheral nervous system consists of all neural structures outside the brain and spinal cord and serves as the communication network between the central nervous system and the rest of the body. It includes cranial nerves, spinal nerves, sensory receptors, and peripheral ganglia. Functionally, it is divided into the somatic nervous system and the autonomic nervous system, each with distinct structural organization and physiological roles.
The somatic nervous system controls voluntary movement of skeletal muscle and transmits conscious sensory information such as touch, pain, temperature, and proprioception to the central nervous system. Its motor pathway consists of a single lower motor neuron that originates in the brainstem or spinal cord and extends directly to skeletal muscle. When an action potential reaches the neuromuscular junction, acetylcholine is released and binds to nicotinic receptors on the muscle fiber, triggering depolarization and contraction. Because this pathway involves only one neuron between the central nervous system and the effector, somatic motor responses are typically rapid and precise. Damage to somatic motor neurons results in weakness, decreased tone, atrophy, and diminished reflexes.
The autonomic nervous system regulates involuntary functions necessary for homeostasis, including heart rate, blood pressure, respiratory rate, digestion, thermoregulation, and glandular secretion. Unlike the somatic system, autonomic pathways use a two neuron chain consisting of a preganglionic neuron and a postganglionic neuron. The preganglionic neuron originates in the central nervous system and synapses in an autonomic ganglion. The postganglionic neuron extends from the ganglion to the target organ. This arrangement allows more diffuse and coordinated responses across multiple organs.
The autonomic nervous system has two primary divisions that generally exert opposing effects: the sympathetic and parasympathetic systems. The sympathetic division prepares the body for activity, stress, or threat. It originates from the thoracolumbar region of the spinal cord. Preganglionic neurons release acetylcholine onto nicotinic receptors in sympathetic ganglia, and most postganglionic neurons release norepinephrine onto target organs. Sympathetic activation increases heart rate and contractility, dilates bronchioles to improve airflow, dilates pupils, decreases gastrointestinal motility, increases glucose availability, and redistributes blood flow toward skeletal muscle. It promotes short term survival by prioritizing oxygen delivery and energy mobilization.
The parasympathetic division promotes restorative and energy conserving processes. It originates from the brainstem and sacral spinal cord, often described as craniosacral outflow. Both preganglionic and postganglionic neurons release acetylcholine, although they bind to different receptor types at the ganglion and target organ. Parasympathetic stimulation slows heart rate, constricts pupils, increases gastrointestinal motility and secretion, promotes digestion, and supports reproductive and elimination functions. Its effects are generally more localized compared to the sympathetic system.
The balance between sympathetic and parasympathetic tone determines autonomic output at any given time. For example, resting heart rate reflects the interplay between sympathetic stimulation and parasympathetic inhibition. Dysregulation of autonomic balance contributes to conditions such as hypertension, arrhythmias, and stress related disorders.
Reflex arcs represent the simplest functional circuits within the nervous system and are designed to produce rapid, protective responses. A reflex begins when a stimulus activates a sensory receptor. The sensory or afferent neuron transmits the signal to the spinal cord. Within the spinal cord, the signal may synapse directly with a motor neuron in a monosynaptic reflex or first synapse with an interneuron in a polysynaptic reflex. The motor or efferent neuron then carries the impulse to an effector such as a skeletal muscle or gland, producing an immediate response.
The patellar reflex is a classic example of a monosynaptic stretch reflex. Tapping the patellar tendon stretches the quadriceps muscle spindle receptors. The sensory neuron carries this information to the spinal cord, where it synapses directly with a motor neuron that causes quadriceps contraction and knee extension. Because this pathway bypasses higher brain centers, the response is rapid and involuntary.
Reflexes serve as diagnostic tools because they provide insight into the integrity of sensory pathways, motor pathways, and specific spinal cord segments. Hyperactive reflexes may indicate upper motor neuron lesions, while diminished reflexes suggest lower motor neuron or peripheral nerve dysfunction.
Together, the somatic and autonomic divisions of the peripheral nervous system enable coordinated voluntary movement, unconscious organ regulation, and rapid protective responses. Their structure reflects their function, and understanding these pathways allows clinicians to interpret neurological findings in the context of underlying physiology.
